### COLD OPEN

[ON-SCREEN: Dr. Kane standing against a wall, a plumb line hanging beside him]

Stand against a wall right now. Just for a second — wherever you are. Put your back against it. Heels, buttocks, shoulder blades touching.

Now — does the back of your head touch the wall? Or is there a gap?

[PAUSE]

If there's a gap — and especially if it's more than two inches — your spine has started to curve forward. And that curve is doing something most people never consider: it's compressing the organs inside your chest and abdomen.

Not dramatically. Not overnight. But slowly, progressively, year after year — the forward curve of your upper spine is reducing the space your lungs need to expand, the room your heart has to beat, and the pathway your digestive system uses to move food.

[LEAN IN]

This isn't about posture. This is about organ function. And once you understand the mechanism, you'll never look at a "dowager's hump" the same way again.

---

### THE MEASUREMENT THAT STARTLED ME

[ON-SCREEN: "Wall Test — the 2-inch threshold"]

I started measuring this in my patients about three years ago. Simple test — stand against a wall, measure the gap between the occiput and the wall. Normal is zero to one inch. Two inches or more indicates significant forward head posture combined with thoracic kyphosis.

In my practice, among patients over 65, about 40% have a gap of two inches or more. And here's what surprised me: the patients with the largest gaps weren't just the ones with back pain. They were the ones with the most unexplained shortness of breath, the most digestive complaints, the most fatigue.

That correlation sent me down a research path that fundamentally changed how I think about spinal curvature in older adults.

---

### THE DOMINO EFFECT — STARTING WITH YOUR LUNGS

[ON-SCREEN: "Domino #1: Your Lungs" — animated spine compressing rib cage]

Your lungs sit inside your rib cage. Your rib cage attaches to your thoracic spine. When that spine curves forward — a condition called hyperkyphosis — the rib cage gets pulled down and inward.

Think of it like a bellows. A bellows works because it can open wide and compress. If you bend the frame of the bellows, it doesn't open as far. Same principle.

A 2024 study in the *Journal of Geriatric Physical Therapy* found that for every 10 degrees of increased thoracic kyphosis, forced vital capacity — the amount of air you can exhale after a full breath — decreased by approximately 5 to 9 percent.

That might not sound like much. But consider this: if your curve has progressed 30 degrees from where it was at 40 — which is common — you've lost 15 to 27 percent of your lung capacity. You're breathing with three-quarters of the lungs you used to have.

[PAUSE]

And here's the part that connects to everything else: when your lungs can't fully expand, your body compensates by breathing faster and shallower. That shallow breathing doesn't oxygenate your blood as efficiently. Which means your heart has to work harder to deliver the same amount of oxygen. Which means your muscles get less oxygen during activity. Which means you fatigue faster.

One curve. Four consequences. And we haven't even gotten to the heart yet.

---

### YOUR HEART IS GETTING SQUEEZED

[ON-SCREEN: "Domino #2: Your Heart" — cross-section showing heart position shift]

Your heart sits in a space called the mediastinum — between your lungs, in front of your spine. When the thoracic spine curves forward and the rib cage compresses, the mediastinum gets crowded.

A 2023 study in *Spine* — the orthopedic journal, not the general one — used MRI to measure cardiac position in patients with varying degrees of kyphosis. They found that in patients with curves above 50 degrees, the heart was displaced anteriorly and rotated, which altered the electrical conduction pattern on EKG.

[ON-SCREEN: "Kyphosis can alter EKG readings — leading to misdiagnosis"]

Why does that matter? Because if your EKG looks abnormal — and it might, simply because your heart is sitting at a different angle — you could be referred for cardiac workup you don't need. Or worse, a real cardiac issue could be attributed to "postural changes" and dismissed.

I had a patient — I'll call her Dorothy, 74 — who was told her EKG abnormalities were "probably positional" because of her kyphosis. They were. But she also had atrial fibrillation that was missed for eight months because everyone assumed the weird EKG was just her spine.

[PAUSE]

That's not a reason to panic. It's a reason to make sure your doctors know about your spinal curvature when they're interpreting cardiac tests.

### THE DIGESTIVE SYSTEM YOU NEVER THOUGHT ABOUT

[ON-SCREEN: "Domino #3: Your Digestive Tract" — diagram showing compressed abdomen]

This is the one nobody talks about.

Your esophagus — the tube from your throat to your stomach — passes through an opening in the diaphragm called the hiatus. When your spine curves forward and compresses your abdominal cavity, the geometry of that opening changes.

A 2024 study in *Diseases of the Esophagus* found that patients with thoracic hyperkyphosis above 45 degrees had a 3.2 times higher prevalence of hiatal hernia — where part of the stomach pushes up through the diaphragm. They also had significantly more reflux symptoms, even when they weren't on reflux medication.

[ON-SCREEN: "Kyphosis → compressed abdomen → hiatal hernia → reflux"]

But it goes further than reflux. When your abdominal cavity is compressed, there's simply less room for your stomach to expand after eating. Which means you feel full faster. Which means you eat less. Which — if you're already losing muscle mass from sarcopenia — means you're not getting enough protein and calories to maintain what muscle you have.

It's a cascade. The curve reduces your lung capacity, which reduces your oxygen, which reduces your energy, which reduces your activity, which accelerates your muscle loss, which weakens the muscles that support your spine, which worsens the curve.

[PAUSE]

It's a loop. And breaking it requires understanding that it exists.

---

### THE CIRCULATION CONNECTION

[ON-SCREEN: "Domino #4: Your Aorta" — showing abdominal aorta compression]

One more domino. And this one is vascular — my area of specialty.

Your abdominal aorta — the largest artery in your body — runs along the front of your spine, right through the space that gets compressed when kyphosis progresses.

A 2023 study in the *Journal of Vascular Surgery* used CT angiography to measure aortic diameter and flow velocity in patients with varying degrees of thoracic kyphosis. In patients with curves above 55 degrees, they found a 12% reduction in aortic diameter at the level of maximum curvature and a corresponding increase in flow velocity — meaning the same volume of blood was being forced through a narrower space.

[ON-SCREEN: "Narrower aorta → faster flow → increased shear stress → endothelial damage"]

That increased flow velocity creates something called shear stress on the endothelial lining of the artery. Over time, chronic shear stress damages the endothelium, which is the first step in atherosclerosis. The very thing we're trying to prevent in vascular medicine — healthy endothelial function — is being undermined by a curved spine.

I want to be clear: this doesn't mean kyphosis causes heart attacks. It means it's a contributing factor to vascular stress that most doctors — including most vascular specialists — never consider.

### HOW TO KNOW WHERE YOU STAND

[ON-SCREEN: "3 self-assessment tests you can do at home"]

Alright. So how do you know if this is affecting you? Three simple checks.

**The Wall Test** — which I described at the beginning. Stand against a wall, heels, buttocks, and shoulder blades touching. If the back of your head doesn't touch the wall, or if there's a gap of more than two inches, you have forward head posture with thoracic kyphosis.

[ON-SCREEN: Wall test demonstration with measurements]

**The Breath Test** — sit up as straight as you can. Take a deep breath. Now slouch forward — round your back, let your head drop forward. Take another breath. Notice the difference? That restriction you feel is what your lungs are dealing with all day if you're chronically curved forward.

[ON-SCREEN: Side-by-side comparison — upright vs. slouched breathing]

**The Height Test** — have someone measure your standing height. Then have them measure your "wall height" — standing against a wall with the same posture you naturally hold. If there's more than an inch of difference between your natural posture and your best upright posture, you're losing functional height to curvature.

[ON-SCREEN: "Lost height = lost organ space"]

These aren't diagnostic. But they give you a starting point for a conversation with your doctor.

### WHAT ACTUALLY HELPS

[ON-SCREEN: "Evidence-based interventions for hyperkyphosis"]

Here's the good news: unlike some age-related changes, thoracic kyphosis can be improved — even after 60.

A 2024 randomized controlled trial published in *Osteoporosis International* tested a specific exercise program in adults over 65 with hyperkyphosis. The program focused on three things: thoracic extension exercises, scapular retraction strengthening, and postural awareness training. After six months, the exercise group reduced their kyphosis angle by an average of 5.2 degrees. The control group worsened by 1.8 degrees.

Five degrees might not sound like much. But remember — every 10 degrees of improvement means 5 to 9% more lung capacity. Five degrees is half that. And it's achievable with exercises that take about 15 minutes a day.

[ON-SCREEN: "3 daily exercises that reduce kyphosis"]

The three exercises that had the strongest evidence:

**One: Seated thoracic extension.** Sit in a chair with a back that comes up to mid-cheap level. Place a rolled towel horizontally behind your upper back. Gently extend backward over the towel, opening your chest. Hold for five seconds. Repeat ten times. This directly counteracts the forward curve.

**Two: Wall angels.** Stand with your back against a wall, arms bent at 90 degrees like a goalpost. Slide your arms up the wall and back down, keeping contact with the wall throughout. This strengthens the lower trapezius and rhomboids — the muscles that pull your shoulders back and open your chest.

**Three: Chin tucks.** Sit or stand tall. Pull your chin straight back — like you're making a double chin. Hold for five seconds. Repeat ten times. This corrects forward head posture, which is the upper extension of the same curve.

[PAUSE]

Fifteen minutes a day. No equipment. No gym membership. And the evidence says it works.

### WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR

[ON-SCREEN: "Red flags that warrant medical evaluation"]

There are situations where kyphosis needs more than exercise.

If you've noticed a rapid increase in curvature over months — not years — that could indicate a compression fracture. Osteoporosis can cause vertebral bodies to collapse, and sometimes the first sign is increasing curvature, not pain.

If you're experiencing new shortness of breath that's getting worse, or if you've developed swallowing difficulties, or if you have persistent digestive symptoms that don't respond to standard treatment — mention your spinal curvature to your doctor. It may be a contributing factor they haven't considered.

And if you're being evaluated for cardiac issues, tell your cardiologist about your kyphosis. It can affect EKG interpretation, as I mentioned with Dorothy.

[ON-SCREEN: "Word-for-word doctor conversation starter"]

Here's a simple way to bring it up:

*"I've noticed my upper back is curving forward more, and I'm wondering if it could be affecting my breathing/digestion/heart tests. Could we evaluate my thoracic kyphosis and whether it's contributing to my symptoms?"*

### THE BIGGER TRUTH

[ON-SCREEN: "Your spine is not just a stack of bones — it's a housing for your organs"]

I think we treat the spine like it's just a structural thing — something that holds us up and occasionally hurts. But it's more than that. Your spine is the frame that protects and positions every organ in your trunk.

When that frame changes shape, everything inside it has to adapt. Your lungs adapt by breathing shallower. Your heart adapts by working harder. Your digestive system adapts by accepting less. Your blood vessels adapt by tolerating more stress.

Those adaptations work for a while. But over years, they accumulate. And what looks like "just aging" — getting shorter of breath, getting more tired, getting more digestive issues — might actually be a structural problem with a structural solution.

[PAUSE]

You can't stop your spine from changing as you age. But you can slow the curve, improve the curve, and make sure the organs inside have the room they need to keep doing their jobs.

Stand tall. Not for appearance. For function.
