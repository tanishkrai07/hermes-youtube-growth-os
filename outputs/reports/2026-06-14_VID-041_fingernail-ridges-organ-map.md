[LEAN IN]

Your nails aren't just protective caps. They're growing, living tissue with a blood supply, a nerve supply, and a growth rate that's remarkably consistent. And because they grow continuously — about three millimeters per month — they create a timeline. A physical record of what's been happening inside your body.

Think of it like tree rings. But on your fingers. And instead of telling you about rainfall, they're telling you about your organs.

### THE FOUR PATTERNS

Let me walk you through the four nail ridge patterns that matter after 60. Each one maps to something different. And I want you to check your hands as we go — because this is one of those things that sticks better when you see it on your own fingers.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Pattern 1: Vertical Ridges"]

**Pattern 1: Vertical Ridges**

These are the ones most people have. Thin lines running from the cuticle to the tip, like tiny railroad tracks. They might be subtle, or they might be deep enough to feel with your finger.

[VISUAL: Close-up of a fingernail with prominent vertical ridges. Label: "Vertical Ridges — Longitudinal Ridging."]

If you have these, and they're your only nail change, the honest answer is: this is primarily aging. The nail matrix — the tissue under your cuticle that produces the nail — becomes less uniform as you age. The keratin layers don't lay down as smoothly. You get ridges.

This is the benign pattern. The one you don't need to lose sleep over.

But — and this is important — if your vertical ridges appeared suddenly, or if they became dramatically more pronounced over a short period (weeks to months), that's worth mentioning to your doctor. A sudden change in a stable pattern is always more significant than a pattern that's been there for years.

[PAUSE]

Now, let's talk about the one that actually can tell you when something went wrong.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Pattern 2: Horizontal Ridges — Beau's Lines"]

**Pattern 2: Horizontal Ridges (Beau's Lines)**

These run side to side, not up and down. They're grooves or dips that cross the nail horizontally. Sometimes they're subtle — just a slight depression. Sometimes they're deep enough that you can catch them with your fingernail.

[VISUAL: Close-up of a fingernail with a horizontal groove. Label: "Beau's Lines — Transverse Grooves." Show a timeline graphic: the groove's distance from the cuticle indicates when the systemic stress occurred.]

Here's where it gets interesting. And this is the part that I find genuinely fascinating, and I think you will too.

Your nails grow at a remarkably consistent rate: approximately three millimeters per month. That's about one millimeter every ten days. This rate is so consistent that dermatologists have used it for decades to estimate when a systemic stress event occurred.

So if you have a horizontal ridge — a Beau's line — and you measure its distance from your cuticle, you can calculate when whatever caused it happened.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Nail growth: ~3mm/month. Ridge distance from cuticle = timeline of systemic stress."]

Let me give you a concrete example. Say you have a horizontal groove on your thumbnail that's about six millimeters from the cuticle. That means whatever disrupted your nail growth happened approximately two months ago.

[PAUSE]

Now, what kind of systemic stress causes Beau's lines? The list is long: severe illness, high fever, surgery, significant emotional stress, chemotherapy, major nutritional deficiency. The nail matrix is incredibly sensitive to systemic disruption. When your body is under significant stress, nail growth slows or temporarily stops. When it resumes, you get a visible line at the point where growth resumed.

I had a patient — I'll call her Margaret — who came in for a completely unrelated vascular concern. While I was examining her hands, I noticed Beau's lines on three of her fingernails. I asked her if she'd been sick recently. She said no. I asked about any major stress — surgery, illness, anything. She paused and said, "Well, I had COVID about three months ago. It was rough for about two weeks."

The math lined up perfectly. The ridges were approximately nine millimeters from her cuticle — right at the three-month mark.

[LEAN IN]

Margaret's nails had recorded her COVID infection like a timestamp. She'd had no idea. And while her ridges weren't dangerous in themselves, they were a data point — a physical record of a significant systemic stress event that her body had experienced.

If you have horizontal ridges, do the math. Measure the distance. Think back. You might be surprised what your nails remember that you've forgotten.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Pattern 3: Spoon Nails — Koilonychia"]

**Pattern 3: Spoon Nails (Koilonychia)**

This one's less common but more specific. Instead of curving gently downward like a normal nail, the nail curves upward at the edges, creating a concave shape. Like a spoon. Sometimes you can actually hold a drop of water on the nail.

[VISUAL: Close-up of a fingernail with spoon-shaped concavity. Label: "Koilonychia — Spoon Nails."]

Spoon nails are most classically associated with iron deficiency. And I mean the kind of iron deficiency that's been present for a while — not just a slightly low ferritin, but a genuine, sustained iron shortage.

But here's what most people don't know: spoon nails can also appear in hemochromatosis — the opposite problem, where you have too much iron. The nail changes in hemochromatosis can include both spooning and a strange, almost flattened appearance.

[PAUSE]

This is why I don't like when people see spoon nails and immediately start taking iron supplements. You might need iron. Or you might have too much. Or it might be something else entirely — hypothyroidism, for instance, can also cause nail changes that resemble koilonychia.

If you notice spoon-shaped nails, the right move is a simple blood test: complete iron panel (serum iron, ferritin, TIBC, transferrin saturation) plus a ferritin level. Not a guess. A test.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Pattern 4: Digital Clubbing — The One That Needs Attention"]

**Pattern 4: Digital Clubbing**

This is the pattern I want you to take most seriously. Not because it's the most common — it's actually the least common of the four — but because it's the one most likely to signal something that needs evaluation.

Digital clubbing is when the tips of your fingers become enlarged and rounded, and the nail curves downward dramatically. The angle between the nail and the nail bed — which is normally about 160 degrees — flattens out. In advanced cases, the fingertip looks like a drumstick.

[VISUAL: Comparison of a normal fingertip (160-degree angle) vs. a clubbed fingertip (180+ degrees). Label: "Digital Clubbing — Lovibond's Angle."]

Here's a test you can do right now. It's called Schamroth's window test.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Schamroth's Window Test — Do This Now"]

Take your two index fingers and press the nail beds together, back to back. You should see a small, diamond-shaped gap between them — a window of light. That's normal.

If that window disappears — if the nail beds touch completely with no gap — that's a positive Schamroth's test. And it suggests digital clubbing.

[VISUAL: Animation showing the Schamroth's window test. Normal: diamond-shaped gap visible. Positive: no gap, nail beds flush.]

[PAUSE]

Now, what does clubbing mean? The most important associations are pulmonary — lung conditions. Lung cancer, chronic lung infections, bronchiectasis, pulmonary fibrosis. Clubbing can appear years before other symptoms of these conditions.

But it's not just lungs. Clubbing can also appear with liver disease (particularly cirrhosis), heart conditions (especially congenital heart disease and infective endocarditis), inflammatory bowel disease, and thyroid conditions.

[LEAN IN]

I want to be very clear: if you have clubbing, it does not mean you have cancer. It means you have a physical finding that warrants evaluation. The most common cause of clubbing in the general population is actually idiopathic — meaning no underlying cause is found. But the evaluation is important because when a cause IS found, early detection matters enormously.

### THE SELF-ASSESSMENT PROTOCOL

Okay. Let me give you a practical, 60-second self-assessment you can do right now. Or the next time you're watching this with your hands free.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "The 60-Second Nail Check"]

**Step 1: Look at your nails under good light.** Natural light is best. Hold your hands at eye level.

**Step 2: Check for vertical ridges.** If present, note whether they've been there for years (benign) or appeared recently (worth mentioning to your doctor).

**Step 3: Check for horizontal ridges.** If present, measure the distance from the cuticle. Think back — was there a significant illness, surgery, or stress event at that time?

**Step 4: Check for spooning.** Does any nail curve upward at the edges? If yes, request an iron panel.

**Step 5: Do the Schamroth's window test.** Press your index fingers together. Is there a gap? If not, mention it to your doctor.

**Step 6: Note any color changes.** Pale nails can suggest anemia. Yellow nails can suggest fungal infection or, less commonly, liver or lung conditions. Dark streaks — a single dark line running the length of the nail — should always be evaluated by a dermatologist.

[PAUSE]

This takes about 60 seconds. And it gives you a snapshot of information that your body is literally displaying on your hands. Most people never look. Most doctors never ask. But the data is there.

### WHEN TO STOP WATCHING AND START CALLING

Let me be specific about when nail changes warrant a doctor visit — because I don't want you to worry about every ridge, and I also don't want you to dismiss something important.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Call Your Doctor If:"]

**Call your doctor if:**

- You have a single dark streak on one nail that appeared recently and is widening
- You have clubbing (positive Schamroth's test) that you haven't discussed with your doctor
- You have spoon-shaped nails, especially if you also feel fatigued, short of breath, or dizzy
- You have horizontal ridges that appeared after a significant illness and haven't grown out after six months
- You have any nail change accompanied by other symptoms — unexplained weight loss, persistent cough, chest pain, or significant fatigue

**Don't worry about:**

- Vertical ridges that have been present for years and are stable
- Minor nail discoloration from aging
- Small white spots (these are usually minor trauma to the nail matrix)
- Slight nail thickening that's been gradual over many years

[PAUSE]

I want to add one more thing about this topic, because it connects to something I care about deeply — the vascular system.

[LEAN IN]

The nail bed is one of the few places in the body where you can directly observe tiny blood vessels. The capillaries under your nails are a window into your microvascular health. When I examine a patient's nails, I'm not just looking at the keratin — I'm looking at the color, the capillary refill, the texture of the nail bed tissue.

In peripheral vascular disease — which is my specialty — the nails can show early signs. They might become thickened, slow-growing, or develop a bluish tint. The nail bed might look pale when the hand is elevated and slow to return to pink when lowered.

Your nails are a vascular report card. And most people never read it.

Because the best time to catch something is when it's still just a signal. Before it becomes a symptom. Before it becomes a crisis.
