### WHAT'S ACTUALLY HAPPENING IN YOUR ARTERIES

Let me explain what was happening inside Robert's body — because the mechanism matters, and it's not what most people think.

[VISUAL: Cross-section of the carotid artery, showing a vulnerable atherosclerotic plaque. The fibrous cap is thin, the lipid core is large. A small piece of the cap ruptures, releasing micro-emboli that travel toward the brain.]

This is the carotid artery. It runs up both sides of your neck, and it's the main blood supply to your brain. Most people know that carotid arteries can develop plaque — fatty deposits that narrow the artery over time. What most people don't know is that the danger isn't just the narrowing. It's the plaque itself.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Narrowing is gradual. Plaque rupture is sudden."]

Some plaques are stable. Thick fibrous cap, small lipid core. They narrow the artery slowly, over years. Your body can compensate. These are the plaques that show up on a routine carotid ultrasound and your doctor says "mild stenosis, let's monitor."

But some plaques are vulnerable. Thin fibrous cap. Large, soft lipid core. Inflamed. These plaques can rupture — not the whole thing, just a small piece of the cap breaks open. And when it does, tiny fragments of clot and debris break free.

[VISUAL: Animation showing micro-emboli traveling from carotid plaque → through the internal carotid → into the cerebral circulation → lodging in a small vessel → causing transient ischemia → then dissolving or moving on.]

These micro-emboli travel up into the brain's blood vessels. They lodge in small arteries. They block blood flow — temporarily. The brain tissue downstream doesn't get enough oxygen. You get symptoms. Visual loss. Language difficulty. Facial droop. Hand clumsiness.

And then — here's the key — the micro-emboli dissolve. Or they break up and move on. Blood flow returns. The tissue recovers. The symptoms resolve.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "TIA = Transient Ischemic Attack. Transient = it goes away. Ischemic = blood flow was blocked. Attack = it was real."]

That's a TIA. A transient ischemic attack. And it is not a "mini-stroke" — that term minimizes what's actually happening. A TIA is a stroke that resolved. The mechanism is identical. The only difference is duration.

[PAUSE]

Here's what the data says: among people who have a TIA, approximately 10 to 15 percent will have a full stroke within 90 days. And the highest-risk period? The first 48 hours.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "After a TIA: 10-15% stroke risk within 90 days. Highest risk: first 48 hours."]

A 2023 study in the New England Journal of Medicine followed over 3,000 TIA patients and found that those who received urgent evaluation and treatment within 24 hours reduced their 90-day stroke risk by nearly 80 percent compared to those who waited more than 72 hours.

[LEAN IN]

Eighty percent reduction. From urgency alone. From getting evaluated quickly.

That's why I'm telling you Robert's story. Not to scare you. Because the scariest part of Robert's story isn't the stroke. It's that he had four warnings and didn't know what they meant.

### THE FOUR WARNING PATTERNS

Let me walk you through the four most common TIA patterns — the ones that precede strokes like Robert's. These are the ones I want you to know. Not because they're the only ones, but because they're the most common and the most frequently dismissed.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "The 4 Warning Patterns — What to Watch For"]

**Pattern 1: Transient Visual Loss**

[VISUAL: Simulation of the visual field — right side fading to black, then returning. Label: "Amaurosis Fugax."]

This is what happened to Robert with the newspaper. Amaurosis fugax — the medical term for temporary vision loss in one eye or one visual field. It's often described as a "curtain coming down" over the eye, or half the visual field just disappearing.

It happens when micro-emboli lodge in the retinal artery or the visual processing areas of the brain. It typically lasts 2 to 15 minutes. Then it resolves completely.

[PAUSE]

If this happens to you — even once, even if it resolves in 30 seconds — that is not "eye strain." That is not "getting older." That is a neurological event. And it warrants urgent evaluation.

**Pattern 2: Language Hesitation or Word-Finding Difficulty**

[VISUAL: Simple animation of a person trying to speak, with words appearing and disappearing. Label: "Transient Aphasia."]

This is what happened to Robert at the restaurant. You know the word. You can see it in your mind. But when you try to say it, it doesn't come out. Or the wrong word comes out. Or you pause mid-sentence and can't find your way back.

This is transient aphasia — temporary language disruption. It happens when micro-emboli affect the language centers of the brain, usually in the left hemisphere. It can last seconds to minutes.

People laugh it off. "Senior moment." "I'm just tired." But a true TIA-related language event has a specific quality: it's sudden, it's clearly abnormal to you, and it resolves completely. It's not the gradual "tip of the tongue" thing everyone experiences. It's abrupt.

**Pattern 3: Facial Asymmetry or Unilateral Numbness**

[VISUAL: Split-face comparison — one side normal, one side with subtle droop. Label: "Transient Facial Droop."]

This is the one people recognize — because it's part of the FAST test. But in a TIA, it comes and goes. You notice your smile is lopsided while brushing your teeth. One side of your face feels numb or tingly. Your mouth doesn't quite work right when you're eating.

And then it's gone. By lunch, everything feels normal.

[PAUSE]

Here's what I want you to hear: the FAST test is for acute strokes. It's designed for the moment when someone is actively having a stroke. But the facial droop of a TIA can be subtle. It can last two hours and disappear. And because it disappears, people don't call 911. They call their doctor's office and get an appointment for three weeks later.

That's too late. If you have transient facial droop — even if it resolves — you need evaluation within 24 to 48 hours. Not three weeks.

**Pattern 4: Unilateral Clumsiness or Spatial Misreach**

[VISUAL: Animation of a hand reaching for a target and missing, then correcting. Label: "Transient Motor Deficit."]

This is what happened to Robert with the coffee mug. Your hand doesn't do what you tell it to. You reach for something and miss. You drop things. Your fingers feel clumsy, not weak — there's a difference. Weakness is "I can't grip." Clumsiness is "I can't aim my grip."

This happens when micro-emboli affect the motor planning areas of the brain. It's often the most dismissed symptom because it's the most subtle. "I was just tired." "I wasn't paying attention."

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "If it's one-sided, sudden, and resolves — it's not 'just tired.'"]

### THE SELF-ADVOCACY PROTOCOL

Okay. Let's say you notice one of these patterns. Maybe it's the visual loss. Maybe it's the word-finding difficulty. Maybe it's something else that feels neurological and transient.

What do you do?

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Step 1: Don't wait. Step 2: Don't drive yourself. Step 3: Say the right words."]

**Step 1: Don't wait for it to happen again.**

I know the temptation. It resolved. You feel fine. Maybe it was nothing. But the data is clear: the highest-risk period for a full stroke after a TIA is the first 48 hours. Every hour matters. Not in a panic — in a "this is time-sensitive" way.

**Step 2: Don't drive yourself to the ER.**

If you're currently having symptoms, call 911. If the symptoms have resolved and you're calling your doctor, that's appropriate — but be prepared to be redirected to the ER if they hear the words "transient neurological symptoms." And honestly? If you've had any of the four patterns I just described, going directly to the ER is reasonable. Don't feel like you're overreacting. You're not.

**Step 3: Say the right words.**

This is the part that most people don't know. When you get to the ER or your doctor's office, the words you use determine how fast you're evaluated. Here's what I tell my patients to say:

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "I had a transient neurological episode. [Describe symptom]. It lasted [duration] and resolved completely. I need to be evaluated for TIA."]

"I had a transient neurological episode. I lost vision in my right eye for four minutes. It resolved completely. I need to be evaluated for TIA."

[PAUSE]

Those words — "transient neurological episode" and "evaluated for TIA" — trigger a specific clinical pathway. You should get a neurological examination, imaging (CT or MRI of the brain), carotid imaging (ultrasound or CT angiography), and an EKG. Ideally within 24 hours.

If you say "I had a weird thing with my eye but it went away," you might get an ophthalmology referral for next month. The words matter.

[LEAN IN]

I'm not telling you to be dramatic. I'm telling you to be precise. There's a difference. Precision gets you the right evaluation. Drama gets you a sedative.

### WHAT THE EVALUATION SHOULD LOOK LIKE

Let me tell you what a proper TIA workup includes — because not every ER or urgent care does this comprehensively, and you should know what to expect.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "The TIA Workup — What You Should Receive"]

**1. Neurological Examination**
A doctor should test your vision, facial symmetry, arm strength, sensation, coordination, and language. Even if your symptoms have resolved, subtle findings can still be present.

**2. Brain Imaging**
An MRI with diffusion-weighted imaging is the gold standard. A CT scan is often done first because it's faster and more available — it's good for ruling out hemorrhage but can miss small ischemic events. If the CT is normal but your symptoms were classic for TIA, push for an MRI.

**3. Carotid Imaging**
This is the one that's most often skipped. A carotid ultrasound or CT angiography of the neck vessels. This is where the vulnerable plaque lives. If you had a TIA and nobody images your carotid arteries, the workup is incomplete.

[PAUSE]

I want to be direct about this: carotid imaging is not optional in a TIA workup. It's standard of care. If you're told "your CT was normal, you're fine" without carotid imaging, ask specifically: "Were my carotid arteries imaged?" If the answer is no, ask why.

**4. Cardiac Monitoring**
An EKG at minimum. Ideally, a period of cardiac monitoring — because atrial fibrillation can also cause embolic strokes, and it can be intermittent. A single EKG might miss it.

**5. Blood Work**
Complete blood count, metabolic panel, coagulation studies, lipid panel, HbA1c. These help identify contributing factors.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "If your TIA workup didn't include carotid imaging, it was incomplete."]
