Now here's the part that matters. If one of those three systems starts to fail, your brain covers for it. You'd never know. But if two of them go? That's when people fall. And a fall after sixty isn't a bump on the hip. It's the number one cause of injury-related death in adults over sixty-five. Not heart disease. Not car accidents. Falls.

So today, I'm going to walk you through the same two-minute screening I do with patients in my office. You're going to find out — right now, while you watch this — which of your three balance systems are strong and which one might be slipping. ### [SECTION 1 — THE THREE SYSTEMS — 0:45–4:00]

[ANIMATED GRAPHIC: Three connected nodes labeled "EYES," "INNER EAR," "FEET & ANKLES" with the brain in the center as a hub. Simple, clean, medical-drawing style.]

Alright. Before we test anything, you need to understand what we're testing. Because this is where most balance advice gets it wrong — they treat balance as one thing. It's not. It's three completely separate systems feeding information to your brain.

**System number one: your eyes.**

Your visual system tells your brain where your body is in space. Not just what you're looking at — it detects the horizon, the tilt of surfaces, the movement around you. About seventy percent of your balance information during normal standing comes from your eyes. Seventy percent. That's why it's harder to stand still in the dark.

[ON-SCREEN: "70% of balance = VISION" with a simple eye icon]

**System number two: your inner ear — the vestibular system.**

Deep inside each ear, there are three fluid-filled loops called semicircular canals. When you move your head, that fluid shifts, and tiny hair cells detect the direction and speed of that movement. It's essentially your body's gyroscope. It tells your brain: "We're turning left. We're tilting forward. We're accelerating."

[ANIMATED GRAPHIC: Cross-section of inner ear showing semicircular canals with fluid moving. Keep it simple — no need for full anatomical detail.]

This system works even with your eyes closed. It's why you can ride a bicycle in the dark — your inner ear knows where you are even when your eyes can't help.

**System number three: proprioception — your feet and ankles.**

This one surprises people. The soles of your feet, the joints in your ankles, the tendons in your legs — they're packed with sensors that constantly report to your brain: "The ground is tilting left. Pressure is shifting to the outside of the right foot. We're on carpet, not tile."

[ON-SCREEN: Close-up graphic of foot sole with pressure-point sensors highlighted]

You know that wobble you feel when you stand on one leg? That's proprioception working overtime, making micro-corrections dozens of times per second. You don't feel those corrections — but you'd notice instantly if they stopped.

[DR. KANE — conversational, almost like he's thinking out loud]

So here's what I want you to hold onto. These three systems vote. Your brain takes input from all three and decides: "Are we stable, or are we about to fall?" Under normal conditions, all three agree. But after sixty, one of them starts sending weaker signals. And your brain — being the remarkable thing it is — compensates. It leans harder on the other two.

That's why most people don't know they have a balance problem. The compensation works. Until it doesn't.

### [SECTION 2 — THE "2 OUT OF 3" RULE — 4:00–6:30]

[ON-SCREEN: Three circles in a triangle. Two are green, one is yellow. Text: "1 system failing = Brain compensates. You feel fine."]

Now, here's the insight I want you to carry with you. I call it the "two out of three" rule, and I don't think you'll hear this from anyone else, because it came from watching patients over thirty years and noticing a pattern.

Your brain can fully compensate for one failing system. One. If your eyes are getting weaker but your inner ear and feet are strong — you'll never notice. If your inner ear is declining but your vision and proprioception are solid — you'll feel fine. The brain reroutes. It adapts. It's extraordinary at this.

[ON-SCREEN: Now two circles turn red. Text: "2 systems failing = Compensation breaks down. Fall risk rises sharply."]

But when two of the three systems are compromised? The brain can't compensate anymore. It's getting bad information from two out of three sources, and it starts making mistakes. That's when you see the subtle signs: holding the wall a little longer in the bathroom. Hesitating before stepping off a curb. Avoiding the dark hallway at night without realizing why.

[PAUSE — let it land]

And here's what concerns me most. Most people over sixty have no idea which system is their weak one. They know they're "not as steady as they used to be," but they can't tell you why. They don't know if it's their eyes, their ears, or their feet. And without knowing which system is failing, they can't do anything about it.

That's what this test is for.

[DR. KANE — warm, encouraging]

I'm not trying to scare you. I'm trying to give you information. Because once you know which system needs attention, there are very specific things you can do. But first — let's see where you stand.

If you couldn't hold either leg for ten seconds without significant instability, that suggests your vestibular system — your inner ear gyroscope — may be contributing less than it should.

[ON-SCREEN: Summary graphic — "Single Leg Stand Results"]

[ON-SCREEN: "TEST 3 of 3 — HEAD TURN TEST" with timer]

[ANIMATED GRAPHIC: Person standing with feet shoulder-width apart. Arrows showing slow head turning left and right. A "DANGER" icon appears if they feel dizzy.]

**This third test isolates your vestibular system. It's the one I find most revealing.**

Back to center. Keep doing this, turning your head side to side, about one full cycle every two seconds.

[DR. KANE — measured pace]

Keep your eyes open. Focus on that spot on the wall. Keep turning your head left... and right... left... and right. I want you to do this for fifteen seconds.

[ON-SCREEN: Timer — 15 seconds]

[PAUSE — 15 seconds]

[ON-SCREEN: Timer — 15 seconds, "EYES CLOSED"]

[PAUSE — 15 seconds]

[DR. KANE — thoughtful]

Okay. Here's what this test reveals. When you turn your head with your eyes open, your brain uses your eyes to help stabilize the world. You see the room staying still, and that visual input helps override any confusing signals from your inner ear.

But when you close your eyes and keep turning your head? Your brain is now relying entirely on the vestibular system and proprioception. If your vestibular system is compromised, you'll feel a sense of spinning, swaying, or the room moving — even though you're standing still.

[ON-SCREEN: Summary graphic — "Head Turn Test Results"]
- ✅ No dizziness with eyes open or closed = Vestibular: GOOD
- ⚠️ Mild dizziness with eyes closed only = Early vestibular decline
- ❌ Significant dizziness, spinning, or need to grab support = Vestibular: NEEDS ATTENTION

[DR. KANE]

If you felt dizzy during that test — especially with your eyes closed — please mention it to your doctor. There are specific vestibular rehabilitation exercises that can make a remarkable difference. But your doctor needs to know.

### [SECTION 8 — WHAT TO DO WITH YOUR RESULTS — 19:30–21:30]

[ON-SCREEN: "WHAT NOW?" with three action boxes]

Now, let me give you something practical. Because knowing which system is weak is only useful if you know what to do about it.

**If proprioception was your weak system** — your feet and ankles need retraining. The single best thing you can do is practice standing on one leg while you brush your teeth. Every morning. Alternate legs. It sounds almost too simple, but the research on this is strong. A 2019 study in the Journal of the American Geriatrics Society found that single-leg standing practice reduced fall risk by over forty percent in adults over sixty-five. Also — check your footwear. Thick, cushioned soles actually reduce the sensory input your feet receive. Thin-soled shoes or barefoot practice on a safe surface can help retrain those sensors.

**If your vestibular system was weak** — this is where vestibular rehabilitation therapy, or VRT, is genuinely transformative. It's a specific set of exercises prescribed by a physical therapist trained in vestibular disorders. It's not general exercise — it's targeted retraining of the inner ear-to-brain connection. Ask your doctor for a referral. The success rate for improving vestibular function with proper rehabilitation is very high.

[ON-SCREEN: "VRT = Vestibular Rehabilitation Therapy — Ask your doctor"]

**If your visual system is the concern** — get your eyes checked. Not just a glasses prescription — a comprehensive eye exam that includes depth perception and contrast sensitivity. Both of these decline with age and both directly affect balance. Also, make sure your home is well-lit, especially hallways and bathrooms. Night lights aren't just for children — they're a balance aid.

[DR. KANE — wrapping up, sincere]

And here's the thing I really want you to hear. Balance isn't fixed. It's not like your height — it's a skill. And like any skill, it can be trained, improved, and maintained. I've seen eighty-year-olds dramatically improve their balance with the right exercises. The key is knowing where to start. And now you do.
