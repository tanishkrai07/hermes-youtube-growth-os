### SCRIPT

[GRAPHIC: Dr. Kane in frame, warm lighting, no white coat — conversational setting]

Most of us know our blood type from a blood drive, a medical form, or maybe a school science project. A-positive. O-negative. AB. It's one of those facts about yourself that sits in a drawer somewhere — vaguely interesting, not really relevant to daily life. At least, that's what I thought for years.

[PAUSE]

Then I started seeing a pattern in my practice. Two patients, same age, same condition, same medication — and one is doing great while the other is struggling with side effects. For a while, I'd chalk it up to the usual variables: weight, kidney function, other medications. But after seeing it enough times with enough patients, I started looking deeper. And what I found was something most doctors don't have time to discuss in a fifteen-minute appointment. Your blood type — the A, B, O on your medical card — it's not just about transfusions. It actually influences how your body handles medications. And after sixty, when most people are managing at least two or three prescriptions, this matters more than you'd think.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Your blood type affects how medications work in your body"]
[GRAPHIC: Animated blood cells flowing through a vessel]

Now, before we go further — I want to be clear about something. I'm not telling anyone to change their medications based on their blood type. That's a conversation between you and your prescribing doctor. What I want to do today is explain the mechanism. Because once you understand WHY this happens, you can ask better questions at your next appointment. And better questions lead to better care. That's what this channel is about.

Let me start with the biology, because once you see it, it makes perfect sense.

[GRAPHIC: Zoom into blood vessel wall, endothelial cells, von Willebrand factor molecules]

Inside your blood, there's a protein called von Willebrand factor. You've probably never heard of it, but it's essential for clotting. When you get a cut or an injury, von Willebrand factor acts like a molecular bridge — it helps platelets stick together and plug the gap. It's also a carrier for another clotting protein, factor VIII. Together, they're a big part of your body's ability to stop bleeding.

Here's where blood type comes in. The ABO gene — the one that determines whether you're type A, B, AB, or O — doesn't just put a label on your red blood cells. It also affects how much von Willebrand factor circulates in your blood. People with blood type O consistently have about twenty-five to thirty percent lower levels of von Willebrand factor compared to people with non-O types. That's not a small difference. And it's been confirmed across multiple large studies.

[ROLL DATA ON SCREEN: "Blood Type O = 25-30% lower von Willebrand factor vs. non-O types"]

A massive study published in *Circulation* in 2016 — using data from over 1.1 million blood donors in Sweden and Denmark with a median follow-up of 12.6 years — found that people with non-O blood types had nearly double the risk of venous thromboembolism. The incidence rate ratio was 1.80. They also had a ten percent higher risk of myocardial infarction and a seven percent higher risk of stroke, compared to type O individuals.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Circulation 2016 | n=1,112,072 | Non-O blood type: 1.80x VTE risk, 1.10x MI risk"]

Now, here's the key sentence from that study that most people miss. Non-O blood groups alone explained more than thirty percent of venous thromboembolic events in that population. Thirty percent. That's a enormous population-level effect from a single genetic variable.

[PAUSE — let that land]

So what does this mean for medications?

When your doctor prescribes a blood thinner — warfarin, Eliquis, Xarelto, aspirin — they're making a calculation. They're trying to thin your blood enough to prevent a clot, but not so much that you bleed excessively. That's a narrow window. And what they may not be accounting for is where you're starting from. A type O person walks in with naturally lower clotting factors. A type AB person walks in with naturally higher ones. Same dose, different baseline, different outcome.

[B-ROLL: Close-up of medication bottles, prescription labels, a doctor writing a prescription]

And this isn't theoretical. A 2016 study in the *Journal of Thrombosis and Haemostasis* — led by Albánez and colleagues — examined von Willebrand factor and factor VIII levels across 207 individuals and found something I find really important. The interaction between aging and blood type is bidirectional and compounding. In younger people, the difference in von Willebrand factor levels between type O and non-O is relatively modest — about 0.13 units per milliliter. But in older individuals, that gap widens to 0.57 units per milliliter. So as you age, the effect of your blood type on your clotting system actually gets stronger.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Journal of Thrombosis and Haemostasis 2016 | VWF gap: 0.13 U/mL (young) → 0.57 U/mL (old)"]

Read that again. The older you are, the more your blood type matters for your clotting system. And the older you are, the more likely you're on a medication that affects clotting. These two facts converge right around age sixty — exactly when most people start managing cardiovascular risk with prescriptions.

[PAUSE]

[GRAPHIC: Animation showing liver metabolism, CYP450 enzymes breaking down drug molecules]

Now let me add another layer, because it gets even more interesting.

Your blood type also appears to influence your lipid profile — which matters if you're on statins. A 2025 analysis from the Taiwan Biobank, published in the journal *Life*, looked at 146,374 participants and found that blood type A was associated with a sixteen percent increased risk of hyperlipidemia compared to type O. Type A individuals also had higher total cholesterol — about 2.3 percent higher — and LDL cholesterol that was 3.3 percent higher.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Life 2025 | n=146,374 | Type A: 16% ↑ hyperlipidemia, LDL +3.3% vs. Type O"]

Three-point-three percent higher LDL might not sound dramatic on its own. But imagine you're a type A individual, you're sixty-three, you've been put on a moderate-dose statin, and your doctor is measuring your response. If your baseline LDL was already higher because of your blood type — not because of your diet, not because of your exercise, but because of your genetics — then a standard dose might not be enough to get you to target. You might need a higher dose or an additional medication. And your doctor might wonder why the statin "isn't working" without realizing there's a genetic variable underneath.

[B-ROLL: Doctor reviewing lab results with patient, lipid panel printout]

Put these two things together — the clotting factor difference AND the lipid difference — and your blood type starts to look less like a curiosity and more like a meaningful variable that affects at least two major categories of medication: anticoagulants and statins. Those are two of the most commonly prescribed drug classes for adults over sixty.

[PAUSE — lean slightly toward camera]

Let me be honest about something. This is not yet standard practice in most clinics. Doctors don't routinely check your blood type before prescribing warfarin or atorvastatin. They use weight, kidney function, liver enzymes, other medications — but not blood type. Partly because large-scale clinical trials haven't incorporated ABO stratification yet, and partly because medicine moves slowly on genetic variables that were established at birth and can't be changed.

But just because it's not standard practice doesn't mean it's not relevant. The research is there. The mechanism is clear. And for a patient who feels like their medication "doesn't quite work" or who's experiencing side effects that seem disproportionate — knowing your blood type could be a useful piece of the puzzle. Something to bring up. Something to explore.

[GRAPHIC: Simple animated checklist — "Know your blood type" appears as a new item]

Alright — so what can you actually do with this information? Here's where I want to be practical, because I'm not in the business of giving you things to worry about without things to act on.

First: know your blood type. I know that sounds basic, but surveys consistently show that a significant percentage of adults — some estimates say around a third — don't actually know their blood type with certainty. They guess. If you haven't had it confirmed, ask at your next blood draw. Know it. Write it down. Keep it with your medication list.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Action Step 1: Know your blood type — and keep it with your medication list"]

Second: when your doctor prescribes a new medication — especially a blood thinner or a statin — mention your blood type and ask whether it affects the dosing decision. You don't need to be pushy about it. Just: "I know I'm type A. Does that matter for this medication?" That question opens a door. Maybe your doctor will say there's no data yet — and that's an honest answer. Or maybe your doctor will say, "Actually, yes, we should monitor you more closely." Either way, you've had a better conversation than most patients have.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Action Step 2: Ask your doctor — 'Does my blood type matter for this medication?'"]

Third: if you've been on a medication for months and the results feel off — your blood isn't thinning enough, your cholesterol isn't dropping as expected, you're having more bruising than you think is normal — bring up the blood type angle. Ask if it might be contributing to an unusual response. Frame it as a question, not a demand. You're partnering with your doctor, not challenging them.

[PAUSE]

And here's one more thing that I think people overlook. When you're on multiple medications — which again, is most people over sixty — the variables multiply. Each drug is being metabolized, cleared, and distributed according to your unique physiology. Your blood type is one of those physiological variables. It's not the biggest one, but it's real, it's measurable, and it doesn't change. Unlike your kidney function or your weight, which fluctuate, your blood type is a fixed genetic marker that's been there your whole life. Stable variables are actually useful in medicine because they give you a baseline.

[B-ROLL: Close-up of a hand reviewing a medication list on paper, then typing into a phone notes app]

I had a patient — I'll call him Robert — who came to me because he'd been on warfarin for almost a year and his INR kept swinging. For those who don't know, INR measures how fast your blood clots. On warfarin, you want it in a specific range — too low and it's not preventing clots, too high and you risk bleeding. Robert's was bouncing around like a ping-pong ball. His GP had adjusted the dose several times. He'd been told to watch his vitamin K intake, which he did. Nothing seemed to stabilize it.

I looked at everything — his diet, his other meds, his liver and kidney function. Everything looked fine. Then I noticed something: Robert was blood type AB, and nobody had factored that in. His baseline von Willebrand factor and因子 VIII levels were naturally higher than someone on the same dose who was type O. It wasn't the only factor in his situation, but I suspected it was contributing. I didn't change his medication — that's his prescribing doctor's decision — but I suggested he bring this up. He did. His INR monitoring was adjusted, and it became more stable.

[PAUSE — that's the story, let it breathe]

I'm not saying blood type was the sole reason. Medicine is rarely that simple. But it was a missing variable. And in medicine, missing variables are where problems hide.

Let me zoom out for a moment and put this in a bigger picture.

[GRAPHIC: Wide shot — human body silhouette with highlighted vascular system]

We're in the early stages of what's called precision medicine. The idea that treatment should be tailored to your individual biology — your genes, your metabolism, your specific risk factors — rather than applied as a one-size-fits-all protocol. It's where medicine is heading, and it's exciting. But right now, in most primary care settings, we're still using population-based dosing. The dose that works for most people gets prescribed to most people, and then we adjust based on response.

Your blood type is one of the oldest known genetic markers in medicine. We've known about ABO for over a century. And yet, we barely use it outside of transfusion medicine. That's starting to change, but slowly. In the meantime, you don't have to wait for the system to catch up. You can know your blood type, understand what the research says, and have an informed conversation with your doctor.

That's not playing doctor. That's being an informed patient. And informed patients get better outcomes. The data on that is very clear.

[PAUSE]

Before I wrap up, let me circle back to the statin piece, because I think it's underappreciated.

If you have blood type A and your doctor has you on a statin, the research from that Taiwan Biobank study suggests your baseline cardiovascular risk profile may be slightly different from someone on the same statin who's type O. Your LDL might be a bit harder to bring down with a standard dose. That doesn't mean statins don't work for type A — they absolutely do. Statins are one of the most effective medications in cardiovascular medicine for all blood types. But it might mean your doctor needs to be a bit more aggressive with dosing, or consider adding a second agent like ezetimibe, to get you to your target. If nobody thinks about the blood type variable, they might just see a statin that's "not working as expected" and not know why.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Blood type doesn't change whether statins work — but it may affect what dose you need"]

The same logic applies in reverse. If you're type O, you're starting from a slightly lower baseline clotting risk and slightly lower LDL. Your standard dose of a blood thinner or statin might work just fine, and you might not need the higher end of dosing. That's actually good news — it means potentially fewer side effects at lower doses.

[PAUSE — transition to close]

I want to be careful here not to overstate this. Your blood type is one variable among many. It's not the dominant factor in how medications work for you. Age, weight, kidney function, liver function, other medications, diet — all of these matter more on a day-to-day basis. But blood type is the variable that's been sitting there this whole time that nobody's talking about in the context of medication response. And after sixty, when you're more likely to be on multiple prescriptions, when your liver and kidney function are changing, when the margin for error gets smaller — every variable matters a little more.

So here's what I'd like you to take away from this. Know your blood type. Bring it up with your doctor the next time a new medication is prescribed, especially if it's a blood thinner or a statin. Not because it's the most important thing in the room, but because it's the thing nobody else is mentioning. And sometimes, the thing nobody mentions is the thing that makes the difference.

[B-ROLL: Warm shot of Dr. Kane, slightly off-center framing, soft background]

If you found this helpful — and if you've never thought about your blood type in this context before, I suspect you might have — I'd genuinely love to keep you in the loop. I put out new videos every week that help you understand what's happening inside your body after sixty. Not just what to do, but why. That distinction matters to me, and I think it matters to you. If you subscribe, you won't miss the next one.

Next week, I'm covering something else that doesn't get discussed enough — how your body sends you signals through things you'd never think to pay attention to. Things like your nails. I know that sounds random, but stick with me. The vascular medicine angle on nail changes after sixty is genuinely fascinating, and it might save someone an early diagnosis they wouldn't have caught otherwise.

I'll see you in the next one.
