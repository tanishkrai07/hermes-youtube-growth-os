## SCRIPT

[OPEN ON CAMERA — Dr. Kane, warm and direct, no dramatic music, no alarm]

I want to tell you about a patient of mine. His name was Richard. He's 71.

About eight months ago, Richard came in for a routine follow-up. Blood pressure fine. Cholesterol fine. EKG fine. Everything on paper looked... fine.

But as I was wrapping up, almost as an afterthought, he said something that I haven't been able to stop thinking about.

He said — and I'm quoting him directly — "Doc, I've been getting a little winded in the mornings. Nothing serious. Just... when I'm getting dressed. Hasn't always been like that."

[SLOW DOWN — this is the moment]

We did a stress test. Then a catheterization. He had a 90% blockage in his left anterior descending artery. That's what cardiologists call "the widow maker."

Six months before that appointment, Richard started getting winded putting on his shirt. Not chest pain. Not arm numbness. Not any of the things you've been told to watch for. Just... slightly more breathless than he used to be, doing something he'd done ten thousand times.

[LEAN IN]

That's what I want to talk about today. The symptom that nobody names — the one that shows up months, sometimes six months or more, before a heart attack. And the reason it's so easy to miss.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "The Morning Exertional Gap"]

Let me be clear about what this is NOT. This is not dramatic chest-clutching pain. This is not the Hollywood heart attack. This is something much quieter. Much more subtle. And because of that — much more dangerous, in a way, because it doesn't trigger alarm.

Here's what it actually looks like.

[PAUSE]

You're getting dressed in the morning. Maybe you're pulling a shirt over your head. Maybe you're tying your shoes. Maybe you're walking from the bedroom to the kitchen. And you notice — maybe not even consciously at first — that you need to catch your breath. Not gasping. Not alarming. Just... a little more effort than before.

Or you're carrying the groceries in from the car. Same bags you've always carried. But now you have to put them down halfway to the kitchen. Or you pause at the bottom of the stairs for just a second.

You don't think anything of it. Or you do, but what you tell yourself is: "I'm getting older. I'm out of shape. I should probably exercise more."

[EMPHASIZE]

What if I told you that for about 72% of people who eventually have a heart attack, this kind of subtle breathlessness was present — on average — six to eight weeks before the event? And for a significant number of people, it started even earlier. Months earlier.

That number comes from a 2023 study published in Circulation. Over 1,600 patients who'd had myocardial infarctions were asked to recall their symptoms in the weeks and months before. The most commonly reported prodromal symptom was exertional dyspnea — getting winded with activity. Not chest pain. Getting winded.

[ON-SCREEN: Circulation 2023 — 72% reported exertional dyspnea before MI]

Now here's the question that matters: WHY does this happen? What's actually going on in the body?

Because understanding the mechanism is what makes this useful to you. It's not about scaring you. It's about giving you a framework to recognize what your body is telling you.

[SLOW DOWN — explaining clearly]

Your heart is a pump. It pumps blood through your coronary arteries — the arteries that feed the heart muscle itself. When those arteries start to narrow from plaque buildup — and this happens gradually, over years, not overnight — the heart muscle still gets enough blood when you're at rest. Sitting in a chair. Sleeping. Watching TV.

But the moment you ask your body to do more — stand up, walk, carry something, even just lift your arms to get dressed — your heart needs more blood flow. And if the coronary arteries are significantly narrowed, they can't deliver the extra flow the heart needs for that increased workload.

So what does the body do? It compensates. And one of the fastest compensatory mechanisms is — you breathe faster.

[ON-SCREEN: Graphic showing narrowed coronary artery → heart needs more oxygen → lungs compensate by increasing breathing rate]

Your lungs aren't the problem. Your heart is. But because the lungs can increase oxygen intake quickly — faster than the heart can increase its own blood supply when the coronaries are blocked — the first symptom you notice isn't chest pain. It's breathlessness.

This is what's called an "angina equivalent." Classic angina is chest pain from the heart not getting enough oxygen. But in many people — especially women, especially people over 60, especially people with diabetes — the angina equivalent isn't pain. It's dyspnea. Breathlessness.

[PAUSE]

Here's why mornings are specifically risky.

Your body has a natural cortisol surge between about 6 and 9 AM. Cortisol is a stress hormone — it raises your blood pressure, it increases your heart rate, it makes your blood slightly more viscous. This is normal. It's your body's way of waking you up.

[SLOW DOWN]

But if you already have significant coronary narrowing, that morning cortisol surge is like turning up the volume on a speaker that's already distorted. Your heart is working harder — higher blood pressure, faster heart rate — and it needs more oxygen to do that work. But the narrowed arteries can't deliver.

So in the morning, when the cortisol surge hits, the compensatory breathlessness becomes MORE noticeable. That's why Richard noticed it getting dressed. That's why so many people report this pattern specifically in the morning.

[ON-SCREEN: Graphic — circadian cortisol curve with "symptom window" highlighted 6-9 AM]

Now — and this is critical — let me explain why this almost always gets missed.

There are three reasons.

The first: it's gradual. This doesn't happen overnight. It's not "I couldn't breathe this morning." It's "I've been... slightly more breathless... I guess... for a few months maybe?" The slow onset means your baseline shifts. You adapt without realizing it.

The second: there's no pain. Our medical system — and frankly, our whole culture around heart disease — is built around chest pain. Chest pressure. Left arm pain. If you don't have those, neither you nor your doctor thinks "heart." You think "lungs" or "I'm out of shape" or "just getting older."

[LEAN IN]

And the third reason — this is the one that really bothers me. When you mention breathlessness to your doctor, the first thing that often happens is they listen to your lungs. And your lungs sound fine. Because the lungs ARE fine. The problem is upstream. But a normal lung exam gets documented as "no abnormalities noted" and the conversation moves on.

[PAUSE]

So here's what I want you to understand. And I want to be very specific, because vague advice doesn't help anyone.

The symptom that should prompt a cardiac evaluation is not sudden, severe breathlessness. That has many causes, and most of them aren't cardiac.

What SHOULD prompt evaluation is a CHANGE — noticed over weeks to months — in your breathing during activities that previously didn't cause any difficulty at all.

[ON-SCREEN — text checklist with checkmarks]

Specifically:
- Getting winded tying your shoes when you didn't before
- Having to pause midway up a flight of stairs you used to do without thinking
- Carrying groceries and needing to rest halfway
- Putting on a shirt overhead and feeling like you need a moment
- Walking the dog at the same pace but needing to slow down

These aren't dramatic symptoms. That's exactly why they're dangerous. They're easy to explain away. "I didn't sleep well." "It's hot out." "I'm just getting old."

But here's what the evidence says. That Circulation study I mentioned — the 72% number — the median time from first prodromal symptom to actual cardiac event was 6.2 weeks. Some patients had symptoms for MONTHS before their event was recognized.

Six months of warning, and most people — and most doctors — never connect the dots.

[EMPHASIZE — genuine concern, not alarm]

Now I need to be responsible here, because I don't want to create unnecessary fear. Let me tell you what this is NOT saying.

This is NOT saying that every time you're a little out of breath, you're about to have a heart attack. That's not true and it's not helpful.

What this IS saying is that if you notice a CHANGE — a new pattern of breathlessness during specific activities that used to be easy for you — that change deserves evaluation. Not panic. Evaluation.

[PAUSE]

So what should you actually do if this resonates with you?

First — and I know this sounds almost too simple — start tracking it. Not obsessively. Just mentally notice: "Am I more breathless doing this than I was a few months ago?" Pick one or two specific activities that are part of your daily routine. Climbing your stairs. Getting dressed. Walking to the mailbox. And honestly assess whether those feel harder than they used to.

Second — if you notice a change that persists for more than two to three weeks, bring it to your doctor. And here's the specific language that will help them take it seriously.

[ON-SCREEN — text: "Script for your doctor"]

Say this: "I've noticed progressive exertional breathlessness over the past several weeks doing activities that didn't previously cause me difficulty. It's worse in the mornings. I'd like to be evaluated for cardiac causes, including a stress test."

That phrase — "progressive exertional breathlessness" — is clinical language that signals to your doctor that this isn't a casual observation. It's a documented change over time.

Third — if you have other risk factors — diabetes, high blood pressure, high cholesterol, family history of heart disease, smoking history — lower your threshold for bringing this up. The more risk factors you have, the more a new breathing change matters.

[SLOW DOWN — warm close]

I think about Richard a lot. Not because his story is tragic — it's actually the opposite. He's doing great now. Stent placed. Cardiac rehab completed. He texted me a photo of himself walking his granddaughter to school last month.

But he almost wasn't here to do that.

The symptom that saved his life wasn't one anyone had warned him about. It was slight. Easy to dismiss. Easy to attribute to age.

But because he mentioned it — almost as an afterthought, on his way out the door — we caught it in time.

That's what I want for you. That your body's quiet signals get heard — by you, and by whoever's helping you navigate your health.

If this was helpful, I'd love to keep you in the loop. I put out new videos every week that help you understand what's actually happening inside your body after sixty.

And next, I'm covering something that might surprise you — why your ears ring louder at night, and what that has to do with your blood vessels. I'll see you there.

[SMILE — genuine, warm]

Take care of yourself.

Let me ask you something.

Have you noticed yourself getting slightly winded doing something that used to be easy? Not dramatic — just... putting on a shirt. Tying your shoes. Carrying the groceries from the car.

If that's a recent change — over the last weeks or months — I want you to hear this.

72% of people who have a heart attack had this exact symptom beforehand. Getting winded with activity. Sometimes six to eight weeks before the event. Often dismissed as "just getting older."

Here's why it happens. Your coronary arteries narrow gradually over time. At rest, your heart gets enough blood. But the moment you do *anything* that asks your heart to work harder — even just raising your arms — a narrowed artery can't deliver the extra blood flow.

So your body compensates. You breathe faster. You don't feel chest pain. You just feel... a little more out of breath than you remember being.

This is called an angina equivalent. And it's one of the most missed warning signs because it's so subtle.

If you've noticed a NEW pattern of breathlessness doing tasks that used to be easy — progressive, over weeks — mention it to your doctor. Specifically say: "I've noticed progressive exertional dyspnea."

That phrase gets their attention.

It got my patient's attention. And it might have saved his life.

I'm Dr. Victor Kane. I'll see you in the full video.
