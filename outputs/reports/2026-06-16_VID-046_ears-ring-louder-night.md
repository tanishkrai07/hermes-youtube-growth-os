## SCRIPT

[OPEN ON CAMERA — Dr. Kane, close, conversational, as if speaking to one person]

Right now, as you're watching this, there's probably a sound in your head that isn't coming from your phone or your speakers.

A ringing. A buzzing. A high-pitched tone that's just... there.

You probably don't notice it most of the day. Traffic, conversation, the TV, the hum of daily life — it covers it up. Buries it.

But at night. When you lie down — when the room goes quiet — that sound gets louder. Or at least, it FEELS louder.

[LEAN IN]

What if I told you that the reason your ears ring louder at night is actually two completely different problems stacked on top of each other? And that one of those problems — if you have a specific type of tinnitus — might be telling you something about your arteries.

Not your ears. Your arteries.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Pulsatile vs. Non-Pulsatile: The Vascular Connection"]

Stay with me on this. It'll make sense.

Let's start with the simpler explanation first — the one that accounts for most of what people experience.

Sound masking. Your brain is incredibly good at filtering out signals it doesn't think are important. During the day, when there's ambient noise around you — even if it's subtle — your auditory processing centers prioritize those external sounds over the internal ringing. The tinnitus is still there. Your brain is literally choosing to ignore it.

[PAUSE]

At night, you remove the masking. And suddenly — that signal your brain was filtering out all day is right there, front and center. The tinnitus didn't actually get louder. The competition left the room.

This is well-established in the hearing research. A 2023 study in the Journal of the Association for Research in Otolaryngology demonstrated that ambient noise levels as low as 30 decibels — that's quieter than a whisper — significantly reduce the perceived loudness of tinnitus. Remove that ambient noise, and the perceived volume increases by what patients describe as two to three times.

That's explanation number one, and for most people, it's the whole story.

But here's where it gets interesting. And vascular.

[EMPHASIZE]

Some people don't just experience louder tinnitus at night. They experience DIFFERENT tinnitus at night. Specifically — the ringing becomes rhythmic. It pulses. Matches their heartbeat.

If that's you, I want you to really hear what I'm about to say. Because pulsatile tinnitus — tinnitus that pulses with your heartbeat — is a fundamentally different thing from regular tinnitus. And it has fundamentally different causes.

[ON-SCREEN: Side-by-side comparison graphic]
- Non-pulsatile: constant ringing/buzzing → inner ear/neurological
- Pulsatile: rhythmic whooshing → VASCULAR

Pulsatile tinnitus is the sound of blood flow. Literally. You're hearing your own blood moving through vessels near your inner ear. And in some cases, that sound is audible because the blood vessels themselves have changed.

[SLOW DOWN — explaining mechanism clearly]

Here's the anatomy. Your internal carotid artery — the big vessel that runs up through your neck to supply blood to your brain — passes extremely close to your inner ear structures. In some people, it passes within millimeters.

When those arteries are healthy and flexible, the blood flow is smooth — laminar flow, in physics terms. Silent. You can't hear it.

But when arteries stiffen — when the walls lose their elastic compliance — the flow becomes turbulent. And turbulent flow makes noise. Just like a river that's silent when it's flowing smoothly over a flat bed starts to roar when it hits rocks and changes direction.

[ON-SCREEN: Graphic — smooth arterial flow (silent) vs. stiff/irregular arterial flow (turbulent, audible)]

Now here's why nighttime makes pulsatile tinnitus specifically worse.

Your blood pressure follows a circadian pattern. In healthy people, BP drops by 10-20% during sleep — this is called "dipping." But in about 40% of adults over 60, the dipping is blunted or absent. Their blood pressure stays relatively high overnight. This is called a "non-dipping" pattern.

[LEAN IN]

Even if your blood pressure is "normal" during the day — even if your doctor has never flagged it — you could still be experiencing higher-than-ideal pressure at night. And that nocturnal pressure means more force pushing blood through stiffened, turbulent vessels.

More turbulence. More noise. Louder pulsatile tinnitus.

A 2023 study in the Journal of Neurology found that patients with pulsatile tinnitus had significantly higher nocturnal blood pressure variability compared to controls — even when daytime readings were identical. The tinnitus wasn't just "unmasked" at night. It was literally amplified by the nighttime hemodynamics.

[PAUSE]

This is the part that most tinnitus content on YouTube doesn't cover. Most videos about tinnitus talk about hearing damage. Noise exposure. Age-related hearing loss. And those are real causes. But the VASCULAR angle — the possibility that your tinnitus is a window into arterial stiffness — is almost never mentioned.

I find that remarkable. Because pulsatile tinnitus, when it's new or changing, can be an early sign of carotid artery stiffness that precedes other vascular symptoms by months or years.

Before we go further, I want to be very careful here. Pulsatile tinnitus is NOT always vascular. There are benign causes too. And I don't want to turn every person who hears a whooshing sound into a cardiac worrier.

So let me give you a framework for understanding when it matters and when it doesn't.

[ON-SCREEN — simple decision framework]

**When pulsatile tinnitus is usually NOT urgent:**
- It's been there for years, unchanged
- It's bilateral (both sides)
- It's soft and only noticed in very quiet environments
- You have no other vascular risk factors

**When pulsatile tinnitus warrants evaluation:**
- It's NEW — started within the last few months
- It's UNILATERAL (one side only)
- It's CHANGING — getting louder or more frequent
- It's accompanied by dizziness, visual changes, or headaches
- You have vascular risk factors — high BP, high cholesterol, diabetes, smoking history

[EMPHASIZE]

If your tinnitus is pulsatile AND any of those second-category features apply, bring it to your doctor. Specifically, mention that you have pulsatile tinnitus that worsens at night. The evaluation they should consider includes blood pressure monitoring — ideally 24-hour ambulatory monitoring, not just a single office reading — and in some cases, imaging of the carotid arteries.

Now — for the non-pulsatile group. The constant-ringing group. Here's what I'd like you to know, because it's actually hopeful.

[SLOW DOWN — practical, grounded]

Tinnitus that's simply "unmasked" at night is annoying. I'm not minimizing that. Sleep disruption from tinnitus is one of the most common complaints I hear. But it's not dangerous in the way that new pulsatile tinnitus can sometimes signal something structural.

For the masking-related nighttime tinnitus, there are genuinely effective strategies.

[ON-SCREEN — text list appearing as described]

First: don't sleep in perfect silence. I know that sounds counterintuitive, but adding low-level ambient sound — a fan, a white noise machine, even a quiet podcast on a sleep timer — gives your brain something external to process instead of turning up the gain on the internal ringing. That 30-decibel threshold I mentioned earlier is very easy to achieve. Most white noise machines have adjustable volume — you don't need it loud. Just enough to provide a sound floor.

Second: be aware that stress and fatigue amplify tinnitus perception. Not the tinnitus itself — the PERCEPTION of it. There's a gating mechanism in the thalamus that modulates how much auditory signal reaches conscious awareness. When you're exhausted or stressed, that gate opens wider. So the tinnitus that seems unbearable at 11 PM after a long day might be barely noticeable at 10 AM after a good night's sleep. Same tinnitus. Different gating.

Third — and this is the one most people don't consider — jaw tension. The temporomandibular joint is right next to your ear canal. If you clench your jaw at night — and many people do without knowing it — you can worsen tinnitus through muscular tension on the structures near the cochlea. If you wake up with a sore jaw or tight temples, this might be contributing. A night guard from your dentist can help.

[PAUSE]

I want to come back to the vascular piece for a moment, because I think it's important.

If you have any type of tinnitus — pulsatile or not — it's worth considering your vascular health as a contributing factor. Even non-pulsatile tinnitus can be influenced by blood flow to the cochlea. The inner ear is extraordinarily sensitive to blood supply — the cochlea has one of the highest metabolic_rates of any tissue in the body, and it's fed by a single artery with no redundant blood supply.

[EMPHASIZE]

That means anything that reduces microvascular blood flow — arterial stiffness, high blood pressure, high cholesterol, diabetes — can affect the cochlea's function and potentially worsen tinnitus.

So in a very real sense, the general vascular health practices that reduce your risk of heart attack and stroke — managing blood pressure, controlling blood sugar, regular exercise, not smoking — are also the best things you can do for your ears.

Your ears are listening to your cardiovascular system. Whether you realize it or not.

[SLOW DOWN — warm close, perspective]

Here's what I'd like you to take away tonight.

If your ears ring louder at night — first, know that the masking removal effect is real and common. It's not your imagination. Your brain was filtering it during the day, and at night it can't.

Second — if your tinnitus is pulsatile, rhythmic, matches your heartbeat — especially if it's new or one-sided — please mention it to your doctor. Ask specifically about 24-hour blood pressure monitoring. It's painless, it's inexpensive, and it might catch something a single office reading misses.

Third — whether it's pulsatile or not — your ears and your vascular system are connected in ways that most people never think about. Taking care of your blood vessels is taking care of your hearing.

I'll see you in the next one. If you found this helpful, subscribe — I put out new videos every week that help you understand what's happening inside your body after sixty.

Take care of yourself. And tonight — if that ringing seems loud, turn on a fan. Give your brain something else to listen to.

[SMILE — genuine]

Goodnight.

Quick question.

Do your ears ring? And does it seem louder when you lie down at night?

Here's why that happens — and it's not just one reason.

Reason one: sound masking. During the day, ambient noise quieter than a whisper tricks your brain into ignoring the ringing. At night, when the noise goes away, your brain stops filtering. What was always there suddenly fills the room.

Reason two — and this is the one nobody talks about. If your tinnitus is pulsINARY — whooshing, rhythmic, matching your heartbeat — it might actually be getting louder at night. Nocturnal blood pressure surges push harder through stiff arteries near your inner ear. The turbulence makes sound. Real sound. You're hearing your own blood flow.

If your whooshing tinnitus is new, or one-sided, or getting worse — mention it to your doctor. Ask for 24-hour blood pressure monitoring. A single office reading misses what happens while you sleep.
