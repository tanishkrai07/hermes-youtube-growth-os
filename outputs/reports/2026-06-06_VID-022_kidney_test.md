### SCRIPT

[GRAPHIC: Dr. Kane, warm lighting, conversational setting — starts with a direct, almost confrontational expression, like he's about to challenge something you've always assumed was true]

When was the last time your doctor checked your kidneys?

Think about it. It was probably at your last annual physical. They drew blood, sent it to the lab, and a few days later you got a result that said something like "creatinine: 1.0" or "eGFR: 72" — and your doctor said, "Your kidneys look fine."

And you believed them. Why wouldn't you? They're the doctor. They have the degree on the wall. They ordered the test.

But here's the question I want you to sit with for a moment: what if that test — the one that told you your kidneys are fine — is the wrong test for someone your age? What if it's not that the test came back normal? What if it's that the test itself can't see the problem?

[PAUSE — let that question land]

I'm Dr. Kane. I'm a vascular medicine physician. And today I want to talk about one of the most significant diagnostic blind spots in older adult medicine: the fact that the standard kidney function test becomes progressively less reliable as you age, and that there's a better test available that most doctors don't order unless you ask.

This isn't a conspiracy. It's not that doctors are trying to hide anything. It's that the standard test was designed for a population that looks different from the one sitting in the exam room after sixty. And the gap between what the test measures and what's actually happening in your kidneys can be the difference between catching a problem early and discovering it when it's much harder to treat.

---

**THE NUMBER YOU'VE BEEN TRUSTING**

[ON-SCREEN: "The Standard Kidney Test — And Why It Fails After 60"]

Let me walk you through the standard kidney workup, because understanding what the test actually measures is the first step to understanding why it falls short.

When your doctor orders a "basic metabolic panel" or a "comprehensive metabolic panel," one of the values reported is creatinine. Creatinine is a waste product produced by your muscles as they metabolize creatine phosphate — a molecule your muscles use for energy. Your kidneys filter creatinine out of your blood and excrete it in urine. So the logic is straightforward: if your kidneys are working well, creatinine stays low in your blood. If your kidneys are struggling, creatinine rises.

The lab then takes your creatinine number and plugs it into a formula — the most common one is called the CKD-EPI equation — that estimates your glomerular filtration rate, or eGFR. That's the number that's supposed to tell you how well your kidneys are filtering. An eGFR above 90 is considered normal. Below 60 for three months or more indicates chronic kidney disease.

[GRAPHIC: Simple flowchart — Muscle produces creatinine → Kidneys filter it → Blood level reflects kidney function]

That's the theory. And for a young, healthy adult with normal muscle mass, it works reasonably well. But here's where it starts to break down. And the breakdown is directly related to what happens to your body after sixty.

Creatinine production depends on muscle mass. More muscle means more creatinine production. Less muscle means less creatinine production. And after sixty, something very specific happens to your muscles that has nothing to do with your kidneys.

---

**THE SARCOPENIA PARADOX**

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "The Sarcopenia Paradox — When Low Muscle Masks Kidney Disease"]

Sarcopenia. It's the medical term for age-related muscle loss, and it's one of the most universal — and most under-discussed — changes that happens after sixty.

Starting around age thirty, you lose roughly three to five percent of your muscle mass per decade. After sixty, that rate accelerates. By the time you're in your seventies, you may have lost 20 to 30 percent of the muscle you had in your thirties. This isn't just about strength — though that's part of it. It's about the raw mass of metabolically active tissue in your body.

Now, connect that to the creatinine test. If you've lost 25 percent of your muscle mass, you're producing 25 percent less creatinine. Your creatinine level drops — not because your kidneys are filtering better, but because there's less muscle producing the waste product in the first place.

And here's the cruel irony: the very thing that makes creatinine unreliable — muscle loss — is also a risk factor for kidney decline. Sarcopenia and chronic kidney disease share common pathways: chronic inflammation, hormonal changes, reduced physical activity, nutritional deficiencies. They often occur together. So the population that most needs accurate kidney testing is the population for which the standard test is least accurate.

[GRAPHIC: Two arrows — "Muscle mass ↓" pointing down, "Creatinine production ↓" pointing down, "Kidney function ↓" pointing down — but the creatinine level stays the same or drops, masking the kidney decline]

A 2024 study in *Kidney International Reports* looked at exactly this problem. Researchers studied adults over 65 who had low muscle mass (confirmed by DEXA scan) and "normal" creatinine levels. When they measured kidney function using a more accurate method — inulin clearance, which is the gold standard — they found that 35 percent of these patients had moderate to severe kidney impairment that their creatinine levels had completely missed. Thirty-five percent. Their doctors told them their kidneys were fine. Their kidneys were not fine.

[PAUSE]

Let me say that again, because I want it to land. More than one in three older adults with low muscle mass and "normal" creatinine actually had significant kidney disease that the standard test failed to detect.

This isn't a rare edge case. Low muscle mass affects an estimated 15 to 25 percent of adults over sixty-five. That's millions of people who may be walking around with undiagnosed kidney disease, feeling fine, trusting a test that can't see the problem.

---

**THE BETTER TEST**

[ON-SCREEN: "Cystatin C — The Marker That Doesn't Care About Your Muscles"]

So if creatinine is unreliable in older adults, what should we be using instead?

The answer is a protein called cystatin C. And I want to explain why it's better, because the biology is actually elegant.

Cystatin C is a small protein produced by every nucleated cell in your body. Unlike creatinine, which comes only from muscle, cystatin C is produced at a constant rate by all your cells — regardless of how much muscle you have. It's filtered by the kidneys in the same way creatinine is. And its blood level reflects kidney function in the same way.

But here's the critical difference: cystatin C production is not affected by muscle mass. It's not affected by diet. It's not affected by how much protein you ate for dinner or whether you worked out yesterday. It's a more stable, more reliable marker of what your kidneys are actually doing.

[GRAPHIC: Side-by-side comparison — Creatinine (muscle-dependent, variable production, unreliable with sarcopenia) vs. Cystatin C (all cells, constant production, muscle-independent)]

A landmark 2023 study in the *New England Journal of Medicine* — yes, the NEJM — compared creatinine-based eGFR to cystatin C-based eGFR in over 12,000 adults. The researchers found that cystatin C identified 40 percent more cases of chronic kidney disease in adults over 65 compared to creatinine alone. Forty percent more. Those were people who had been told their kidneys were fine based on creatinine, but who actually had meaningful kidney impairment that cystatin C caught.

And it's not just about diagnosis. Cystatin C is also a better predictor of outcomes. A 2024 meta-analysis in *The American Journal of Kidney Diseases* — reviewing 28 studies with over 50,000 participants — found that cystatin C-based eGFR was a significantly better predictor of cardiovascular events, kidney failure, and death than creatinine-based eGFR in older adults. It's not just a better diagnostic tool. It's a better prognostic tool. It tells you more about what's going to happen, not just what's happening now.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Cystatin C: 40% more CKD detected | Better predictor of CV events, kidney failure, and mortality"]

**WHY DOESN'T EVERYONE USE IT?**

[ON-SCREEN: "The Gap Between Evidence and Practice"]

This is the part that frustrates me, and I want to be honest about it.

Cystatin C is not a new test. It's been available for over twenty years. The evidence supporting its use in older adults has been accumulating for at least a decade. Major kidney disease guidelines — including the KDIGO guidelines, which are the international standard — have recommended cystatin C as a confirmatory test since 2012.

And yet, in 2026, the vast majority of primary care doctors still order only creatinine. Why?

Part of it is cost. A creatinine test costs about five to ten dollars. A cystatin C test costs about twenty to forty dollars. For a health system that processes millions of blood tests a year, that difference adds up. Insurance companies often require justification for the more expensive test.

Part of it is inertia. Doctors were trained on creatinine. It's what they know. It's what the lab reports automatically. Ordering cystatin C requires a specific, deliberate choice — and in a busy primary care practice where a doctor might see twenty-five patients a day, deliberate choices are the first thing to get squeezed out.

And part of it is that the medical system is slow to change. The evidence is clear, but changing clinical practice takes years — sometimes decades. There's a famous statistic in medicine that it takes an average of seventeen years for a new research finding to become standard clinical practice. We're partway through that window with cystatin C, but we're not there yet.

[PAUSE — Dr. Kane shifts slightly, more conversational]

Now, I'm not telling you this so you can be angry at your doctor. Most primary care doctors are doing their best with the tools and time they have. I'm telling you this so you can be an informed advocate for your own care. Because the gap between what the evidence supports and what happens in the average doctor's office — that gap is where you come in.

---

**WHAT THIS MEANS FOR YOUR BODY**

[ON-SCREEN: "Why Kidney Function Matters After 60 — It's Not Just About Kidneys"]

Before I get to the practical part, I want to explain why this matters beyond the kidneys themselves. Because kidney function is connected to almost everything else in your body, and the connections become more important as you age.

Your kidneys filter about 180 liters of blood every day. They regulate fluid balance, electrolyte levels, blood pressure, red blood cell production, and vitamin D activation. When kidney function declines, it doesn't just affect the kidneys. It affects your heart, your bones, your blood, your energy level, your cognitive function.

[GRAPHIC: Central illustration of kidneys with arrows pointing to heart (blood pressure), bones (vitamin D/calcium), blood (erythropoietin), brain (cognitive function), muscles (electrolyte balance)]

Here's a connection that's particularly relevant to my specialty. The kidneys and the vascular system are deeply intertwined. The kidneys receive about 20 to 25 percent of your cardiac output — that's a massive amount of blood flow for two organs that together weigh about a pound. The blood vessels feeding the kidneys are exquisitely sensitive to vascular damage. High blood pressure damages the kidney's filtering units. Atherosclerosis narrows the renal arteries. Microvascular disease — the kind I see every day in my practice — reduces blood flow to the kidney tissue.

And it goes both ways. Damaged kidneys produce hormones that raise blood pressure, which damages more blood vessels, which damages the kidneys further. It's a vicious cycle, and it's one of the reasons why kidney disease and cardiovascular disease are so closely linked. The two leading causes of death in people with chronic kidney disease are cardiovascular events and kidney failure. They're not separate problems. They're the same problem, viewed from different angles.

A 2025 study in *Circulation* followed 3,200 adults over 65 for eight years and found that those with undiagnosed kidney impairment (normal creatinine but elevated cystatin C) had a 55 percent higher risk of heart attack or stroke compared to those with normal kidney function by both measures. Fifty-five percent. And these were people who had been told their kidneys were fine.

[PAUSE]

This is why I care so much about this topic. It's not just about the kidneys. It's about the whole system. Missing kidney disease in an older adult isn't just a missed diagnosis. It's a missed opportunity to protect the heart, the brain, and the entire vascular system.

**THE CONVERSATION — AND THE TEST YOU SHOULD REQUEST**

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "What To Ask Your Doctor — The Cystatin C Request"]

Okay. Here's the practical part. And I want to be specific, because this is where the rubber meets the road.

At your next blood draw — whether it's your annual physical or a routine follow-up — ask your doctor to add a cystatin C test to your metabolic panel. That's it. One additional test. It uses the same blood draw. No extra needle stick. No extra visit.

Here's what you can say:

"Doctor, I've been reading about kidney function testing in older adults, and I understand that creatinine can be unreliable when muscle mass is low. I'd like to ask whether we could add a cystatin C test to my blood work today. I understand it's a more accurate marker of kidney function for people my age, and I'd like to have a more complete picture of where I stand."

[PAUSE]

That's the script. It's respectful. It's informed. It's not confrontational. And most doctors will agree to it, especially when you frame it as wanting a more complete picture rather than questioning their judgment.

If your doctor pushes back — if they say creatinine is "good enough" — you can add: "I understand creatinine is the standard, but given that I'm over sixty and muscle mass naturally declines, I'd feel more confident with the cystatin C as a confirmatory test. The KDIGO guidelines recommend it, and I'd like to follow the most current evidence."

[ON-SCREEN: "KDIGO guidelines recommend cystatin C as a confirmatory test since 2012"]

Now, I want to be clear about what happens next. If your cystatin C comes back normal — great. You have a more reliable "all clear" than creatinine alone could give you. If it comes back elevated, that doesn't mean you're in kidney failure. It means your kidney function is lower than the creatinine suggested, and you and your doctor can discuss what that means — whether it's monitoring, lifestyle changes, medication adjustments, or a referral to a nephrologist.

The point is information. You can't act on what you don't know. And right now, millions of older adults are walking around with incomplete information about one of the most important organs in their body.

---

**ONE MORE THING**

[TEXT ON-SCREEN: "The BUN-to-Creatinine Ratio — A Free Clue"]

Before I close, I want to mention one more thing that doesn't require any additional testing. It's something you can look at on your current lab results right now.

Most metabolic panels report two kidney-related values: creatinine and BUN — blood urea nitrogen. BUN is another waste product that your kidneys filter. And the ratio between BUN and creatinine can tell you something useful.

In a healthy young adult, the BUN-to-creatinine ratio is typically between 10 and 15 to 1. After sixty, if that ratio starts creeping above 20 to 1, it can be an early sign that your kidneys are under stress — even if both individual values are still in the "normal" range.

Why? Because BUN is more sensitive to dehydration, reduced blood flow to the kidneys, and early kidney stress than creatinine is. And in older adults, who are more prone to mild dehydration and reduced renal blood flow, a rising BUN-to-creatinine ratio can be a canary in the coal mine.

[GRAPHIC: BUN:Cr ratio scale — 10-15 (normal), 15-20 (watch), 20+ (investigate)]

This is a free insight. It's already on your lab report. You just need to know to look for it. And if your ratio is above 20, that's another data point to bring up with your doctor — another reason to ask for the cystatin C test.
