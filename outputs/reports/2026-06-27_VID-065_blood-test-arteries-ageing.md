I had a patient — I'll call him Robert, that's not his name — who came to see me three years ago. He was 67. Walked every morning, ate well, never smoked. His cholesterol was beautiful. LDL of 95, HDL of 55, triglycerides under 100. His primary care doctor told him his numbers were excellent.

Robert had a heart attack four months later.

[PAUSE]

Not a small one. Anterior wall STEMI — that's the big one, the one people die from. We stented him open, he did well, he recovered. But I couldn't stop thinking about those numbers. Because his cholesterol was genuinely good. By every standard metric, Robert should have been low risk.

So what did we miss?

That question has stayed with me. And the answer — or at least the most important part of the answer — is something I want to share with you today. Because it's a test that almost nobody gets, and it might be the one that matters most after 60.

---

### What Your Cholesterol Test Actually Measures

Let me start with what we do measure, because understanding the gap requires understanding what the standard test is actually looking at.

When you get your lipid panel — that blood test you get every year — it measures four things. Total cholesterol, LDL cholesterol, HDL cholesterol, and triglycerides. Sometimes they add LDL particle number or apolipoprotein B if your doctor is being thorough.

Here's what those numbers actually tell you. They tell you how much cholesterol is in your blood. That's it. LDL is the amount of low-density lipoprotein carrying cholesterol through your bloodstream. HDL is the amount of high-density lipoprotein carrying it back. Triglycerides are fat in the blood after you eat.

[ON-SCREEN: Simple graphic showing standard lipid panel with 4 values labeled]

Now, that's useful information. I'm not saying it isn't. But here's what it doesn't tell you. It doesn't tell you whether that cholesterol is getting stuck in your artery walls. It doesn't tell you whether the plaque that might be forming is stable or unstable. It doesn't tell you how much inflammation is happening inside your arteries.

Think of it this way. Knowing your LDL is like knowing how much water is flowing through a pipe. It's useful to know the volume. But it doesn't tell you whether the pipe is corroding. It doesn't tell you whether the inside of that pipe is inflamed, irritated, or starting to rust.

[B-ROLL: Pipe analogy — clean pipe vs. corroded pipe with same water flow]

And corrosion — inflammation — is what actually causes the pipe to fail.

---

### The Inflammation Gap

Here's the thing that changed how I think about cardiac risk after 60. The cholesterol hypothesis — the idea that cholesterol in your blood automatically becomes cholesterol in your artery walls — is incomplete. It's not wrong, but it's incomplete.

What actually happens is this. LDL particles enter the space beneath your endothelial cells — that's the thin layer of cells lining your arteries. Once they're in there, they get oxidized. Your immune system recognizes oxidized LDL as a threat and sends in macrophages — those are your cleanup cells. The macrophages eat the oxidized LDL and become foam cells. Foam cells are the building blocks of plaque.

But here's the critical part. That process — LDL entering, oxidizing, immune response — only happens significantly when the endothelium is inflamed. When the endothelial lining is healthy and intact, LDL passes through without getting trapped. It's when the lining is damaged — by inflammation, by high blood pressure, by smoking, by years of metabolic stress — that LDL starts accumulating.

[PAUSE]

So the question isn't just "how much LDL do you have?" The question is "how inflamed are your arteries?" Because two people with the same LDL of 130 can have completely different levels of arterial inflammation. And the one with more inflammation is the one having the heart attack.

A 2024 study in Circulation found that — and this is striking — among people with LDL under 130, which is considered desirable, those with elevated arterial inflammatory markers had a 3.2 times higher rate of cardiovascular events over five years compared to those with low inflammatory markers.

Same cholesterol. Three times different risk. The difference was inflammation.

---

### GlycA — The Test That Sees the Inflammation

So how do we measure arterial inflammation? There are a few ways. High-sensitivity CRP, or hs-CRP, is the most commonly known. It's a general marker of systemic inflammation. It's useful but nonspecific — it goes up when you have an infection, when you have arthritis, when you have any inflammatory process anywhere in your body.

But there's a test that's more specific. It's called GlycA. And it's measured using a technique called nuclear magnetic resonance — NMR spectroscopy.

Here's what GlycA actually measures. When your body is in an inflammatory state, your liver produces acute-phase proteins — these are proteins that help coordinate the immune response. And those proteins get decorated with sugar molecules — glycans — in a specific pattern during inflammation.

GlycA measures the signal from those glycan residues on those acute-phase proteins. Essentially, it's a readout of how much acute-phase inflammatory activity your body is producing. It's like a thermometer for systemic inflammation, but one that's specifically tuned to the kind of low-grade chronic inflammation that damages arteries.

[ON-SCREEN: Simple illustration — liver producing proteins, glycan "tags" attaching, NMR measurement]

A 2024 study in the Journal of the American College of Cardiology found that GlycA levels were strongly associated with subclinical atherosclerosis progression — meaning, the rate at which plaque was building up in people who didn't yet have symptoms. And this association held even after adjusting for LDL cholesterol, blood pressure, smoking, and every other standard risk factor.

In other words, GlycA was seeing something the standard tests couldn't see.

---

### The "Arterial Age" Concept

Now, here's where this gets really practical. And here's the framework I use with my patients.

I want you to think about your arteries as having their own age. Not the age on your driver's license. The biological age of your blood vessels based on their inflammatory burden.

If you're 65 and your arteries are healthy — good endothelial function, minimal plaque, low inflammation — your arterial age might be 55. Your blood vessels are younger than you are. That's great.

But if you're 65 and you've had years of untreated high blood pressure, or you've been sedentary, or you have metabolic syndrome, or you've had chronic inflammation from any source — your arterial age might be 75 or 80. Your blood vessels are older than you are. And that gap — between your chronological age and your arterial age — is where cardiovascular risk lives.

[LEAN IN]

GlycA gives us a window into that gap. Higher GlycA means more arterial inflammation. More arterial inflammation means faster biological ageing of your blood vessels. And faster biological ageing means higher risk of the kind of plaque events that cause heart attacks and strokes.

A 2024 analysis in the European Heart Journal showed that people in the highest quartile of GlycA had arterial age estimates 10-15 years older than their chronological age, while those in the lowest quartile had arterial age estimates within two years of their actual age.

That's a huge difference. And it's invisible on a standard lipid panel.

---

### What the Numbers Mean

Alright, so let's get practical. If you were to get this test — and I'll explain how in a moment — what would the results look like?

GlycA is reported in micromoles per liter — μmol/L. The reference ranges can vary slightly between labs, but here's the general framework.

Under 380 μmol/L is considered low inflammatory burden. That's where you want to be. Your arterial age is likely close to your chronological age.

Between 380 and 450 is moderate. This is the yellow zone. It means there's some inflammatory activity happening, and it's worth investigating why.

Over 450 is high. This is the red zone. At this level, there's significant systemic inflammation that is likely affecting your vascular system. Your arterial age is probably meaningfully older than you are.

[ON-SCREEN: Simple scale graphic — green zone <380, yellow 380-450, red >450, with arterial age estimates]

Now, I want to be clear. These are population-level ranges. Your individual interpretation needs to be done with your doctor, in the context of your other risk factors. But as a general framework, this gives you something to work with.

And here's what I find clinically useful. When I see someone with a GlycA over 450 and a "normal" LDL, that person is not as safe as their standard numbers suggest. They have hidden arterial inflammation that needs to be addressed. And when I see someone with GlycA under 380 and an LDL of 160, that person might be at lower risk than their LDL number suggests, because their arteries aren't inflamed enough for that LDL to be causing significant problems.

It changes the conversation from "your cholesterol is high, we need to lower it" to "your inflammation is high, we need to find out why."

---

### How to Get This Test

So here's the practical part. How do you actually get GlycA measured?

The good news is that it's a blood test. It requires a blood draw, just like your standard labs. The less good news is that it's not part of a standard lipid panel. You have to specifically request it, or your doctor has to specifically order it.

The test is available through major reference laboratories — Labcorp, Quest Diagnostics. It's often included in what's called an NMR LipoProfile, which is an advanced lipid panel that uses the same NMR technology. Some doctors order this routinely. Many don't.

If you want to ask your doctor about it, here's what I'd suggest saying: "I'd like to know about my arterial inflammation. Can we check a GlycA level? I understand it's an NMR-based inflammatory marker that gives information beyond the standard lipid panel."

That's a reasonable, informed request. Most doctors will either order it or explain why they think it's not necessary in your specific case. Either answer is fine. The point is to have the conversation.

[PAUSE]

One thing to know — insurance coverage varies. Some plans cover it, some don't. The out-of-pocket cost is typically between $50 and $150 depending on the lab. I'm not going to tell you that's cheap. But compared to a cardiac catheterization, it's a bargain.

---

### What To Do With the Results

Let's say you get tested. Your GlycA comes back at 480. You're in the high zone. What do you do?

This is where it gets interesting, because we're not going to treat the number. We're going to look for the source.

Elevated GlycA after 60 can come from several places. And finding the source is what matters.

**Source one — metabolic.** Insulin resistance, visceral obesity, and metabolic syndrome all drive chronic inflammation. If your waist circumference is over 40 inches for men or 35 for women, that visceral fat is producing inflammatory cytokines 24/7. Addressing metabolic health — through movement, nutrition, sleep — can lower GlycA significantly.

**Source two — oral health.** And this connects directly to what I covered in my previous video. Periodontal disease, bleeding gums, chronic oral infection — these are persistent inflammatory sources that elevate systemic markers. Treating gum disease has been shown to reduce inflammatory markers.

**Source three — gut health.** Intestinal permeability, dysbiosis, chronic low-grade gut inflammation — these can elevate systemic inflammatory markers. This is an emerging area of research, but the gut-artery axis is real.

**Source four — chronic infection or autoimmune conditions.** Low-grade chronic infections — things like chronic sinusitis, low-grade urinary tract issues — or autoimmune conditions that haven't been diagnosed. These keep the immune system activated.

**Source five — lifestyle factors.** Chronic sleep deprivation, sedentary behavior, chronic psychological stress, poor diet quality. These all contribute to low-grade inflammation.

[B-ROLL: Dr. Kane in clinic setting, explaining with hand gestures]

The point is — if your GlycA is elevated, it's not a diagnosis. It's a signal. It's your body telling you there's inflammation somewhere, and your job — with your doctor — is to find it and address it.

A 2024 study in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition found that a 12-week intervention combining Mediterranean-style eating pattern with daily walking reduced GlycA levels by an average of 18% in adults over 60. That's not a drug. That's food and movement.

---

### The Reframe That Changes Everything

I want to close with something that I think is genuinely important. And it's a reframe that I wish more of my patients understood.

For decades, the conversation about heart disease has been about cholesterol. Lower your LDL. Watch your fat intake. Take your statin if your number is too high. And statins are good medications — I prescribe them regularly. But the cholesterol conversation has created a false sense of security.

Here's what I mean. If your LDL is controlled, you might think you're safe. Your doctor might tell you you're doing well. And for some people, that's true. But for a significant number of people — like my patient Robert — the cholesterol is fine and the arteries are still inflamed.

The future of cardiovascular prevention isn't just about how much cholesterol you have. It's about how inflamed your arteries are. And we can measure that now. We have the tools. We just need to use them.

[PAUSE]

I'm not saying everyone needs a GlycA test tomorrow. What I am saying is that if you're over 60, and you have any cardiac risk factors — family history, high blood pressure, metabolic syndrome, a history of smoking — you deserve to know more than just your LDL number. You deserve to know whether your arteries are inflamed. Because that inflammation is what's actually going to determine whether you have a cardiac event.

And the beautiful thing is — unlike your genetics, unlike your age — inflammation is modifiable. You can change it. You can find the source and address it. You can close the gap between your chronological age and your arterial age.

That's not scary. That's empowering.

---

### The Practical Close

So here's what I want you to take away.

Your standard lipid panel tells you how much cholesterol is in your blood. That's useful. But it doesn't tell you how inflamed your arteries are. And after 60, arterial inflammation is what actually drives cardiac events.

GlycA is a blood test that measures systemic inflammatory burden. It can reveal hidden arterial inflammation that standard tests miss. And it gives you a window into your "arterial age" — the biological age of your blood vessels.

If you want to explore this, talk to your doctor. Ask about GlycA or an NMR-based inflammatory panel. It's a reasonable, evidence-based request.

And remember — if your numbers come back elevated, it's not a sentence. It's a signal. It's information you can act on. Find the source. Address the inflammation. Close the gap.

Robert did well, by the way. After his heart attack, we dug deeper. Turned out he had significant arterial inflammation that his standard tests had never caught. We addressed it — aggressively. He's 70 now, still walking every morning, and his arterial age has come down. Not to 65 — that's not realistic. But to 72. That gap closed from 13 years to 3 years. That matters.

Take care of your arteries. They're carrying you through every day of your life.
