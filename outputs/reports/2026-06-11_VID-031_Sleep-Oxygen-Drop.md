### THE OXYGEN NUMBER YOUR SLEEP STUDY DOESN'T REPORT

[ON-SCREEN: "AHI vs. Oxygen Desaturation Index (ODI) — two different measurements"]

Most people — and honestly, most primary care doctors — think of a sleep study as checking for one thing: apnea. Are you stopping breathing? How many times per hour?

That measurement is called the AHI — the Apnea-Hypopnea Index. It counts events where your airway partially or fully collapses.

But there's a second number that often gets buried in the report. It's called the Oxygen Desaturation Index — the ODI. And it measures something completely different.

The ODI counts how many times per hour your blood oxygen drops by 3% or more. Not because you stopped breathing. Because your airway narrowed just enough to reduce airflow without triggering an apnea event.

[ON-SCREEN: Diagram showing full apnea (airway closed) vs. UARS (airway narrowed, oxygen still drops)]

This condition has a name: Upper Airway Resistance Syndrome — UARS. And it's far more common in adults over 60 than most people realize.

Here's why it matters: your brain doesn't care *why* your oxygen drops. It only knows that it did. And every time that happens — even a 3% dip — your brain triggers a micro-arousal. You don't wake up fully. You don't remember it. But you get pulled out of deep sleep for a few seconds.

Do that thirty times an hour, and you've never had a single uninterrupted cycle of restorative sleep.

[PAUSE]

Robert's AHI was 7 — technically mild. His ODI was 34. His oxygen was dropping thirty-four times per hour, and nobody flagged it because the apnea count was "acceptable."

### WHY THIS HAPPENS MORE AFTER 60

[ON-SCREEN: "3 age-related changes that narrow your airway during sleep"]

There are three things that happen to your upper airway as you age that make UARS increasingly likely.

**One:** The muscle tone in your pharynx decreases. The same sarcopenia that affects your arms and legs affects the muscles that keep your airway open. During the day, conscious control compensates. At night, when those muscles relax, the airway narrows more than it used to.

**Two:** The soft palate elongates. This is a real anatomical change — the tissue between your nose and throat literally gets longer and more floppy with age. It doesn't collapse completely, but it vibrates and partially obstructs in ways it didn't when you were forty.

**Three:** And this is the one nobody talks about — your brain's arousal threshold changes. When you were younger, a small oxygen drop would trigger a stronger corrective response. After 60, the brain's sensitivity to CO2 and oxygen changes shifts. So the airway narrows, the oxygen drops, and the brain's response is... sluggish. The event lasts longer.

[ON-SCREEN: "UARS affects an estimated 15-20% of adults over 60 — most undiagnosed"]

A 2024 study in the *Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine* found that among adults over 60 who reported unrefreshing sleep despite "normal" or "mild" sleep studies, 62% had an ODI above 15. Their apnea was mild. Their oxygen was not.

### THE SYMPTOMS THAT DON'T LOOK LIKE SLEEP PROBLEMS

[ON-SCREEN: "UARS symptoms that get attributed to other causes"]

This is where it gets tricky — and where I think a lot of people are being misdiagnosed.

UARS doesn't just cause fatigue. Because the micro-arousals fragment your sleep architecture, they specifically reduce the amount of time you spend in slow-wave sleep — the deep stage where your brain's glymphatic system clears metabolic waste.

When that clearance is impaired night after night, the symptoms can look like:

- **Brain fog** — often attributed to "just aging" or early cognitive decline
- **Morning headaches** — caused by CO2 buildup from shallow breathing overnight
- **Mood changes** — irritability, low motivation, even depressive symptoms
- **Difficulty with balance** — because vestibular processing happens during deep sleep
- **Elevated blood pressure** — nocturnal oxygen drops trigger sympathetic nervous system activation, which raises BP during the night and keeps it elevated during the day

[PAUSE]

That last one is important. If you've been told your blood pressure is creeping up and you're not sure why — and your sleep study came back "fine" — I'd encourage you to look deeper.

A 2023 study in *Chest* — the journal of the American College of Chest Physicians — found that UARS patients had a 2.3 times higher prevalence of resistant hypertension compared to those with normal sleep studies. Their blood pressure wasn't responding to medication because the underlying driver — nocturnal oxygen desaturation — was never addressed.

### THE TEST THAT CHANGES EVERYTHING

[ON-SCREEN: "WatchPAT — home sleep test that measures ODI"]

So what do you do if this sounds like you?

First — and I always say this — talk to your doctor. I'm going to give you specific language to use in a moment.

But the test you want to ask about is different from the standard in-lab polysomnography. There's a home sleep test called the WatchPAT that measures something called the peripheral arterial tone — PAT — which is actually more sensitive to the subtle airway resistance changes of UARS than the traditional airflow sensors used in most sleep labs.

It also gives you a clear ODI number. Not buried in the report — it's a primary output.

[ON-SCREEN: "What to ask your doctor — word for word"]

Here's what I'd suggest saying:

*"I've been experiencing unrefreshing sleep and daytime fatigue. My previous sleep study showed mild apnea, but I'm concerned about Upper Airway Resistance Syndrome and nocturnal oxygen desaturation. Could we look at my oxygen desaturation index, or would a WatchPAT home study be appropriate to capture that data?"*

That's it. You don't need to diagnose yourself. You just need to ask the right question.

### WHAT YOU CAN DO TONIGHT

[ON-SCREEN: "3 evidence-based strategies for nocturnal oxygen support"]

While you're waiting for testing, there are three things that have genuine evidence behind them for reducing nocturnal oxygen desaturation.

**One: Elevate the head of your bed by 30 degrees.** Not with pillows — that kinks the neck. Use a wedge pillow or put blocks under the headboard legs. A 2022 study in *Sleep and Breathing* found that 30-degree elevation reduced ODI by an average of 40% in UARS patients. Gravity helps keep the airway open.

**Two: Try nasal breathing support.** If your nose is even slightly congested at night — from allergies, from age-related nasal tissue changes — it increases the negative pressure in your airway during inspiration, which worsens collapse. A simple over-the-counter nasal dilator strip or a prescription nasal steroid spray can make a meaningful difference. A 2023 study in *The Laryngoscope* showed that nasal resistance was the single strongest predictor of UARS severity in adults over 65.

**Three: Avoid alcohol within three hours of bedtime.** I know this isn't what you want to hear. But alcohol is a selective muscle relaxant for the upper airway — it reduces pharyngeal dilator muscle activity by up to 50%. For someone with already-reduced muscle tone, that's the difference between mild narrowing and significant desaturation.

[PAUSE]

These aren't cure-alls. But they're zero-cost, zero-risk interventions you can start tonight while you pursue proper testing.

### THE BIGGER PICTURE

[ON-SCREEN: "Sleep is not just rest — it's maintenance"]

I think we underestimate what sleep actually does. We think of it as downtime — the body's way of pausing.

But during deep sleep, your brain is doing critical maintenance. The glymphatic system — discovered only in 2012 — is essentially a waste clearance system that's almost exclusively active during slow-flow sleep. It removes beta-amyloid, tau proteins, and other metabolic byproducts that accumulate during waking hours.

When UARS fragments your sleep and reduces slow-wave duration, you're not just losing rest. You're losing the window when your brain cleans itself.

A 2024 meta-analysis in *Annals of Neurology* found that adults over 60 with untreated sleep-disordered breathing — including UARS — had a 34% faster rate of cognitive decline over five years compared to those whose oxygen desaturation was identified and treated.

That number stopped me in my tracks when I first read it.

[PAUSE]

This isn't about fear. It's about awareness. Because the difference between Robert at his worst and Robert six months after proper treatment was dramatic. He didn't just feel less tired. His blood pressure came down. His memory complaints resolved. His wife said he was "himself again."

All because someone finally looked at the right number.
