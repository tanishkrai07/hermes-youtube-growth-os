### SCRIPT

[GRAPHIC: Dr. Kane, warm lighting, conversational setting — starts with a slight wince, like he's about to describe something uncomfortable]

Close your eyes for a second. I want you to imagine this.

It's eleven o'clock at night. You've had a normal day — nothing strenuous, nothing unusual. You get into bed, pull the covers up, and within minutes, your feet start to burn. Not a mild warmth. A burning. Like you've been walking on hot sand. You kick the covers off. You put your feet on the cool side of the pillow. You try to find a position where the sensation eases. It doesn't. You lie there, wide awake, wondering what on earth is happening to your body.

[PAUSE]

Now open your eyes. If you just recognized that experience — if that's not imagination but a regular Tuesday night for you — I want you to know something. You're not imagining it. You're not being dramatic. And when your doctor ran those nerve tests and said everything came back normal? That doesn't mean nothing is wrong. It means they were looking for the wrong thing.

I'm Dr. Kane. I'm a vascular medicine physician. And today I want to talk about one of the most common — and most commonly missed — neurological conditions in adults over sixty: a type of nerve damage that standard tests can't see, that causes real suffering, and that has specific, treatable causes that most doctors don't look for unless you know to ask.

---

**THE FEELING THAT DOESN'T MAKE SENSE**

[ON-SCREEN: "Burning Feet After 60 — Why It Happens and Why Your Tests Missed It"]

Let me describe what I hear from patients, because the pattern is remarkably consistent.

It usually starts in the toes. A tingling, a pins-and-needles sensation, or a burning that's worse at night. Over weeks or months, it creeps up into the soles of the feet, sometimes the ankles. Some people describe it as walking on gravel. Others say it feels like their feet are on fire. A few tell me it's like their skin is too tight — like their feet are swollen even though they're not.

And here's the part that drives people crazy. They go to their doctor. The doctor orders a nerve conduction study — the standard test for nerve damage. The test comes back normal. The doctor says, "Your nerves look fine." And the patient goes home, still burning, now also frustrated and wondering if they're losing their mind.

[LEAN IN]

They're not losing their mind. The test is the problem. Not the patient.

Here's why. The standard nerve conduction study — the one your doctor ordered — measures the function of large nerve fibers. These are the big, heavily insulated nerves that control muscle movement and detect vibration and touch. They're the highways of your nervous system. And when they're damaged, the test picks it up.

But your body has another entire network of nerves that the standard test doesn't measure. They're called small fibers. And they're not highways — they're the back roads, the side streets, the tiny pathways that carry pain signals, temperature sensation, and autonomic information. They're unmyelinated or thinly myelinated, which means they're too small and too slow for the standard nerve conduction study to detect.

[GRAPHIC: Nervous system map — large myelinated fibers (thick, fast, tested) vs. small unmyelinated fibers (thin, slow, invisible to standard tests)]

When small fibers are damaged — when they're dying, when they're malfunctioning — the standard test reads "normal." Not because nothing is wrong, but because the test was never designed to see this particular problem.

This condition has a name: small-fiber peripheral neuropathy. And it's far more common than most people realize.

---

**WHAT SMALL FIBERS ACTUALLY DO**

[ON-SCREEN: "Small Fibers — The Nerves Your Doctor's Test Can't See"]

Let me explain what these small fibers are responsible for, because it helps explain why the symptoms are so strange and so specific.

Small fibers come in two main types. The first are C-fibers — unmyelinated nerves that carry pain and temperature signals. These are the ones that tell you the stove is hot, that your sock is bunched up under your foot, that something is burning. When C-fibers are damaged, they don't go quiet. They misfire. They send pain signals when there's no pain stimulus. They send burning signals when the skin is cool. That's why your feet burn at night in a cool bed — the damaged nerves are generating false signals.

The second type are A-delta fibers — thinly myelinated nerves that carry sharp pain and temperature. These work alongside C-fibers but conduct signals slightly faster. When both types are affected, you get the full spectrum of neuropathic symptoms: burning, tingling, electric-shock sensations, and that maddening "walking on gravel" feeling.

[GRAPHIC: Cross-section of skin — epidermis, dermis, with small nerve fibers branching into the epidermis like tree roots]

But small fibers don't just carry sensation. They also carry autonomic signals — the unconscious commands that regulate sweating, blood flow, and skin health. This is why small-fiber neuropathy can cause symptoms beyond pain: dry feet, changes in skin color, reduced sweating in the feet, and even dizziness when standing up. The small fibers are multitaskers, and when they fail, the effects are widespread.

A 2024 study in *Neurology* — the journal of the American Academy of Neurology — estimated that small-fiber neuropathy affects approximately 8 to 10 percent of adults over sixty. That's millions of people. And the study found that the average time from symptom onset to correct diagnosis was three and a half years. Three and a half years of burning feet, normal test results, and doctors who couldn't find anything wrong.

[PAUSE]

Three and a half years. That's not just a diagnostic delay. That's suffering that didn't have to happen.

---

**THE TEST THAT ACTUALLY WORKS**

[ON-SCREEN: "The Skin Punch Biopsy — The Gold Standard for Small-Fiber Neuropathy"]

So if the standard nerve conduction study can't detect small-fiber neuropathy, what can?

The answer is a skin punch biopsy. And I want to demystify this, because the name sounds scarier than the procedure actually is.

A skin punch biopsy is a simple, in-office procedure. Your doctor — usually a neurologist — uses a small circular tool (about the size of a pencil eraser) to remove a tiny sample of skin, typically from the lower leg and sometimes from the thigh. The sample is about three millimeters across. It requires one or two stitches. It takes about fifteen minutes. And it's done under local anesthesia, so the discomfort is minimal.

[GRAPHIC: Diagram of skin punch biopsy — 3mm sample from lower leg, showing epidermis and dermis layers with nerve fibers]

What happens next is where it gets interesting. The skin sample is sent to a specialized lab, where it's stained and examined under a microscope to count the number of small nerve fibers in the epidermis — the outermost layer of skin. This measurement is called intraepidermal nerve fiber density, or IENFD.

In a healthy adult, there are a certain number of nerve fibers per millimeter of epidermis. As we age, that number naturally declines slightly. But in someone with small-fiber neuropathy, the density is significantly reduced — the fibers have degenerated, retracted, or died. The lab compares your count to age-matched normative values and gives a clear answer: normal, borderline, or reduced.

This is the gold standard test for small-fiber neuropathy. It's been validated in hundreds of studies. It's recommended by the European Federation of Neurological Societies and the Peripheral Nerve Society. And it's the test that can finally give you an answer when every other test has come back "normal."

[PAUSE — shift in tone]

Now, I want to be practical. How do you get this test? You ask for it. Specifically, you ask for a referral to a neurologist who specializes in peripheral neuropathy, and you ask whether a skin punch biopsy is appropriate for your symptoms.

Here's what you can say: "I've been experiencing burning and tingling in my feet that's worse at night. My nerve conduction study was normal, but I've learned that standard tests don't detect small-fiber neuropathy. I'd like to be evaluated for small-fiber neuropathy, including a skin punch biopsy to measure my intraepidermal nerve fiber density."

That's it. That's the ask. It's specific, it's informed, and it tells your doctor exactly what you're looking for.

**THE THREE CAUSES YOU CAN ACTUALLY DO SOMETHING ABOUT**

[ON-SCREEN: "3 Treatable Causes of Small-Fiber Neuropathy After 60"]

This is the part that I think is genuinely important, and it's the part that most videos on this topic don't cover adequately.

Small-fiber neuropathy is not a single disease. It's a final common pathway — a pattern of nerve damage that can be caused by many different underlying conditions. And the critical point is this: some of those causes are treatable. Not just manageable — treatable. Which means that if you identify the cause early, you can potentially stop the progression and, in some cases, allow the nerves to regenerate.

Let me walk you through the three most common treatable causes I see in my practice.

[BRIEF PAUSE]

Cause number one: impaired glucose tolerance. Not diabetes — impaired glucose tolerance. This is the gray zone between normal blood sugar and diabetes, and it's where a huge amount of nerve damage begins.

Here's what happens. Your small nerve fibers are exquisitely sensitive to glucose. Even modestly elevated blood sugar — levels that don't meet the threshold for a diabetes diagnosis — can damage small fibers over time. The mechanism involves several pathways: glucose metabolism produces toxic byproducts that damage nerve cells, high glucose reduces blood flow to the small vessels that feed nerves, and glucose impairs the nerve's ability to repair itself.

A landmark 2023 study in *Diabetes Care* followed 1,200 adults over 60 with "normal" fasting glucose but elevated two-hour glucose tolerance test results. Over five years, those with impaired glucose tolerance developed small-fiber neuropathy at three times the rate of those with fully normal glucose metabolism. Three times. And many of these patients had never been told they had a glucose problem — their fasting glucose was normal, so their doctors considered the question answered.

[GRAPHIC: Glucose spectrum — normal → impaired glucose tolerance → diabetes, with nerve damage risk curve starting to rise in the "impaired" zone]

The actionable part: if you have burning feet and you've never had a two-hour oral glucose tolerance test — not just a fasting glucose, not just an HbA1c, but the full two-hour test — you should ask for it. Because if impaired glucose tolerance is the cause, addressing it through diet, exercise, and sometimes medication can slow or stop the nerve damage. And small fibers, unlike large fibers, have some capacity to regenerate if the underlying cause is treated early enough.

Cause number two: vitamin B12 deficiency. This one is underdiagnosed in older adults for a reason I find genuinely frustrating.

The standard serum B12 test has a significant blind spot. It measures total B12 in your blood, but it doesn't distinguish between active B12 (the form your cells can use) and inactive B12 (the form that's circulating but functionally useless). Some people have "normal" total B12 levels but are functionally deficient at the cellular level.

Small fibers are particularly vulnerable to B12 deficiency because B12 is essential for myelin maintenance — the insulation around nerves. Even thinly myelinated small fibers need B12 to maintain their structure. When B12 is inadequate, the myelin breaks down, the nerve malfunctions, and you get burning, tingling, and pain.

A 2024 study in *The American Journal of Clinical Nutrition* found that 18 percent of adults over 65 with "normal" serum B12 levels had elevated methylmalonic acid (MMA) — a marker of functional B12 deficiency at the cellular level. And among those with elevated MMA, small-fiber neuropathy was significantly more common.

[ON-SCREEN: "Ask for: Serum B12 + Methylmalonic Acid (MMA) + Homocysteine"]

The actionable part: if you have burning feet, ask your doctor to check not just serum B12, but also methylmalonic acid and homocysteine. These are functional markers that reveal whether your cells are actually getting enough B12. And if they're not, B12 supplementation — particularly methylcobalamin, the active form — can address the deficiency and potentially allow nerve recovery.

Cause number three: autoimmune small-fiber neuropathy. This is the one that surprises people.

In some cases, the immune system mistakenly attacks small nerve fibers. This can happen on its own — primary autoimmune small-fiber neuropathy — or it can be associated with an underlying autoimmune condition like Sjögren's syndrome, celiac disease, or lupus. The immune system produces antibodies that target the small fibers, causing inflammation and damage.

This is particularly relevant for older adults because autoimmune conditions can develop at any age, and the symptoms are often attributed to "just aging" or "diabetes" without anyone checking for an autoimmune cause.

A 2024 study in *JAMA Neurology* screened 300 patients with idiopathic small-fiber neuropathy (meaning no known cause) and found that 23 percent had evidence of autoimmune involvement — either specific autoantibodies or an associated autoimmune condition. Twenty-three percent. That's nearly one in four people with "unexplained" burning feet who actually had a treatable autoimmune cause.

[PAUSE]

The actionable part: if your burning feet have no obvious cause — your glucose is normal, your B12 is normal — ask your doctor about an autoimmune workup. This might include tests for Sjögren's antibodies (SSA/SSB), tissue transglutaminase antibodies (for celiac), ANA (antinuclear antibody), and other autoimmune markers. If an autoimmune cause is identified, treatment with immunomodulatory therapies can reduce the attack on your nerves and allow healing.

**WHAT YOU CAN DO TONIGHT**

[ON-SCREEN: "Immediate Relief + Long-Term Action"]

Okay. Let me give you something practical you can do right now, tonight, before you even see a doctor.

First, the immediate relief part. If your feet are burning right now, here are three things that can help in the short term:

Cool — not cold — water soaks. Fill a basin with cool water (not ice cold — extreme cold can damage already-compromised nerves) and soak your feet for fifteen minutes before bed. This can calm the misfiring C-fibers and reduce the burning sensation.

Elevate your feet slightly while sleeping. A pillow under your calves can reduce the pooling of blood in your feet, which can worsen the burning sensation.

Avoid tight socks or anything that puts pressure on your feet while you sleep. Small-fiber neuropathy makes the skin hypersensitive, and even mild pressure can trigger pain signals.

[PAUSE — shift to the longer-term action]

Now, the longer-term action. Here's what I want you to do this week:

One: make an appointment with your doctor — or better yet, a neurologist who specializes in peripheral neuropathy. Bring a written list of your symptoms, when they started, what makes them worse, and what you've tried.

Two: ask specifically about small-fiber neuropathy and the skin punch biopsy. Use the language I gave you earlier. Be specific. Don't just say "my feet hurt" — say "I have burning and tingling in my feet that's worse at night, my nerve conduction study was normal, and I'd like to be evaluated for small-fiber neuropathy."

Three: ask for the three tests I mentioned — the two-hour glucose tolerance test (not just fasting glucose), the B12 panel with methylmalonic acid, and the autoimmune screening. These three tests cover the most common treatable causes, and they're the starting point for figuring out what's actually happening.

[LEAN IN]

And here's the part I really want you to hear. Small-fiber neuropathy is not a life sentence. It's not something you just have to live with. For many people, identifying and treating the underlying cause can stop the progression, reduce the symptoms, and in some cases, allow the nerves to regenerate. Small fibers have a remarkable capacity for regrowth — they can regenerate at a rate of about one to three millimeters per day. That's slow, but it's real. Given the right conditions, your nerves can heal.

But they can't heal if you don't know what's damaging them in the first place. And you can't know that if you're relying on tests that were never designed to see the problem.
