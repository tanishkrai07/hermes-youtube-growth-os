**[SECTION 1: WHY YOUR BLOOD TYPE MATTERS MORE AFTER 60]**

[ON-SCREEN: Section title card — "Why Blood Type Matters More After 60"]

Let me start with a fact that surprises almost every patient I have ever treated.

Your blood type — A, B, AB, or O — is determined by specific antigens on the surface of your red blood cells. These antigens are not just markers for transfusions. They are active biological signals that interact with your immune system, your blood vessels, your clotting system, and even your gut bacteria.

[PAUSE]

For the first 50 or so years of your life, your body compensates. Your organs are resilient. Your blood vessels are flexible. Your clotting system is balanced.

But after 60?

[SLOW DOWN]

Everything changes.

Your blood vessels begin to stiffen. Your clotting factors shift. Your immune system becomes less precise. And suddenly — the antigens on your red blood cells start sending different signals. Signals that can either protect you… or put you at risk.

[ON-SCREEN: Simple animation — blood vessel cross-section showing antigen interaction with vessel wall]

A landmark study published in the journal *Neurology* in 2022 — analyzing data from over 65,000 participants — found that Blood Type A was associated with a 16% higher risk of cognitive decline after age 65, while Blood Type O showed a measurable protective effect on brain health.

[PAUSE]

Another study, published in *Thrombosis Research*, confirmed that individuals with Blood Type O have approximately 25% lower levels of von Willebrand factor — a key protein involved in blood clotting. This means Type O individuals have a naturally lower risk of dangerous blood clots, deep vein thrombosis, and pulmonary embolism.

[LEAN IN]

Let me say that again, because it is important.

If you are Blood Type O, your body is naturally less likely to form the kinds of blood clots that cause strokes and heart attacks. That is a genuine, measurable, biological advantage.

[PAUSE]

But — and this is the part nobody talks about — that same advantage comes with a trade-off. A dangerous weakness that most Type O seniors never see coming.

I will reveal that weakness in a moment. But first, let me walk you through all four blood types — because even if you are Type O, understanding the full picture will help you protect the people you love.

**[SECTION 2: BLOOD TYPE O — THE HIDDEN ADVANTAGE]**

[ON-SCREEN: "TYPE O: The Natural Protector"]

If you are Blood Type O, here is what is working in your favor.

First: lower clotting risk. As I mentioned, your von Willebrand factor levels are naturally lower. This means your blood flows more freely. You are less likely to develop the dangerous clots that cause strokes, heart attacks, and pulmonary embolisms. A 2015 meta-analysis in the *Journal of Thrombosis and Haemostasis* confirmed this across multiple populations.

[PAUSE]

Second: lower cardiovascular disease risk. Research published in *Arteriosclerosis, Thrombosis, and Vascular Biology* — one of the most respected journals in heart health — found that Type O individuals had a significantly lower risk of coronary heart disease compared to Type A and Type B.

Third: better response to certain infections. Type O blood has been associated with lower susceptibility to severe malaria and, according to emerging research from the COVID-19 pandemic, potentially lower risk of severe outcomes from certain respiratory infections.

[LEAN IN]

And here is one more advantage that most people do not know.

Type O individuals tend to have a more robust gut microbiome. Research from the *Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health* has shown that blood type influences the composition of gut bacteria — and Type O is associated with higher levels of certain beneficial bacteria that support immune function and reduce inflammation.

[SLOW DOWN]

So if you are Type O, you have been given a head start. A biological advantage that protects your heart, your brain, and your immune system.

But — and I need you to hear this clearly — that advantage has a shadow.

**[SECTION 3: THE ONE DANGEROUS WEAKNESS TYPE O SENIORS MUST WATCH FOR]**

[ON-SCREEN: "TYPE O: The Hidden Danger" — red warning icon]

[PAUSE]

Here is what most doctors will not tell you.

Because Type O blood has lower levels of von Willebrand factor — the same protein that protects you from clots — it also means your blood does not clot as efficiently when you need it to.

[LEAN IN]

In plain language: if you are Type O and you get a cut, a wound, or undergo surgery, you are at higher risk for excessive bleeding.

This is not a theoretical concern. A 2017 study in *The New England Journal of Medicine* — one of the most prestigious medical journals in the world — found that Type O patients who experienced major trauma had a significantly higher mortality rate, partly due to increased bleeding complications.

[PAUSE]

And here is what makes this especially dangerous after 60.

As we age, our skin becomes thinner. Our blood vessels become more fragile. Falls become more common. And if you are on any kind of blood-thinning medication — even a daily aspirin — the combination of Type O blood and reduced clotting factors can turn a minor fall into a medical emergency.

[SLOW DOWN]

This is the dangerous weakness. Not clots. Bleeding.

And it is the reason why every Type O senior over 60 needs to know three things:

One: Tell your doctor your blood type — specifically — before any procedure, no matter how minor.

Two: If you are on blood thinners, ask your doctor whether your dosage accounts for your blood type. The standard protocol does not always consider this.

Three: Consider keeping a medical alert card in your wallet that states your blood type. In an emergency, this information could save your life.

[ON-SCREEN: "TYPE O ACTION STEPS: 1. Tell your doctor. 2. Review blood thinners. 3. Carry a medical alert card."]

**[SECTION 4: BLOOD TYPE A — YOUR HEART'S SILENT ENEMY AFTER 60]**

[ON-SCREEN: "TYPE A: The Heart Risk"]

Now, if you are Blood Type A, I need you to pay close attention.

Type A is the second most common blood type. And while it does not carry the bleeding risk of Type O, it comes with its own set of challenges — particularly for your heart.

Research published in the *British Medical Journal* has shown that Type A individuals have approximately 5% higher total cholesterol levels and significantly higher levels of LDL — the "bad" cholesterol — compared to Type O.

[PAUSE]

Why? Because the A antigen on your red blood cells interacts with your liver's cholesterol processing in a way that promotes higher circulating LDL. Over decades, this adds up.

After 60, when your arteries are already beginning to stiffen, this elevated cholesterol accelerates the buildup of plaque. And plaque is the silent killer behind most heart attacks.

[LEAN IN]

A massive study from the *Harvard Nurses' Health Study* — following over 80,000 participants for more than 20 years — found that Type A individuals had a 24% higher risk of developing coronary heart disease compared to Type O.

That is not a small number.

If you are Type A, here is what I want you to do: at your next checkup, ask your doctor for a full lipid panel — not just total cholesterol, but LDL, HDL, triglycerides, and lipoprotein(a). And ask specifically whether your blood type should influence how aggressively you manage your cholesterol.

[ON-SCREEN: "TYPE A ACTION: Full lipid panel + ask about blood type and cholesterol management"]

---

**[SECTION 5: BLOOD TYPE B — THE STROKE CONNECTION NOBODY TALKS ABOUT]**

[ON-SCREEN: "TYPE B: The Stroke Connection"]

Blood Type B is less common in Western populations but carries its own unique risks.

Research from the *American Heart Association's Stroke Journal* has shown that Type B individuals have a higher baseline level of certain inflammatory markers — particularly C-reactive protein and interleukin-6 — compared to Type O.

[PAUSE]

Chronic, low-grade inflammation is one of the most underappreciated drivers of stroke. It damages the lining of your blood vessels, makes your blood more prone to clotting, and accelerates the aging of your brain's delicate vascular network.

After 60, when your brain's blood supply is already under pressure from years of high blood pressure, diabetes, or simply aging, this inflammatory burden can be the tipping point.

[LEAN IN]

A 2020 study in *Neurology* found that Type B individuals over 65 had a measurably higher risk of ischemic stroke — the kind caused by a blood clot blocking blood flow to the brain — compared to Type O.

If you are Type B, here is your action step: ask your doctor to test your C-reactive protein and homocysteine levels. These are simple blood tests that reveal your inflammatory burden. And if they are elevated, there are specific dietary and lifestyle changes — which I will cover in my next video — that can bring them down.

[ON-SCREEN: "TYPE B ACTION: Test CRP + homocysteine. Watch for next video on inflammation reduction."]

**[SECTION 6: BLOOD TYPE AB — TWO RISKS, ONE SIMPLE DAILY SHIELD]**

[ON-SCREEN: "TYPE AB: Two Risks, One Shield"]

Blood Type AB is the rarest — only about 4% of the population. And unfortunately, it carries the highest cardiovascular risk of all four types.

Why? Because Type AB inherits the antigens of both Type A and Type B. And with those antigens come the risks of both.

[PAUSE]

A landmark study published in *Circulation* — the flagship journal of the American Heart Association — found that Type AB individuals had a 23% higher risk of heart disease and a 27% higher risk of stroke compared to Type O.

[SLOW DOWN]

That is the highest risk of any blood type.

But here is the good news — and this is important.

Research has shown that Type AB individuals also respond the most dramatically to lifestyle interventions. Because your risk is driven by the combined inflammatory and clotting factors of both A and B antigens, even small changes in diet, movement, and stress management can produce outsized results.

[LEAN IN]

The one simple daily shield for Type AB? A 20-minute walk after your largest meal.

A study in the *Journal of the American Geriatrics Society* found that a post-meal walk reduced blood sugar spikes by 30% and inflammatory markers by 25% in older adults. For Type AB seniors, this single habit can address both the clotting and inflammatory risks simultaneously.

[ON-SCREEN: "TYPE AB ACTION: 20-minute walk after your largest meal — every day"]
