### SCRIPT

[GRAPHIC: Dr. Kane in frame, warm lighting, conversational setting — no white coat]

I want to talk about something that terrifies people.

Not in a vague, abstract way. In a deeply personal, private way. The kind of thing people notice alone — at the kitchen table trying to sign a check, or holding a cup of coffee that's suddenly not staying still — and immediately think one word.

Parkinson's.

[PAUSE — let that word hang in the air]

I'm Dr. Kane. I'm a vascular medicine physician. And I've had patients come to me — more than I can count — who've been living with a hand tremor for weeks or months, quietly terrified, too afraid to look it up, too afraid to say it out loud, convinced that their body is telling them something devastating.

And here's what I want to say to you, right at the start of this video, before we get into any of the science.

Most hand shaking is not Parkinson's.

[PAUSE]

That doesn't mean it's nothing. It doesn't mean you should ignore it. But it means the fear you're carrying — that specific, named fear — is statistically unlikely to be the answer. And there are real, identifiable, sometimes treatable reasons that hands start shaking after sixty. And that's what I want to walk you through today.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Most hand tremors after 60 are NOT Parkinson's — here's what's actually happening"]

Let me give you the landscape first. Because the word "tremor" is actually a category, not a diagnosis. It's like saying "pain" — pain can mean a hundred different things depending on where it is, when it happens, what it feels like, and what makes it better or worse. Tremor works the same way.

There are several distinct types of tremor. And the type you have — the specific pattern of when it happens, how it looks, what makes it worse — tells you what's causing it. So let me walk you through the main categories, because understanding the taxonomy is how you stop worrying about the wrong thing and start paying attention to the right thing.

[GRAPHIC: Clean animated taxonomy tree — "Tremor" branching into types]

Type one: resting tremor. This is the one people associate with Parkinson's, and for good reason — it's the most common tremor in Parkinson's disease. A resting tremor happens when your muscles are relaxed and supported. Your hand is resting on your lap, or hanging at your side, and it shakes. The classic description is "pill-rolling" — the thumb and forefinger move in a circular motion like you're rolling a small pill between them. It's typically four to six cycles per second. And it usually starts on one side of the body before potentially spreading.

[PAUSE]

If you have a tremor that happens when your hand is completely at rest — not when you're using it, not when you're holding something, but when it's just sitting there — that's the type that warrants a neurological evaluation. I'm not going to sugarcoat that. A resting tremor, especially if it's one-sided, especially if it's accompanied by stiffness or slowness of movement, should be evaluated by a neurologist.

But here's the important context. Even among people with a resting tremor, not all of them have Parkinson's. There are other causes — certain medications, other neurological conditions. And the majority of hand tremors I see in my practice are NOT resting tremors.

[B-ROLL: Animation showing hand at rest with pill-rolling tremor, then hand reaching for object with action tremor]

Type two: action tremor — and this is the big category that most people actually have. An action tremor happens when you're using your muscles. Reaching for something. Holding a posture. Writing. Lifting a cup. Action tremor has two sub-types, and the distinction matters.

The first sub-type is postural tremor — it happens when you hold a position against gravity. Arms outstretched, holding a glass, holding a book. The second is kinetic tremor — it happens during voluntary movement. Reaching, writing, touching your finger to your nose.

The most common cause of action tremor in adults over sixty is something called essential tremor. And I need you to hear this clearly: essential tremor is NOT Parkinson's disease. It's a completely different condition. It's actually more common than Parkinson's — roughly ten times more common. An estimated ten million Americans have essential tremor. It tends to run in families, it often starts in middle age or later, and it primarily affects the hands, head, and voice.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Essential Tremor — 10 million Americans | NOT Parkinson's | Often hereditary | Hands, head, voice"]

Essential tremor is what's called an "action tremor" — it gets worse when you use your hands and improves when they're at rest. It can be embarrassing. It can make signing documents, eating in public, or threading a needle difficult. But it is not neurodegenerative in the way Parkinson's is. It doesn't shorten your life. And there are treatments — medications like propranolol and primidone that can reduce the tremor significantly.

[PAUSE — transition]

Now, type three. And this is the one I spend the most time on with my patients, because it's the one nobody expects.

Medication-induced tremor.

[GRAPHIC: Pill bottle with tremor waves emanating from it]

A remarkable number of commonly prescribed medications can cause or worsen tremor. And because medication-induced tremor is an action tremor — it looks like essential tremor — it's frequently misdiagnosed as essential tremor. The patient is told "you have essential tremor" and started on treatment, when the actual cause is a medication they're already taking.

Let me give you the main culprits.

Valproic acid — an anti-seizure medication also used for mood stabilization and migraine prevention — causes tremor in roughly sixteen to twenty percent of people who take it. That's not rare. That's common.

Lithium — used for bipolar disorder — causes tremor in up to sixty percent of patients at therapeutic doses. Sixty percent. It's one of the most common side effects of the drug.

Bronchodilators — albuterol, salmeterol, medications people with asthma or COPD use every day — are well-known tremor inducers. They stimulate beta-2 receptors, which directly increases the tremor frequency in skeletal muscle.

[B-ROLL: Animation — medication molecules binding to receptors, triggering muscle fiber tremor]

Certain antidepressants — particularly SSRIs like fluoxetine and sertraline — can cause or worsen tremor in some patients. A 2024 review in the Journal of Clinical Psychopharmacology found that SSRI-associated tremor occurred in approximately fifteen to twenty percent of users, though it was often mild enough that patients didn't report it.

And here's one that surprises people: blood pressure medications. Specifically, calcium channel blockers like amlodipine and nifedipine. They can cause a fine action tremor that's easily mistaken for essential tremor.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Medication-Induced Tremor — Common Culprits: Valproic acid (16-20%), Lithium (up to 60%), Bronchodilators, SSRIs (15-20%), Calcium channel blockers"]

Now, here's why this matters so much in the over-sixty population. If you're over sixty, you're more likely to be on one or more of these medications. And if you develop a tremor, the default assumption — even among some doctors — might be "essential tremor" or "early Parkinson's." The medication connection gets overlooked because the tremor might have started gradually, or because the medication was started months or years before the tremor appeared, and nobody connected the two.

[PAUSE — lean toward camera]

I had a patient — I'll call him David — sixty-seven years old, referred to me because his primary care doctor was concerned about early Parkinson's. David had a fine tremor in both hands, worse when he was doing things, better at rest. His doctor had already started the referral process to a neurologist.

I sat down with David and went through his medication list. He was on five medications. One of them was amlodipine for blood pressure — had been on it for three years. But about eight months ago, his dose had been increased from five to ten milligrams. Within a few weeks of that dose increase, his wife noticed his hands were shaking.

Now, I'm not a neurologist, and I didn't make a diagnosis. But I wrote a note to his doctor suggesting that the amlodipine dose increase might be contributing to the tremor and asking whether a dose reduction or alternative medication could be considered. His doctor agreed to try reducing the dose back to five. Within six weeks, David's tremor had decreased by about seventy percent. Not gone — he still had a very mild tremor that might have been age-related essential tremor underneath. But the dramatic improvement told us the medication was the primary driver.

[PAUSE — let that land]

David spent two months terrified he had Parkinson's. Two months of anxiety, of googling symptoms, of lying awake at night. And the answer was in his medication list the whole time.

I'm not saying every tremor is medication-induced. I'm not saying you should stop any medication on your own. But I am saying that if you develop a new tremor — or if an existing tremor gets worse — the medication list is the first place to look. Before the neurological referral. Before the MRI. Before the fear spiral. Look at the medications.

[GRAPHIC: Checklist appearing on screen]

Let me give you one more tremor type, because it's important and it's different from everything I've described so far.

Physiologic tremor. Every human being has a tremor. Right now, your hands are shaking — you just can't see it. It's there. It's a normal function of the motor system, and it's so fine that it's invisible to the naked eye. But certain things can amplify it until it becomes visible.

Caffeine. Stress. Anxiety. Low blood sugar. Thyroid overactivity. Sleep deprivation. Alcohol withdrawal. These are all amplifiers. They take your normal, invisible physiologic tremor and turn up the volume until you can see it.

This is actually one of the most common reasons I see patients for tremor in my practice. They've had a stressful week, they're sleeping poorly, they've been drinking more coffee than usual, and suddenly their hands are shaking. They panic. They google. They convince themselves something is terribly wrong. And when we address the amplifiers — better sleep, less caffeine, stress management — the tremor resolves.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Physiologic Tremor — Everyone has it | Amplified by: caffeine, stress, anxiety, low blood sugar, thyroid, poor sleep, alcohol withdrawal"]

A 2025 study in the journal Tremor and Other Hyperkinetic Movements looked at 340 patients referred to a movement disorder clinic for evaluation of tremor. And here's what I found remarkable: twenty-two percent of those referred were ultimately diagnosed with "exaggerated physiologic tremor" — meaning their tremor was a normal tremor that had been amplified by lifestyle factors or medications, not a neurological disease. Twenty-two percent. That's more than one in five people who were worried about Parkinson's or essential tremor who actually had an amplified normal tremor.

[PAUSE — transition to practical framework]

Alright. So let me give you a practical framework for what to do if you notice a tremor. Because I don't want to leave you with just information — I want to leave you with a way to think about this.

Step one: characterize the tremor. When does it happen? At rest or during action? One hand or both? Fine or coarse? Constant or intermittent? Does caffeine make it worse? Does alcohol make it better — essential tremor characteristically improves temporarily with alcohol, which is actually a useful diagnostic clue. Write this down. Be specific. This information is gold for your doctor.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Step 1: Characterize — Rest or action? One side or both? Fine or constant? Caffeine effect? Alcohol effect?"]

Step two: review your medications. Every prescription, every over-the-counter drug, every supplement. Look specifically for the categories I mentioned — mood stabilizers, bronchodilators, SSRIs, calcium channel blockers, lithium. And think about timing. Did the tremor start or worsen after a medication was added or a dose was changed?

Step three: check the amplifiers. How's your sleep? Your stress level? Your caffeine intake? Your thyroid function — a simple blood test can rule out hyperthyroidism, which is a common and treatable cause of tremor.

Step four: see your doctor. Bring your characterization from step one, your medication review from step two, and your amplifier assessment from step three. This isn't something to self-diagnose. But going in with organized information makes the evaluation dramatically more efficient.

[PAUSE]

And step five — and this is the one that's hardest — try not to catastrophize while you're waiting for answers. I know that's easier said than done. But remember the statistics. Most hand tremors are not Parkinson's. Many are medication-related. Some are amplified physiologic tremor. Even essential tremor, which is the most common pathological tremor, is manageable and not life-threatening. The odds are in your favor.

[B-ROLL: Warm shot of Dr. Kane, slightly off-center framing]

Let me close with this. Your hands are how you interact with the world — how you feed yourself, how you write, how you hold someone's hand. When they start doing something you didn't ask them to do, it's unsettling. It feels like a loss of control. And that feeling is valid.

But a tremor is a symptom, not a verdict. It's your body sending a signal, and the signal has a source. Sometimes that source is a medication that can be adjusted. Sometimes it's a lifestyle factor that can be modified. Sometimes it's a neurological condition that benefits from early treatment. And sometimes — more often than you'd expect — it's a combination of small factors that, once identified, can be addressed.

The key is not to sit with the fear alone. Bring it to your doctor. Bring your observations. And if your doctor dismisses it without investigating, get a second opinion. You deserve to know what's happening in your body.

[PAUSE — direct to camera]

If this helped put tremor in perspective — or if you've been quietly worried about this — I'd love to keep you in the loop. I put out new videos every week that help you understand what's happening inside your body after sixty. Subscribe if you haven't already.

Next week, I'm covering something that connects to what we discussed today — why food can start tasting different after sixty, and what that change might be telling you about your body. It's a strange topic, but the mechanism is fascinating, and it might explain something you've been experiencing without knowing why.

I'll see you in the next one.
