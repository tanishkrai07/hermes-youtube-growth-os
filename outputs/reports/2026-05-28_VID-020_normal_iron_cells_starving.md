But they're not fine.

And here's the thing — I believe them. Not because I'm a nice person. Because in a remarkable number of cases, something IS wrong. It's just that the standard blood test didn't catch it.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Normal blood work does NOT always mean normal iron"]

Today I want to talk about one of the most common diagnostic gaps I see in older adults: the difference between having "normal" iron on a standard blood panel and actually having enough iron inside your cells to function.

Because those two things are not the same. Not even close.

---

**THE STANDARD IRON TEST — AND WHY IT ISN'T ENOUGH**

[ON-SCREEN: "Standard Iron Panel — What It Measures"]

When your doctor orders a "standard metabolic panel" or a "CBC with iron studies," what you're typically getting is:

A complete blood count — which measures your hemoglobin, hematocrit, and red blood cell characteristics. This tells your doctor about the oxygen-carrying capacity of your blood.

A serum iron level — which measures the iron currently circulating in your blood. This is a snapshot of what's floating around right now, today.

A ferritin level — which is the one most people focus on. Ferritin is a protein that stores iron. Your serum ferritin level is supposed to reflect your total body iron stores. The lab's "normal" range is typically something like 15 to 150 nanograms per milliliter for older adults.

And a TIBC or transferrin saturation — which measures how much of your iron-transport system is being used.

That's the standard workup. And here's the problem.

[LEAN IN]

Ferritin is not just an iron storage protein. It's also what's called an acute-phase reactant. That means your ferritin levels rise in response to inflammation, infection, liver disease, and — critically — the chronic low-grade inflammation that increases naturally with age, a phenomenon sometimes called "inflammaging."

[GRAPHIC: Split screen — "Ferritin = Iron Storage" on left, "Ferritin = Inflammation Signal" on right, with arrows showing both inputs]

Let me make this concrete. If you have a ferritin level of 60 — solidly in the normal range — that number might mean you have adequate iron stores, and no inflammation. Or it might mean your iron stores are actually quite low, but your body is producing enough inflammatory signals to push your ferritin up into the normal range anyway.

The test can't tell you which one it is. It just gives you a single number that's influenced by two completely different inputs. And the inflammation side of that equation can mask a significant iron deficiency right under the test's nose.

[PAUSE]

A 2023 study in the *Journal of Nutrition, Health and Aging* looked at exactly this problem. Researchers studied adults over 65 who had "normal" ferritin levels but also had clinical signs consistent with iron deficiency — fatigue, reduced exercise tolerance, cognitive fog. When they applied additional testing (which I'll describe in a moment), they found that roughly 22 percent of these "normal" patients actually had functional iron deficiency. Their ferritin looked fine. Their cells were starving.

Twenty-two percent. That's not a rounding error. That's potentially millions of older adults who are told they're fine when they're not.

---

**WHAT "FUNCTIONAL IRON DEFICIENCY" ACTUALLY MEANS**

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Functional Iron Deficiency — Your stores look full, but your cells are empty"]

Let me explain what's happening at the cellular level, because it matters.

Inside your cells, iron is essential. It's not optional. Iron is a critical component of:

Hemoglobin — which carries oxygen in your blood. You probably know this one.

Myoglobin — which stores and delivers oxygen inside your muscle tissue. Without enough myoglobin, your muscles can't work efficiently. They fatigue faster. They recover slower.

Cytochromes — these are proteins in your mitochondria, the energy-producing structures inside every cell. Cytochromes use iron to generate ATP — the energy molecule your body runs on. Low iron means low cytochromes means low cellular energy. Every cell in your body, from your brain to your heart to your muscles, needs this.

And your brain specifically uses iron for the synthesis of neurotransmitters — dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine. Iron deficiency doesn't just make you tired. It affects your mood, your cognition, your ability to concentrate. There's a reason that unexplained cognitive fog is one of the most common symptoms of iron deficiency in older adults.

[GRAPHIC: Cell diagram showing iron dependent processes — hemoglobin, myoglobin, cytochromes, neurotransmitter synthesis — each dimming when iron drops]

Now, here's the cascade. When your cells are not getting enough usable iron, they need a way to signal for more. And they do this through a remarkable mechanism involving a receptor on the surface of every cell that needs iron.

It's called the transferrin receptor. When iron is plentiful, your cells don't express many transferrin receptors on their surface — they don't need to grab for iron. But when iron inside the cell drops, the cell starts producing more transferrin receptors. It's desperately trying to pull iron in from the bloodstream.

And here's the key: when the cell produces excess transferrin receptors, some of them get cleaved off and enter the bloodstream. They become what's called soluble transferrin receptor — or sTfR.

[ON-SCREEN: "Soluble Transferrin Receptor (sTfR) — The Body's 'I Need Iron' Signal"]

The level of sTfR in your blood tells you how hard your cells are fighting for iron. It's a direct signal from your tissues about their iron status. And unlike ferritin, sTfR is NOT affected by inflammation. It's not an acute-phase reactant. It goes up when cells need iron, and it goes down when they have enough. Period.

It's a cleaner signal. A more honest signal.

---

**THE MISSING TEST**

[ON-SCREEN: "sTfR — The Test Your Doctor May Not Order"]

So here's the uncomfortable question: if sTfR is a better measure of your body's actual iron status, why doesn't every doctor order it?

The answer is partly inertia, partly cost, partly the fact that standard panels have been the standard for decades. Most physicians were trained to evaluate ferritin and hemoglobin and consider the iron question answered. sTfR is a more specialized test. It's not typically included in a standard metabolic panel. Your doctor has to specifically order it.

But here's what the evidence says.

A landmark 2019 meta-analysis published in *The Lancet Haematology* reviewed 33 studies comparing sTfR to bone marrow iron staining — the gold standard for assessing actual body iron stores. The conclusion: sTfR was significantly more accurate than ferritin at detecting true iron deficiency, particularly in patients with chronic inflammation or chronic disease. In populations with any degree of inflammatory burden — which describes most adults over sixty — ferritin was unreliable. sTfR was not.

This isn't fringe science. This is in *The Lancet*. But it hasn't made its way into routine clinical practice yet. And that disconnect is costing patients years of unexplained fatigue.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "sTfR — More accurate than ferritin when inflammation is present"]

---

**IT'S NOT JUST ABOUT FATIGUE**

[PAUSE — shift in tone, slightly more serious]

I want to cover this because it's important, and because it's the part that usually surprises people.

Iron deficiency in older adults — even mild, "subclinical" iron deficiency where hemoglobin is still normal — has been associated with several serious outcomes beyond fatigue.

A 2024 study in *BMJ Open* following 2,800 adults over 65 for five years found that those with functional iron deficiency (normal ferritin but elevated sTfR) had a 40 percent higher risk of cardiovascular events compared to those with normal iron status by both measures. The researchers hypothesized that the chronic cellular energy deficit — particularly in cardiac muscle — was contributing to declining heart function over time.

Now, association is not causation. I want to be careful with that language. But a 40 percent increase in cardiovascular events is not a small finding. And it points to something I see clinically: older adults whose heart function is slowly declining, whose doctors attribute it to "age," when the real driver might be insufficient iron delivery to cardiac muscle that can't produce enough energy to pump efficiently.

[B-ROLL: Simple animation — heart muscle cells with mitochondrial energy production declining as iron drops]

There's also the cognitive connection. The brain uses roughly 20 percent of the body's total oxygen consumption, despite being only 2 percent of body weight. That's an enormous energy demand. When iron-dependent energy production drops, the brain is one of the first organs to show it — as difficulty concentrating, memory lapses, and that persistent, hard-to-describe "fog" that so many patients report.

A 2023 study in *Age and Ageing* found that functional iron deficiency in adults over 65 was associated with measurably slower processing speed and reduced executive function, even when hemoglobin was normal. These patients didn't have anemia by standard definitions. But their brains weren't getting enough iron to function at full capacity.

[PAUSE]

This is what I mean when I say the standard test isn't enough. It's not just about catching "anemia" — the state where your hemoglobin drops low enough to meet a diagnostic threshold for treatment. It's about catching the decline before you get there.

---

**WHAT COULD BE MAKING THIS WORSE**

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "3 Hidden Iron Drains After 60"]

Before I get to the conversation script, I want to cover three other factors that can contribute to functional iron deficiency in older adults. Because even if your test results are "normal," understanding these factors helps you and your doctor think more completely about what's going on.

[BRIEF PAUSE]

Factor one: the stomach acid issue I talked about in another video. Iron absorption is acid-dependent. If your stomach acid has declined — or if you're taking a proton pump inhibitor — you may be absorbing significantly less iron from your food and supplements than you think. This is a compounding factor. Low intake combined with poor absorption creates a double squeeze on your iron supply.

Factor two: chronic blood loss from the gastrointestinal tract. This is particularly important for older adults. Slow, occult GI bleeding — bleeding that's not visible in stool — can come from polyps, angiodysplasia (small vascular malformations in the GI tract that become more common with age), or even from chronic use of NSAIDs like ibuprofen or aspirin. The blood loss can be gradual enough that you don't notice it, but consistent enough to deplete iron stores over months or years.

This is one reason why I always say: if your iron status is declining and you can't explain why, your doctor should be evaluating your GI tract, not just handing you an iron supplement. The iron deficiency might be the symptom, not the disease.

[ON-SCREEN: "Rule: Declining iron after 60 → Evaluate GI tract, don't just supplement"]

Factor three: hepcidin. This is a hormone your liver produces that acts as the master regulator of iron in your body. When hepcidin levels are high, your body blocks iron absorption from the gut and locks iron inside storage sites, making it unavailable for use. Hepcidin increases in response to inflammation. So if you have any chronic inflammatory condition — arthritis, cardiovascular disease, chronic infections — your elevated hepcidin levels can be simultaneously blocking iron absorption AND trapping iron in storage.

This is the mechanism behind what's called "anemia of chronic disease" — and it's far more common in older adults than most people realize. Your ferritin looks normal or even high (because iron is locked in storage), but your cells can't access it. sTfR can help distinguish this pattern from true iron deficiency, which is another reason it's so valuable as a test.

[GRAPHIC: Hepcidin mechanism — liver produces hepcidin → iron locked in storage → cells can't access → function fails]

**THE CONVERSATION — WORD FOR WORD**

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "What To Say To Your Doctor — The sTfR Script"]

Okay. If any of this sounds like you — if you're over sixty, you have fatigue or brain fog or declining exercise tolerance, and your doctor said your iron was "fine" — here's what you want to say at your next appointment. You don't have to memorize it. You can pull up this video and read it directly.

"Doctor, I've been experiencing persistent fatigue and [whatever your symptoms are]. I know my ferritin was in the normal range, but I've learned that ferritin can be falsely elevated by inflammation. I'd like to ask whether we could run a soluble transferrin receptor test — sTfR — to get a more accurate picture of whether my cells are actually getting enough iron. I understand it's a more specific marker for functional iron deficiency, especially in older adults with any degree of chronic inflammation."

[PAUSE]

That's the whole script. That's it. It's respectful, it's specific, and it shows you've done your homework without being confrontational.

Your doctor may or may not be familiar with sTfR. That's okay. It's a real test. It's covered by most insurance when medically indicated. It's available at standard labs like Quest and LabCorp. Your doctor can look it up or consult with a hematologist. The point is to open the door.

You can also add: "And if the sTfR is elevated, could we talk about why? Whether it's absorption, GI blood loss, or inflammation-driven hepcidin elevation — I'd like to understand the cause, not just treat the number."

Because the cause matters. If your iron is low because of GI bleeding, supplementing without investigating the source would be missing something potentially serious. If it's low because of stomach acid decline affecting absorption, the fix might be as simple as changing your supplement form or timing. If it's low because of chronic inflammation locking iron in storage, the real treatment is managing the underlying inflammation.

Those are very different treatment paths. And none of them start with "here's an iron pill, come back in six months."

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Your iron is 'normal' but you're still tired. Here's why."]

If you're over 60 and constantly exhausted — but your doctor says your blood work is fine — you might have something called functional iron deficiency.

Here's the problem. The standard test measures ferritin — a protein that stores iron. BUT ferritin also rises when you have ANY inflammation in your body.

So you can have low iron stores that your body desperately needs... while your ferritin levels look completely normal. The inflammation masks the deficiency.

There's a better test called soluble transferrin receptor — sTfR. It's NOT affected by inflammation. It shows what your cells are actually experiencing.

At your next appointment, ask your doctor: "Can we run an sTfR test? I want to make sure my cells are actually getting iron."

Bring the question. Most doctors will respect it.
