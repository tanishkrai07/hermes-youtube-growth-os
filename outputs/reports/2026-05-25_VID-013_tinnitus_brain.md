**DR. KANE (V.O.):**

Here's what's actually happening inside your neck right now. On each side of your cervical spine — running from the base of your skull all the way down to about the sixth cervical vertebra — you have two arteries called the vertebral arteries. They're the main blood supply to the back of your brain. The brainstem. The cerebellum. And critically — the auditory cortex. The part of your brain that interprets sound.

Now here's the detail that changes everything. These arteries don't just float freely in your neck. They travel through tiny bony holes — tunnels, essentially — in each vertebra. The medical term is "transverse foramina." Every cervical vertebra from C1 to C6 has one of these tunnels on each side, and the vertebral arteries pass directly through them.

**[ON SCREEN: Close-up diagram of a single cervical vertebra from the side, with the transverse foramen highlighted and labeled. Arrow shows the vertebral artery passing through.]**

Think of it like a garden hose running through a series of small rings. The hose is the artery. The rings are the vertebrae. As long as those rings are open and the hose has room, blood flows freely.

But what happens when those rings start to change shape?

### [SECTION 2 — WHAT AGING DOES TO YOUR NECK BONES — 4:15–8:30]

**[B-ROLL: Time-lapse style animation — a healthy cervical vertebra slowly developing bone spurs (osteophytes) around the edges of the transverse foramen. The opening narrows progressively. The vertebral artery inside gets slightly compressed. Text overlay: "Cervical Spondylosis — Age-Related Spinal Changes"]**

**DR. KANE:**

After sixty, most people's cervical spines have started to change. I'm not talking about a disease. I'm talking about the same process that gives you creaky knees and a stiff back in the morning.

It's called cervical spondylosis. And it's extraordinarily common. Studies using MRI scans show that by age 65, over 80 percent of people have some degree of cervical spine degeneration — whether they feel it or not.

**[ON SCREEN: "85% of people over 65 show cervical spondylosis on MRI — most have no symptoms"]**

Here's what that looks like on the inside. The discs between your vertebrae lose height. The joints develop osteoarthritis. And the body, trying to stabilize itself, grows extra bone. These are called osteophytes — bone spurs.

Now, most of the time, we think about bone spurs in terms of pinched nerves. Sciatica. Radiculopathy. That's what gets all the attention.

But bone spurs don't only grow where nerves are. They grow around the transverse foramina too. And when they grow inward — into that tiny tunnel where the vertebral artery passes — they narrow the space. They compress the artery. Sometimes partially. Sometimes significantly.

**[B-ROLL: Side-by-side comparison — LEFT: Open transverse foramen, artery with full blood flow (animated red pulse). RIGHT: Narrowed foramen with bone spurs, artery compressed, reduced blood flow (dimmer, slower pulse). Text: "Normal vs. Compressed"]**

**DR. KANE (V.O.):**

And here's the critical piece. The vertebral arteries are the ONLY blood supply to the posterior part of your brain. There's no backup route. No detour. If these arteries deliver less blood, the brainstem and the auditory cortex get less oxygen and fewer nutrients. Period.

**[CUT TO: Dr. Kane on camera]**

**DR. KANE:**

So what does a brain region with reduced blood flow do? It misfires. Neurons that should fire in an organized pattern start firing erratically. And when neurons in the auditory cortex fire erratically, your brain interprets that random electrical activity as sound.

That's tinnitus. Not from your ear. From your brain. Starved of blood because an artery in your neck is being squeezed by your own bones.

**[PAUSE — let it land]**

I know that sounds like a lot. But stay with me, because this is actually good news. And I'll tell you why in a moment.

### [SECTION 3 — WHY THIS GETS MISSED — 8:30–11:00]

When a patient comes in with ringing ears, the standard workup is an audiogram. A hearing test. And if there's hearing loss — which there often is, because we lose high-frequency hearing with age — the tinnitus gets attributed to that. "Your cochlea is damaged. The ringing is your brain filling in the missing frequencies."

That model isn't wrong. It's just incomplete. It explains maybe 20 to 30 percent of cases.

**[ON SCREEN: "The Cochlear Model explains ~20-30% of tinnitus cases"]**

But if you take a step back and look at the vascular anatomy — if you actually image the vertebral arteries and assess blood flow velocity through the posterior circulation — a different picture emerges.

But they're all one problem. One mechanism. The cervical spine is changing, the vertebral arteries are being affected, and the brain is telling you about it — in the only language it has. Sound.

### [SECTION 4 — THE THREE MORNING MOVES — 11:00–17:00]

**[B-ROLL: Calm morning setting. A person in their 60s waking up, putting on comfortable clothes. Soft morning light. Text overlay: "3 Morning Moves for Better Neck Blood Flow"]**

**DR. KANE:**

Alright. Now for the part you came for. What do we actually do about this?

I want to give you three simple movements. You can do them in bed before you even stand up, or right after you get up. They take about four minutes total. And they're designed to do one thing: open up the space in your cervical vertebrae, release tension on the vertebral arteries, and improve blood flow to the back of your brain.

**[ON SCREEN: "Move #1 — Chin Tucks"]**

**DR. KANE:**

**Number one. Chin tucks.**

These are the single most effective exercise I recommend for cervical spine health, and almost nobody does them.

Here's how. Lie on your back or sit up straight. Now, without tilting your head up or down, pull your chin straight back — like you're making a double chin. You should feel a gentle stretch at the base of your skull and the back of your neck.

**[B-ROLL: Close-up demonstration from the side. Slow, clear movement. Arrows show the direction of motion. Text: "Pull chin STRAIGHT BACK — not down, not up."]**

**DR. KANE (V.O.):**

Hold that for five seconds. Relax. And repeat ten times.

What this does mechanically is create a tiny bit of space between the vertebrae at the top of your neck — C1, C2, C3 — exactly where the vertebral arteries are most vulnerable to compression. You're essentially giving those arteries a little more room to do their job.

**[CUT TO: Dr. Kane on camera]**

**DR. KANE:**

**Move number two. Slow neck rotations.**

**[ON SCREEN: "Move #2 — Neck Rotations"]**

This one's about mobility and blood flow. Turn your head slowly to the right — just until you feel a gentle stretch. Hold for three seconds. Come back to center. Then slowly to the left. Three seconds. Back to center.

**[B-ROLL: Demonstration from the front. Very slow, controlled rotation. Text: "SLOW. No forcing. No bouncing."]**

**DR. KANE (V.O.):**

Do five on each side. And I mean slow. We're not stretching a muscle here. We're gently pumping blood through arteries that may have been compressed all night while you were sleeping.

**DR. KANE:**

Now, I want to be specific about something. If you feel dizzy during these rotations — stop. That's actually useful information. It's not an emergency in most cases, but it's a data point they should know about.

**[ON SCREEN: "⚠️ Dizziness during neck rotation? Tell your doctor."]**

**DR. KANE:**

**And number three. The upper trapezius stretch.**

**[ON SCREEN: "Move #3 — Upper Trap Stretch"]**

**[B-ROLL: Demonstration. Person sitting, one hand gently pulling the head toward the opposite shoulder. The other hand reaches down, holding the edge of the chair for a deeper stretch.]**

**DR. KANE (V.O.):**

The upper trapezius muscle runs from the base of your skull down to your shoulder. When this muscle gets tight — and it almost always does after sixty, especially if you've spent years at a desk or looking down at a phone — it pulls on the structures at the base of your skull and can indirectly compress the vertebral arteries where they enter the spine.

Here's the stretch. Sit in a chair. Take your right hand and place it under your thigh, holding the chair seat. Now gently tilt your left ear toward your left shoulder. You should feel a stretch along the right side of your neck and into the top of your shoulder.

Hold for twenty seconds. Breathe. Switch sides.

**[CUT TO: Dr. Kane on camera]**

**DR. KANE:**

Twenty seconds each side. Deep breaths. Let the muscle release.

**[ON SCREEN: Summary card — "The 3 Morning Moves: 1) Chin Tucks — 10 reps, 5-sec hold / 2) Neck Rotations — 5 each side, slow / 3) Upper Trap Stretch — 20 sec each side / Total time: ~4 minutes"]**

**DR. KANE:**

That's it. Four minutes. Every morning. Do these consistently for two weeks and pay attention to whether the ringing changes — whether it gets quieter, less frequent, or changes in character. Your body will tell you if this is the right track.

And I want to be clear — these movements support vascular health. They're not a cure. But for a significant number of people, improving vertebral artery blood flow can meaningfully reduce tinnitus that's been driven by posterior circulation insufficiency.
