### THE HORMONAL ALARM CLOCK

**DR. KANE:**
Your body doesn't just passively exist overnight. It has a schedule. A circadian program that runs whether you're paying attention to it or not.

[ON-SCREEN: Graphic — simple timeline showing 10 PM to 8 AM with hormone markers]

Between about 4 and 8 AM, two hormones surge. Growth hormone — produced by your pituitary gland — and cortisol, produced by your adrenal glands. Both of these hormones are supposed to be highest in the early morning hours. This is normal. Everyone does this.

Here's what they do to your blood sugar.

[ON-SCREEN: Animated graphic — liver releasing glucose into bloodstream]

Growth hormone tells your liver: release stored glucose. It signals your hepatocytes — your liver cells — to convert glycogen into glucose and push it into your bloodstream. Cortisol amplifies this signal. Together, they prepare your body for waking up. Evolutionarily, this makes sense — your brain needs fuel before your eyes even open.

In a younger body with a robust insulin response, the pancreas detects this glucose release and compensates. Insulin rises gently, muscle tissue absorbs the extra glucose, and by the time you wake up, your blood sugar is essentially unchanged.

[PAUSE]

After 60, that compensatory mechanism gets unreliable. Insulin response slows. Muscle tissue — particularly if you've lost some of it to sarcopenia — absorbs less glucose. And the hormonal surge itself may be slightly exaggerated because of changes in sleep architecture and HPA axis regulation.

The result: your liver dumps glucose, your body can't clear it fast enough, and your fasting number is significantly higher than your bedtime number.

That's the dawn phenomenon.

---

### WHY DOCTORS MISS THIS

**DR. KANE:**
Here's where it gets frustrating — and I say this with respect for my colleagues in primary care, because I understand the time constraints they work under.

Most fasting glucose is measured at a single point. You go to your doctor, you get a blood draw, and the lab reports a number. If that number is over 126 mg/dL on two separate occasions, it meets the threshold for diabetes diagnosis. Between 100 and 125, it's prediabetic.

But the lab report doesn't tell you *when* you ate last, *when* you woke up, or *when* the blood was drawn relative to your hormonal surge. It doesn't capture the trajectory.

So a patient who goes to bed at 100 and wakes at 147 — that patient's fasting glucose might be drawn at 8:30 AM, when the dawn surge is waning, or at 10 AM, when it's mostly resolved. The number captures one moment on a curve nobody is measuring.

[ON-SCREEN: Graph — blood sugar curve over 24 hours, showing the dawn peak between 5-8 AM]

What's more, when a doctor sees an elevated fasting glucose, the instinct is to adjust medication — often by increasing a diabetes drug dose, or adding one. And here's where it can go wrong.

Because there's a *second* reason your fasting blood sugar can be high. And the treatment is the opposite.

---

### THE FORK IN THE ROAD: DAWN VS. SOMOGYI

**DR. KANE:**
If your fasting blood sugar is higher than your bedtime number, there are two possible explanations. And they require completely different responses.

[ON-SCREEN: Two columns — "Dawn Phenomenon" vs. "Somogyi Effect"]

The first is what we've been discussing — the dawn phenomenon. Your liver releases glucose because of a natural hormonal surge. No medication involved. No nighttime low. Just your circadian hormones doing their job while your insulin response lags behind.

The second is the Somogyi effect — named after the researcher who described it. In this scenario, your blood sugar actually drops too low overnight — usually because of medication. A sulfonylurea dose that's too high, or insulin that peaks while you're sleeping. Your blood sugar crashes, maybe into the 60s or 50s, and your body panics. The same hormones — cortisol, growth hormone — surge as an emergency response, and your liver floods your bloodstream with glucose to bring you back up.

By morning, your glucose is high. But it got there by going low first.

[LEAN IN]

Here's why this distinction matters so much. If you have the dawn phenomenon, the response might involve adjusting evening routine, changing what you eat at dinner, or — in some cases — adjusting medication timing so that peak drug effect covers the early morning hours.

If you have the Somogyi effect, the response is to *reduce* medication. Taking more medication when you're already crashing overnight will make the problem worse, not better.

And without checking your overnight glucose, you — and your doctor — are guessing.

---

### THE 3-NIGHT TEST

**DR. KANE:**
So how do you figure out which one you have? You do a simple overnight check.

[ON-SCREEN: Protocol card — "The 3-Night Dawn Protocol"]

Here's the protocol. For three nights, at the following times, check your blood sugar:

- **Night 1:** Bedtime, then 3 AM
- **Night 2:** Bedtime, then 3 AM
- **Night 3:** Bedtime, then 3 AM

That's it. Six measurements over three nights.

[PAUSE]

If your bedtime glucose is in a normal range and your 3 AM glucose is *also* normal or slightly elevated — say, within 20 points of your bedtime number — you're likely looking at the dawn phenomenon. Your glucose is rising gradually toward morning because of hormones, not because of a crash.

If your 3 AM glucose is *low* — below 70, or even below 80 if you're symptomatic — that's the Somogyi signal. Your body overcorrected, and the morning high is a rebound.

I know — waking up at 3 AM isn't anyone's favorite activity. But three nights of data is worth more than months of guessing.

[ON-SCREEN: Quick note — "If you wear a CGM (continuous glucose monitor), this data is already being collected automatically."]

If you have a continuous glucose monitor, this is even easier. Just pull up your overnight trend. You'll see the pattern clearly — a steady rise from bedtime to morning, or a dip-and-rebound curve.

### WHAT ACTUALLY WORKS

**DR. KANE:**
Okay. So you've done your overnight test. Your 3 AM numbers are fine — no crash. You've confirmed it's the dawn phenomenon. What do you actually *do* about it?

Let me walk you through what the evidence supports, in order of impact.

**[EMPHASIZE]**

First: what you eat at dinner matters more than what you eat at breakfast. I know — everyone talks about breakfast. But if your evening meal is heavy on refined carbohydrates — white rice, bread, pasta — you've given your liver more substrate to convert into glucose overnight. Shifting dinner toward protein and non-starchy vegetables changes the substrate your liver has available.

[ON-SCREEN: Quick visual — "Dinner shift: more protein + vegetables, fewer refined carbs"]

Second — and this one has actual research behind it — a small protein snack before bed. Not a large meal. Something in the range of 15 to 20 grams of protein. A small serving of Greek yogurt, a handful of nuts, or a slice of turkey. A 2023 study in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition found that a pre-sleep protein snack blunted the dawn glucose spike without increasing overall caloric load meaningfully. The mechanism: protein stimulates a modest insulin release and provides amino acids that slow hepatic glucose output.

[ON-SCREEN: "Study reference: Am J Clin Nutr 2023 — pre-sleep protein blunted dawn glucose spike"]

Third — and this is where it gets interesting from a vascular medicine standpoint — gentle movement after dinner. A 10 to 15 minute walk. Not exercise. Just walking. This activates your GLUT-4 transporters in muscle tissue — those are the channels that bring glucose into muscle cells without requiring as much insulin. That glucose has somewhere to go besides just sitting in your bloodstream.

Fourth — there's a small but frequently cited study from 2017, published in Diabetes Care, showing that apple cider vinegar before bed was associated with lower morning glucose. The proposed mechanism is acetic acid slowing gastric emptying and reducing hepatic glucose output. The effect size is modest. I'm not going to oversell it. But it's safe, and if you're looking for something to add to your routine, it has more research backing than most supplements.

[PAUSE]

And fifth — talk to your doctor about medication *timing*. Not dose. Timing. Some diabetes medications have duration of action that peaks in the morning when taken at night, which can shift peak coverage to the early morning hours. This is different from increasing your dose. This is chronopharmacology — matching the drug's peak effect to the time your body needs it most.

**Do not adjust your medication on your own.** But bring your overnight glucose data to your appointment and ask: "Could my medication timing be optimized for the dawn phenomenon?"

---

### WHEN TO BRING THIS TO YOUR DOCTOR

**DR. KANE:**
There are a few red flags that mean this isn't just routine dawn physiology — it's something that needs medical evaluation.

[ON-SCREEN: Red flag list]

If your fasting glucose is **consistently above 180**, that's not dawn phenomenon — that's uncontrolled diabetes, and it needs treatment.

If you're experiencing **night sweats, confusion on waking, or vivid nightmares** alongside high morning glucose, that could be the Somogyi effect — your body is crashing overnight and you may not even be aware of it.

If you've made dietary changes and your fasting glucose **hasn't budged after two to three weeks**, it's time to have a conversation about whether medication adjustment is appropriate.

And if you're on a sulfonylurea — drugs like glipizide or glyburide — and you're experiencing dawn phenomenon, that's a specific conversation to have with your prescriber, because those medications carry a higher risk of nocturnal hypoglycemia, which means you could be getting *both* the Somogyi effect and the dawn phenomenon simultaneously.
