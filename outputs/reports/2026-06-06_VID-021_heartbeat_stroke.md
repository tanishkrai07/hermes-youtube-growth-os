### SCRIPT

[GRAPHIC: Dr. Kane, warm lighting, conversational setting — starts with a calm, measured expression, like he's about to share something important but not alarming]

I want to tell you about a patient. I won't use his name — I'll call him Robert.

Robert is seventy-one. Retired engineer. The kind of person who keeps lists, checks things twice, and doesn't go to the doctor unless something is measurably wrong. He came to me for a routine vascular check — nothing urgent, just a follow-up on some mild peripheral artery disease we'd been monitoring.

And while I was taking his history, he mentioned something almost in passing. He said, "Oh, and my watch has been telling me my heart is doing something weird. But I'm sure it's nothing."

[PAUSE]

I asked him what he meant. He pulled out his Apple Watch and showed me. Over the past three weeks, the watch had flagged an irregular heartbeat notification four times. Four separate episodes. Each one lasting somewhere between two and eight minutes. And then — nothing. Back to normal.

Now, here's what's important. Robert had seen his primary care doctor two months before this visit. He'd had an EKG in the office. It was completely normal. His doctor told him his heart rhythm was fine. And Robert, being the kind of person who trusts his doctor, had dismissed the watch notifications as a glitch.

But they weren't a glitch.

[LEAN IN]

Robert had atrial fibrillation. Intermittent, paroxysmal atrial fibrillation — the kind that comes and goes, the kind that a standard in-office EKG almost never catches because it only records for about six seconds. And here's the part that should get your attention: atrial fibrillation is responsible for roughly one in four strokes in people over sixty-five. One in four. And a significant portion of those strokes happen in people who had no idea they had AFib — because it was silent, intermittent, and never captured on a standard test.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "AFib causes 1 in 4 strokes after 65 — and up to 40% is SILENT"]

I'm Dr. Kane. I'm a vascular medicine physician. And today I want to talk about something that I think is one of the most under-discussed stroke prevention opportunities for adults over sixty: the fact that your heartbeat can change in ways that predict stroke risk, and the standard medical system is not set up to catch it.

This isn't about being afraid of your heart. This is about understanding what your pulse can tell you — and what you can do with that information.

---

**THE RHYTHM YOU DON'T FEEL**

[ON-SCREEN: "Atrial Fibrillation — What It Actually Is"]

Let me explain what's happening electrically, because the mechanism matters for understanding why it's so hard to detect.

Your heart has its own electrical system. A small cluster of cells in the upper right chamber — the sinoatrial node — acts as a natural pacemaker. It fires an electrical signal that travels in an organized wave through the upper chambers (the atria), then down to the lower chambers (the ventricles), causing them to contract in a coordinated rhythm. That's the lub-dub you hear through a stethoscope. That's the pulse you feel at your wrist.

In atrial fibrillation, that organized electrical signal breaks down. Instead of one clean wave, the atria start firing electrical signals from multiple locations at once — chaotically, irregularly. The upper chambers don't contract properly. They just quiver. And the lower chambers respond to this chaos by beating irregularly — sometimes fast, sometimes slow, sometimes with pauses in between.

[GRAPHIC: Animated heart — normal sinus rhythm (organized wave) vs. AFib (chaotic firing, irregular ventricular response)]

Now, here's the critical part for stroke risk. When the atria don't contract properly, blood pools in a small pouch called the left atrial appendage. Pooled blood can form clots. And if a clot breaks free, it travels through the arterial system — and if it reaches the brain, it causes a stroke. An AFib-related stroke tends to be particularly severe because the clots are often large.

A 2024 meta-analysis in *Stroke* — the journal of the American Heart Association — reviewed 47 studies and found that AFib-related strokes were associated with a 2.5-fold increase in mortality compared to non-AFib strokes, and significantly higher rates of permanent disability. This isn't a minor risk. This is one of the most consequential stroke mechanisms we know about.

[PAUSE]

But here's what makes AFib so tricky. And this is the part that I think is genuinely underappreciated.

---

**THE DETECTION GAP**

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Why Your Doctor Might Miss Your AFib"]

There are three types of atrial fibrillation, and the distinction matters enormously for detection.

The first is persistent AFib — the heart stays in AFib continuously. This is the easiest to detect. An EKG in the doctor's office will catch it. A stethoscope will hear it. It's obvious.

The second is permanent AFib — the heart has been in AFib for so long that attempts to restore normal rhythm have been abandoned. Also easy to detect.

The third — and this is the one I want to focus on — is paroxysmal AFib. Paroxysmal means it comes and goes. The heart flips into AFib for minutes or hours, then spontaneously converts back to normal rhythm. It might happen three times a week. It might happen once a month. It might happen at 3 AM when you're asleep and you never feel a thing.

[GRAPHIC: Timeline showing paroxysmal AFib episodes — irregular, unpredictable, with long normal-rhythm gaps between them]

Paroxysmal AFib accounts for roughly 30 to 40 percent of all AFib cases. And it is dramatically underdiagnosed. Why? Because the standard detection method — a 12-lead EKG in the doctor's office — records about six to ten seconds of heart rhythm. If your heart is in normal rhythm during those six seconds, the EKG reads "normal sinus rhythm." The AFib that happened yesterday, or that will happen tomorrow, is invisible to that test.

A 2023 study in *JAMA Cardiology* looked at exactly this problem. Researchers gave 200 adults over 65 — all of whom had "normal" in-office EKGs — a wearable cardiac monitor to wear for 30 days. Over that month, 12 percent of them were found to have episodes of paroxysmal AFib that their office EKG had completely missed. Twelve percent. In a group of people who had been told their hearts were fine.

Twelve percent of two hundred is twenty-four people. Twenty-four people who thought they were fine, who had a significant stroke risk they didn't know about.

[PAUSE — let that number sit]

Now, I want to be careful here. I'm not saying everyone needs a 30-day cardiac monitor. Those are expensive, and for most people, they're not necessary. But I am saying that there's a middle ground between "trust the six-second EKG" and "wear a medical device for a month." And that middle ground is something you can do at home, for free, in about thirty seconds.

---

**THE 30-SECOND CHECK**

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "The 30-Second Pulse Check — What Your Doctor Doesn't Have Time To Do"]

Here's what I recommend to my patients. And I want to be specific, because vague advice isn't useful.

Once a week — pick a day, make it a habit, maybe Sunday morning with your coffee — sit down, relax for two minutes, and check your pulse for a full thirty seconds. Not six seconds. Not ten seconds. Thirty.

Here's how. Place the index and middle fingers of one hand on the inside of the other wrist, just below the thumb. You're feeling the radial artery. Apply light pressure until you can feel the pulse clearly. Then count the beats for thirty seconds and multiply by two to get your beats per minute.

But here's the part that matters more than the number. Pay attention to the rhythm. Is it regular? Is it metronome-steady — beat, beat, beat, beat — or is it irregular? Does it feel like it's skipping? Does the spacing between beats vary — short-long-short-long-short — in a way that doesn't follow a pattern?

[ON-SCREEN: "Regular: beat-beat-beat-beat (metronome) | Irregular: beat--beat---beat-beat----beat (no pattern)"]

A normal sinus rhythm is remarkably regular. The variation between beats is minimal — maybe a few milliseconds. You might notice a slight variation with breathing (faster on the inhale, slower on the exhale) — that's normal, that's called respiratory sinus arrhythmia, and it's a sign of a healthy autonomic nervous system.

What you're listening for — feeling for — is something different. An irregularly irregular rhythm. A pattern that has no pattern. Beats that come in clusters, then pause, then come again. That's the hallmark of atrial fibrillation. And it's something you can feel with your fingers, even if you've never had medical training.

[PAUSE]

Now, I want to address the obvious question. "Dr. Kane, I'm not a doctor. How am I supposed to tell the difference between a normal variation and something serious?"

Fair question. Here's my answer. You don't need to make a diagnosis. You need to notice a pattern. If your pulse feels irregular — truly irregular, not just "I'm not sure" — on two or more checks, separated by at least a few days, that's worth mentioning to your doctor. You're not diagnosing yourself. You're reporting an observation. And a good doctor will take that observation seriously.

[B-ROLL: Close-up of fingers on wrist, pulse waveform on screen transitioning from regular to irregular]

There's actually research supporting this approach. A 2024 study in the *British Journal of General Practice* trained 150 older adults to perform a 30-second pulse check and report irregularities. Over six months, the participants detected paroxysmal AFib at a rate three times higher than the control group that relied solely on routine office visits. The study concluded that "patient-performed pulse palpation is a feasible, low-cost screening strategy for paroxysmal AFib in community-dwelling older adults."

Three times higher detection rate. With fingers and a clock.

---

**WHY DOCTORS CHECK FOR SIX SECONDS**

[ON-SCREEN: "The 6-Second Problem"]

I want to explain why the standard medical approach misses this, because it's not your doctor's fault — it's a system problem.

When your doctor listens to your heart or does an EKG, they're getting a snapshot. Six to ten seconds of data. If you have paroxysmal AFib that happens, say, twice a week for a few minutes at a time, the probability of catching it during a random six-second window in the doctor's office is very low. It's like trying to diagnose a intermittent car engine problem by listening to it for six seconds while it's idling in the parking lot.

The medical system knows this. That's why there are longer-term monitoring options — Holter monitors (24-48 hours), event monitors (weeks to months), and implantable loop recorders (years). These are excellent tools. But they're typically reserved for patients who already have symptoms — palpitations, dizziness, fainting — or who have already had a stroke of unknown origin.

The problem is that a huge number of people with paroxysmal AFib have no symptoms at all. They feel fine. They don't feel the irregular rhythm. The AFib is silent. And because they feel fine, they don't report symptoms. And because they don't report symptoms, they don't get the longer monitoring. And because they don't get the longer monitoring, the AFib goes undetected. And then one day, a clot forms, breaks free, and causes a stroke. And nobody saw it coming.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "The Silent AFib Cycle: No symptoms → No report → No monitoring → No detection → Stroke"]

A 2025 analysis in *Circulation: Arrhythmia and Electrophysiology* estimated that approximately 30 to 40 percent of AFib-related strokes occur in patients who were not previously diagnosed with AFib. The stroke is the first sign. That's not prevention. That's too late.

[PAUSE — shift in tone]

This is why I'm so passionate about the simple pulse check. It's not a replacement for medical evaluation. It's a screening tool. It's a way to close the detection gap between "I feel fine" and "my heart is doing something I don't know about."

---

**WHAT MAKES THIS MORE LIKELY AFTER 60**

[ON-SCREEN: "Why AFib Risk Increases After 60"]

Let me give you the risk factors, because understanding them helps you assess your own situation.

Age is the single biggest risk factor for atrial fibrillation. The prevalence roughly doubles with each decade after fifty. By age sixty-five, about 4 percent of the population has AFib. By age eighty, it's closer to 10 to 12 percent.

Why? Several mechanisms. The atria — the upper chambers — undergo structural changes as you age. The heart muscle fibers can become fibrotic, meaning they're replaced by scar-like tissue that doesn't conduct electricity well. This creates the substrate for the chaotic electrical firing that defines AFib.

[GRAPHIC: Cross-section of atrium — young (uniform tissue) vs. aging (fibrotic patches disrupting electrical conduction)]

High blood pressure — which affects roughly two-thirds of adults over sixty — stretches the atria over time, which promotes fibrosis. Sleep apnea — which is vastly underdiagnosed in older adults — causes intermittent oxygen drops that stress the atrial tissue. Obesity, diabetes, thyroid disease, excessive alcohol use — all of these increase AFib risk.

And here's one that's particularly relevant to my vascular medicine background: the health of the small blood vessels supplying the heart's electrical system matters. The sinoatrial node — the natural pacemaker — is fed by a small artery. If that artery narrows or becomes less efficient with age, the pacemaker cells can become ischemic — oxygen-starved — and start firing errically. It's a microvascular problem that creates a macro-scale rhythm problem.

A 2024 study in the *Journal of the American Heart Association* found that patients with evidence of coronary microvascular dysfunction — reduced blood flow in the small vessels of the heart — had a 60 percent higher incidence of new-onset AFib over five years compared to those with normal microvascular function. This is the kind of connection that doesn't get discussed enough. Your heartbeat isn't just an electrical problem. It's a vascular problem. The health of your smallest blood vessels affects the rhythm of your largest muscle.

[PAUSE]

---

**WHAT TO DO IF YOU FIND SOMETHING**

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "If Your Pulse Feels Irregular — A Step-by-Step Guide"]

Okay. Let's say you've been doing the 30-second check, and you've noticed an irregular rhythm on more than one occasion. What do I recommend?

Step one: don't panic. An irregular pulse can have causes other than AFib. Premature atrial contractions — extra beats that originate in the atria — are extremely common in older adults and are usually benign. They feel like a skip or a flutter, and they're different from the sustained irregularly irregular rhythm of AFib. But you don't need to distinguish between these on your own. That's what your doctor is for.

Step two: document it. Write down when you noticed it, how long it lasted, and what it felt like. If you have a smartwatch or a KardiaMobile device, take a recording. This information is incredibly valuable for your doctor.

Step three: call your doctor. Not the emergency room — unless you're having chest pain, shortness of breath, or other acute symptoms. Call your primary care doctor or cardiologist and say: "I've been checking my pulse at home, and I've noticed an irregular rhythm on multiple occasions. I'd like to be evaluated for possible atrial fibrillation."

[ON-SCREEN: "What to say: 'I've noticed an irregular pulse on multiple occasions. I'd like to be evaluated for possible AFib.'"]

Step four: ask about extended monitoring. If your in-office EKG is normal — which it might be, especially if you're not in AFib at that moment — ask whether a longer-term monitor would be appropriate. A 30-day event monitor or a patch monitor (like the Zio patch) can capture intermittent episodes that a six-second EKG misses.

Step five: if AFib is confirmed, understand your treatment options. This is where the conversation gets important, and where I want to be very clear: I'm not going to tell you what treatment you should accept. That's between you and your cardiologist. But I want you to know that the treatment landscape for AFib has improved dramatically. There are anticoagulant medications that significantly reduce stroke risk. There are procedures — catheter ablation — that can restore normal rhythm in many patients. And there are lifestyle modifications that can reduce the frequency of episodes.

The key is knowing it's there. Everything else follows from that.

**THE BIGGER PICTURE**

[PAUSE — Dr. Kane sits back slightly, reflective]

I want to zoom out for a moment, because this topic connects to something I think about a lot in my practice.

We live in a medical system that's very good at responding to acute problems. You have a heart attack — the system responds. You have a stroke — the system responds. But we're not nearly as good at the quiet, preventive work — the things that happen before the crisis, the signals your body sends that something is changing.

Your pulse is one of those signals. It's been there your entire life, beating away, telling you something about the state of your cardiovascular system. And after sixty, when the risk of atrial fibrillation increases, when the consequences of missing it are so severe, paying attention to that signal isn't anxiety. It's stewardship. It's taking responsibility for the information your body is giving you.

Robert — the patient I told you about at the start — was started on an anticoagulant after we confirmed his AFib with a 30-day monitor. He's had no strokes. No episodes that he's noticed. His watch still occasionally flags an irregular rhythm, and he still checks his pulse every Sunday morning. He's not living in fear. He's living with awareness. And that awareness — that simple, thirty-second habit — may have prevented a stroke.

If you're over 60, your doctor's EKG might be missing something important.
