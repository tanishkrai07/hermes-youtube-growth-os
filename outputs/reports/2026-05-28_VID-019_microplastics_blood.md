### SCRIPT

[GRAPHIC: Close-up of a standard blood test vial — then a subtle shift: tiny particles visible floating among the blood cells, slightly glowing]

Last week, a sixty-eight-year-old patient came to me for a routine consultation. Nothing urgent. He'd just had his annual bloodwork done. Numbers looked fine — cholesterol manageable, kidney function stable. But he'd been reading.

"They found microplastics in blood now," he said. "Is that... is that in my blood too?"

[PAUSE]

Honest answer? Almost certainly. And the research increasingly suggests that after sixty, it's not just whether they're there — it's what they're doing now that they've arrived.

[PAUSE]

I'm Dr. Kane. I'm a vascular medicine physician. Not a toxicologist, not an environmental scientist. But I spend my days thinking about what flows through blood vessels and how blood vessels respond to what's in them. And from that vantage point, what microplastics are doing inside the circulatory system — particularly in older adults — is something I find both genuinely concerning and genuinely underexplained.

[VIDEO CHAPTER MARKER: "The Journey — How Microplastics Enter Your Blood"]

Let me walk you through how they get there, because the journey matters.

You swallow microplastics. They're in your food, in your water, in the air you breathe. A 2025 study from the journal Environment International — led by Abbasi and colleagues at the University of Bergen — estimated that the average person ingests roughly five grams of plastic per week. That's the weight of a credit card. Per week. Much of it is in the form of particles smaller than a micrometer — nanoplastic scale. Small enough to cross biological barriers that are supposed to keep foreign material out.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "~5 grams of plastic ingested per week — Environment International 2025 | Weight of a credit card"]

From your gut — and this connects to what I covered in Part 2 of this series on gut permeability — these particles cross the intestinal lining. In younger, healthier guts, the mucosal barrier is relatively intact. But after sixty, that barrier becomes "leakier." Tight junction proteins — the molecules that seal the gaps between gut lining cells — degrade with age. Chronic low-grade inflammation, which is nearly universal in older adults, further disrupts the barrier. So a seventy-year-old's gut is more permissive than a thirty-year-old's. Not dramatically so, but enough to matter over years of cumulative exposure.

[GRAPHIC: Animation — intestinal wall, tight junctions, particles crossing from gut lumen into capillary network]

Once across the gut lining, microplastics enter the portal circulation — the blood vessels that drain the intestines and travel directly to the liver. The liver is supposed to be the filter. Hepatocytes and Kupffer cells — specialized immune cells in the liver — should capture and neutralize foreign particles. And in younger people, they do. But here's where age is a factor. Kupffer cell function declines significantly with age. A 2024 review in Mechanisms of Ageing and Development found that phagocytic efficiency — the ability of these cells to engulf and destroy foreign material — drops by roughly forty percent between ages thirty and seventy.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Kupffer cell phagocytic efficiency ↓ ~40% from age 30 → 70 | Mechanisms of Ageing and Development 2024"]

So more particles are crossing the gut, and fewer are being captured by the liver. What's left? Particles that bypass the liver's filtration and enter the systemic circulation. The main highway. The same blood vessels that carry oxygen to your heart, your brain, your kidneys.

[VIDEO CHAPTER MARKER: "Inside the Bloodstream — What Microplastics Meet"]

Now, picture this. A microplastic particle enters a blood vessel in the systemic circulation. It's just been released from the portal system. It's tiny — we're talking about particles ranging from a few micrometers down to nanometers. And now it's flowing through a vessel that might be carrying it toward your brain, your heart, or your legs.

What does it find? Red blood cells — billions of them — actively transporting oxygen. Platelets — small cell fragments that are primed to trigger clotting if they detect damage. And a smooth, glistening inner lining: the endothelium. A single layer of cells that lines every blood vessel in your body, from your largest aorta to your smallest capillary. The endothelium is not just a wall. It's an active organ — it regulates blood flow, controls inflammation, prevents clotting, and maintains the barrier between your blood and your tissues.

Microplastics interact with all three of these components. Let me take them one at a time.

First: red blood cells. A landmark 2024 study published in Nature Biomedical Engineering — from a collaboration between several Chinese universities and University College London — used advanced microscopy to observe how nanoplastics interact with red blood cells in vitro. They found that polystyrene nanoparticles — one of the most common microplastic types — adhere directly to the red blood cell membrane. This adhesion changes the cell's surface properties, making the cells more fragile and more prone to aggregation. The red blood cells started sticking to each other in rouleaux formations — stacks that look like coins under a microscope.

[B-ROLL: Microscopy footage or animation — red blood cells with nanoparticles adhering, forming stacks]

Why does that matter? Because rouleaux formation increases blood viscosity — thickness. Thicker blood flows more slowly, particularly through small vessels. In a young person with healthy, flexible red cells and wide blood vessels, this is compensated for. In an older adult with already-stiff red blood cells, slightly narrowed arteries, and possibly borderline anemia? Even a small increase in viscosity can meaningfully reduce microcirculatory blood flow — the flow through the tiniest vessels that supply your extremities, your kidneys, your retina.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Nanoplastics adhere to red blood cells → rouleaux formation → ↑ blood viscosity | Nature Biomedical Engineering 2024"]

Second: platelets. This is where it gets more serious. The same study found that nanoplastic particles activate platelets. Platelets are the body's first responders to vascular injury — when they detect damage to the vessel wall, they change shape, become sticky, and start forming clots. But microplastics appear to trigger a low-level, chronic platelet activation without any actual injury present. The platelets are reacting to the foreign surface of the particles themselves.

Now, a little bit of platelet activation is manageable — your body has built-in anticoagulant mechanisms that keep this in check. But chronic, low-grade platelet activation is different. It leads to a state where the blood is slightly more prone to clotting than it should be. And in an older adult who may already have some degree of hypercoagulability — a more clot-prone state that comes with aging — this is an additional push in the wrong direction.

[PAUSE — let that framework build]

Third, and this is the one I spend the most time thinking about from a vascular medicine perspective: the endothelium.

[VIDEO CHAPTER MARKER: "The Endothelium — Your Blood Vessel's Last Line of Defense"]

Your endothelial cells are remarkable. They're in constant communication with your blood — sensing flow, sensing pressure, sensing chemical signals. When they detect danger, they release nitric oxide to dilate the vessel and reduce inflammation. When they detect damage, they signal platelets and initiate repair. They're the interface between your blood and everything else.

Microplastic particles that make it into the systemic circulation eventually come to rest against this endothelial lining. And what happens next is what concerns me most.

In 2024, a team at the University of Pavia in Italy published research in Particle and Fibre Toxicology examining how polystyrene nanoplastics interact with human endothelial cells in culture. They found that the particles are internalized by the endothelial cells — the cells literally engulf them. Once inside, the particles trigger an oxidative stress response. The cells start producing reactive oxygen species — free radicals — as if they're fighting an infection. This oxidative stress damages the cell's own machinery and, critically, reduces the cell's ability to produce nitric oxide.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Microplastics → endothelial oxidative stress → ↓ nitric oxide → impaired vessel dilation | Particle and Fibre Toxicology 2024"]

Reduced nitric oxide means the blood vessel can't dilate properly. It also means the endothelial surface becomes more adhesive — white blood cells start sticking to it. This is the same process that initiates atherosclerosis. The early stages of plaque formation. Now, I want to be very careful here — I'm not saying microplastics cause heart attacks. The research hasn't established that causation in humans yet. But the mechanistic pathway is there: microplastics trigger endothelial dysfunction through oxidative stress, and endothelial dysfunction is the foundation of atherosclerosis.

[B-ROLL: Animation — endothelial cell engulfing nanoparticle, free radicals forming, nitric oxide reduction, white cell adhesion]

For a younger person with robust endothelial repair capacity, this is a manageable insult. The cells recover, antioxidants neutralize the free radicals, nitric oxide production resumes. But endothelial repair capacity declines with age. A sixty-five-year-old's endothelial cells divide more slowly, produce less nitric oxide at baseline, and are already handling decades of oxidative stress from normal metabolism. Adding microplastic-induced oxidative stress on top of that is like adding weight to an already burdened structure.

[PAUSE — transition to human evidence]

Okay. That's the mechanism. What about the human evidence? Because I don't want to leave you with just laboratory findings.

In March 2024, a study published in the New England Journal of Medicine — one of the most prestigious medical journals in the world — changed the conversation. Researchers at the University of Campania in Italy, led by Dr. Raffaele Marfella, analyzed carotid artery plaque specimens from 304 patients who were undergoing carotid endarterectomy — surgery to remove plaque from the carotid artery in the neck. They found microplastic particles — specifically polyethylene and polyvinyl chloride — embedded within the plaque itself.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "NEJM 2024 | n=304 | Microplastics found WITHIN carotid artery plaque — polyethylene + PVC detected"]

This was not in the bloodstream. This was inside the arterial wall. Integrated into the plaque.

And here's the finding that made this study headline news. Over a mean follow-up of 34 months, patients whose plaque contained microplastics had a 4.5 times higher risk of myocardial infarction, stroke, or death compared to those whose plaque was free of microplastics. Four-point-five times.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "4.5x higher risk of MI, stroke, or death when microplastics present in carotid plaque | NEJM 2024 | 34-month follow-up"]

Now, the study authors were careful to note that this was observational — they found an association, not proof of causation. But the mechanistic data from the laboratory studies I described earlier provides a plausible pathway: microplastics integrate into plaque, they trigger chronic inflammation and endothelial dysfunction, and the plaque becomes more unstable — more likely to rupture and cause a clot.

[VIDEO CHAPTER MARKER: "What About After 60?"]

Let me bring this specifically to the over-six0 population, because this is where my clinical concern is focused.

After sixty, several things converge. You've had more decades of cumulative microplastic exposure than a younger person. Your gut barrier is more permeable. Your liver filtration is less efficient. Your endothelial repair capacity is reduced. Your baseline inflammation — what geriatricians call "inflammaging" — is already elevated. You're more likely to have atherosclerosis already developing. And you're more likely to be on medications for blood pressure, cholesterol, or diabetes — conditions that already challenge the vascular system.

So when microplastics enter the bloodstream of a sixty-five-year-old, they're entering a system that has less reserve, less resilience, and less ability to compensate than a thirty-five-year-old's. The dose might be similar. The response is different.

[PAUSE — practical transition]

Let me give you what I actually think about this, as a doctor who sees patients every day. I think the NEJM study is a signal we should take seriously. I think the mechanistic data is strong enough to warrant concern but not panic. And I think there are practical, rational steps people can take — not to eliminate microplastics from their lives, because that's essentially impossible at this point — but to reduce ongoing exposure and support the body's ability to cope with what's already there.

[Checklist appearing on screen — not a "detox" list, a reduction and support list]

Step one — and this is the easiest win — reduce ingestion from water. Microplastics are present in both tap and bottled water, and a 2024 study in PNAS found that bottled water contains roughly 240,000 nanoplastic particles per liter on average. Using a filtered water system — particularly one with a sub-micron filter — can significantly reduce this intake. If you're drinking primarily from plastic water bottles, switching to glass or stainless steel is one of the single highest-impact changes you can make.

Step two: reduce food-contact plastic. Heating food in plastic containers — microwaving in plastic, for instance — dramatically increases microplastic leaching into food. Transfer food to glass or ceramic before heating. Avoid storing acidic foods (tomatoes, citrus) in plastic containers, as acidity increases particle release.

Step three: support endothelial health. This is where my vascular medicine perspective genuinely applies. The things that support endothelial function — regular aerobic activity, a diet rich in polyphenols (berries, leafy greens, olive oil), maintaining healthy blood pressure, managing blood sugar — these are the same things that help your blood vessels handle any kind of insult, including microplastic exposure. I'm not presenting this as a detox strategy. I'm presenting it as: the healthier your endothelium, the more resilient it is.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Endothelial Support: Exercise + polyphenol-rich diet + BP management + blood sugar control = vascular resilience"]

Step four: bring this up with your doctor. Specifically, if you have early signs of vascular disease — elevated blood pressure, borderline cholesterol, early carotid plaque found on ultrasound, or a history of cardiovascular events — mention microplastic exposure as part of the conversation about cardiovascular risk factors. Your doctor may not bring it up. This is an emerging area, and most clinical guidelines haven't incorporated it yet. But informed patients who raise new evidence help move the conversation forward. And your doctor should at least be aware that you're thinking about environmental factors in cardiovascular health.

[PAUSE]

I want to be clear about what this is not. This is not a reason to panic. Microplastics have only recently begun to be studied in human tissue. We don't yet have long-term prospective data showing that reducing microplastic exposure improves outcomes. The science is moving fast, but it's early. What we do know is that the mechanistic pathway is plausible, the human tissue evidence is alarming, and the practical steps to reduce exposure are low-risk.

If you take nothing else from this video, take this: you don't need to be perfect about avoiding microplastics. You need to be informed. And you need to do the foundational things that support vascular health — because those things help your body handle all kinds of insults, including ones we're only beginning to understand.

[B-ROLL: Warm shot, slight lean toward camera]

I'll close with the question my patient asked me. "Is that in my blood too?" The answer, almost certainly, is yes. The better question — the one I'd encourage you to sit with — is: what am I doing to support the system that's handling it?

If you found this valuable — especially Part 3 of the microplastics series — I'd genuinely love to keep you in the loop. I put out new videos every week that help you understand what's happening inside your body after sixty. Subscribe if you haven't already.

[End card tease]

Next week, I'm covering something that connects to what we discussed today — why getting breathless after sixty isn't something you should just accept as aging. There's a pattern to breathlessness that, once you understand it, changes how you think about your heart and lungs. I promise it's more useful than anything you've heard on this topic before.

I'll see you in the next one.
