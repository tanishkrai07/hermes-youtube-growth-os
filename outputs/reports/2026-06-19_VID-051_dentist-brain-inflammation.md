## SCRIPT

[OPEN ON CAMERA — Dr. Kane, direct, slightly conspiratorial tone]

Here's something that happened in my practice that I haven't been able to stop thinking about.

A patient — I'll call him Robert — came to see me for a routine vascular check. He was 71. Sharp. Active. Played chess at the community center three times a week.

But his wife had noticed something. Small things. He'd forget the word for "kettle." He'd repeat a story he'd told her the day before. Nothing dramatic. Nothing that would send anyone running to a neurologist.

I ran the standard workup. Everything was within normal limits. I told him to come back in six months.

But here's the thing. Six months earlier, Robert had been to his dentist. And his dentist had noted something in his chart: generalized moderate periodontal disease. Probing depths of five to six millimeters in multiple quadrants. Bleeding on probing. The dentist had recommended a deep cleaning.

Robert had postponed it. He told me, "It's just my gums. It's not urgent."

[PAUSE]

I'm telling you this story because what Robert's dentist saw in his mouth — what Robert dismissed as "just his gums" — might have been the earliest visible sign of a process that was already affecting his brain.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "The Oral-Brain Bacterial Highway"]

Let me explain.

Your mouth is home to over 700 species of bacteria. Most of them are harmless — or even helpful — as long as they stay where they belong. But when gum disease develops, the landscape changes.

Two bacteria in particular become dominant: Porphyromonas gingivalis and Treponema denticola. You don't need to remember the names. Just know that these two are the primary pathogens behind periodontal disease. And they are very, very good at what they do.

[LEAN IN]

What they do is this: they create pockets between your gums and your teeth. These pockets — that's what your dentist measures with the little ruler they poke along your gumline — these pockets are breeding grounds. The bacteria multiply. The pocket deepens. And the tissue lining that pocket becomes inflamed and fragile.

And fragile tissue bleeds.

Every time you brush. Every time you floss. Every time you eat something with a little crunch. Blood.

[ON-SCREEN: Simple diagram showing gum pocket with bacteria, blood vessel connection]

Now, bleeding gums might seem like a minor annoyance. But every time your gums bleed, you're creating a doorway. A direct opening from the bacteria in your mouth into your bloodstream.

And once those bacteria are in your blood, they can go anywhere.

Including your brain.

[PAUSE — let this land]

I know that sounds alarming. Let me be precise about what the research shows.

A landmark 2019 study published in Science Advances found gingipains — which are toxic enzymes produced by P. gingivalis — in the brains of people who had been diagnosed with Alzheimer's disease. Not in healthy brains. In Alzheimer's-affected brains.

The same bacteria that cause gum disease were found in the brain tissue of people with dementia.

[ON-SCREEN: "Gingipains found in Alzheimer's brains — Science Advances, 2019"]

Now, finding bacteria in a diseased brain doesn't prove the bacteria caused the disease. But here's what makes the connection more than coincidental.

Animal studies have shown that P. gingivalis can cross the blood-brain barrier. Once inside the brain, it activates microglia — the brain's immune cells. And when microglia are chronically activated, they produce inflammatory chemicals that damage neurons and accelerate the accumulation of amyloid-beta — the protein that forms the plaques characteristic of Alzheimer's.

[ON-SCREEN: Simple animation showing bacteria entering bloodstream → crossing blood-brain barrier → activating microglia → amyloid-beta accumulation]

In other words, the bacteria in your mouth don't just cause cavities and bad breath. In the right conditions — conditions that become more common after 60 — they can trigger the same inflammatory cascade that destroys brain cells.

[EMPHASIZE]

This is not a fringe theory. A 2023 study in the Journal of Alzheimer's Disease followed over 6,000 adults for 18 years and found that people with periodontal disease had a 1.7 times higher risk of developing dementia — even after controlling for other risk factors.

1.7 times higher. That's not a small number.

[PAUSE]

Now, here's why I said your dentist might be your first line of defense.

Your dentist sees things your doctor doesn't. They measure your gum health at every visit. They track probing depths. They note bleeding. They see the bacterial environment in your mouth in a way that no other healthcare provider does.

And they see it years — sometimes decades — before cognitive symptoms appear.

The oral-brain bacterial highway is a slow process. It takes years for the inflammation to build. But the mouth shows the signs first.

[ON-SCREEN: "Your dentist sees it first"]

So what should you be looking for? What are the oral signs that your mouth might be sending bacteria toward your brain?

There are three.

[ON-SCREEN: "3 Oral Signs of Brain Inflammation Risk"]

Sign one: Bleeding when you brush or floss.

I know — everyone's gums bleed sometimes. But if it happens regularly, that's not normal. That's a sign of active inflammation. And active inflammation means open doorways into your bloodstream.

Sign two: Receding gums.

When your gums pull back from your teeth, they expose more surface area — and create deeper pockets where bacteria can hide. If your teeth look longer than they used to, or if you're noticing sensitivity near the gumline, that's recession. And recession means more bacterial real estate.

[PAUSE]

Sign three: Persistent bad breath that doesn't go away with brushing.

This one's embarrassing, which is exactly why people don't talk about it. But persistent halitosis — especially the kind that smells rotten — is often a sign of anaerobic bacterial overgrowth. The same bacteria that cause periodontal disease produce volatile sulfur compounds. If brushing doesn't fix it, the bacteria are below the gumline where your toothbrush can't reach.

[ON-SCREEN: Each sign appearing as Dr. Kane lists them]

If you have one or more of these signs, I'm not saying you're going to develop Alzheimer's. I'm saying your mouth is telling you something important about your systemic inflammation level. And systemic inflammation is one of the most modifiable risk factors for cognitive decline.

[LEAN IN]

So what do you do about it?

First — and I cannot stress this enough — go to the dentist. Not someday. Soon. If you haven't had a cleaning in over a year, make the appointment. A professional cleaning removes the bacterial biofilm that you cannot remove at home, no matter how well you brush.

Second, ask your dentist to measure your probing depths. This is standard practice, but not every dentist does it thoroughly at every visit. Say: "Can you tell me my probing depths? Are any of them over four millimeters?"

Four millimeters is the threshold. Below four, you're in the maintenance zone. Above four, you have active periodontal disease that needs treatment.

[ON-SCREEN: "Ask your dentist: 'What are my probing depths?'"]

Third, if your probing depths are over four millimeters, ask about a deep cleaning — also called scaling and root planing. This isn't a cosmetic procedure. It's a medical intervention that removes bacteria from below the gumline and allows the tissue to reattach to the tooth.

And here's the part that connects back to your brain: after a deep cleaning, systemic inflammatory markers drop measurably. Your CRP goes down. Your IL-6 goes down. The inflammatory burden on your entire body — including your brain — decreases.

[EMPHASIZE]

A dental cleaning is a brain health intervention. I want you to sit with that for a moment.

Every time you get your teeth cleaned, you're not just protecting your smile. You're reducing the bacterial load that could be traveling to your brain. You're lowering the inflammatory cascade that could be damaging your neurons.

Your dentist isn't just a tooth doctor. They're a frontline defender of your cognitive health.

[PAUSE]

Let me come back to Robert.

After I learned about his periodontal disease, I referred him to a periodontist. He got the deep cleaning. His probing depths improved. His bleeding stopped.

And his wife? She called me three months later. She said the word-finding issues had stabilized. He wasn't repeating stories as much. She said, "I don't know if it's related, but something shifted."

I can't prove the cleaning caused the improvement. One patient isn't a study. But the mechanism is real. The research is real. And the connection between oral health and brain health is no longer something we can afford to ignore.

[ON-SCREEN: "Robert — 3 months post-treatment: cognitive symptoms stabilized"]

If you're over 60 and you haven't seen a dentist recently, let this be your sign. Not because of your teeth — because of your brain.

Your mouth is talking. Your dentist is listening. Make sure you're part of that conversation.

[PAUSE]

If this was helpful, hit subscribe. I put out new videos every week that help you understand what's happening inside your body after 60 — including the things your doctor might not have time to explain.

Next week, I'm covering something your gut is doing that could be making your joints hurt — even when your arthritis tests are completely normal. I'll link it at the end.
