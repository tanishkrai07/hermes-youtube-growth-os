### FULL SCRIPT

**[COLD OPEN — Quiet, direct, empathetic]**

"I got your blood work back. Your iron levels are completely normal."

If a doctor has said that to you in the last few years — and you're still exhausted, still foggy, still feeling like something is off — this video is for you.

Because here's what I've seen happen hundreds of times in my practice. A patient comes in. They're tired. Not "I stayed up too late" tired. Not "I need a vacation" tired. The kind of tired that makes getting through the afternoon feel like a accomplishment.

They've had their blood work done. Their doctor checked their iron. The results came back normal. And they were told — sometimes explicitly, sometimes by implication — that their symptoms aren't from iron deficiency.

**[PAUSE]**

And technically, the doctor was right. By the standard definition, their iron levels were normal.

But here's the problem. The standard test for iron doesn't measure what your cells actually have access to. It measures a storage protein that can be artificially elevated by something completely unrelated to your iron status.

And after 60, that "something" is incredibly common.

**[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Your ferritin can be normal while your cells are iron-starving"]**

---

**[SECTION 1: THE TEST THAT LIES BY ACCIDENT]**

Let me explain how this happens. And I want to be clear — this isn't a case of doctors ordering the wrong test on purpose. The standard iron panel is a reasonable screening tool. It just has a blind spot that becomes much more relevant after 60.

When your doctor checks your iron, they're usually ordering a panel that includes serum iron, TIBC — that's total iron-binding capacity — and ferritin.

Ferritin is the one I want to focus on. Because ferritin is what doctors use as the primary marker of your iron stores. It's a protein that stores iron inside your cells and releases a small amount into your blood. The logic is simple: more ferritin in your blood means more iron stored in your body. Less ferritin means less iron stored.

**[ON-SCREEN: Diagram showing ferritin as "iron storage protein" with normal range marked]**

And for most people under 50, that logic works pretty well.

But ferritin has another identity. It's also what's called an acute-phase reactant. That means it's a protein that rises in response to inflammation. Any inflammation. Infection, injury, chronic disease, autoimmune activity, even the low-grade chronic inflammation that comes with aging.

**[ON-SCREEN: Split identity — "Ferritin = Iron Storage Protein" AND "Ferritin = Inflammation Marker"]**

So here's the scenario that plays out constantly after 60.

You have genuine iron deficiency. Your body's iron stores are depleted. Your cells aren't getting enough iron to function properly.

But you also have chronic low-grade inflammation — which is extremely common after 60. Research from Nature Aging in 2024 shows that 30 to 50 percent of adults over 60 have elevated inflammatory markers even without a diagnosed inflammatory condition. It's sometimes called "inflammaging" — the chronic, low-grade inflammatory state that accompanies aging.

That inflammation raises your ferritin. Artificially. Your ferritin level goes up — not because you have more iron, but because your body is producing more ferritin in response to inflammation.

**[ON-SCREEN: Animation showing inflammation pushing ferritin UP while actual iron stores go DOWN]**

And now your blood test shows a ferritin level that's in the "normal" range. Your doctor sees that number and concludes your iron is fine.

But your cells? They're still starving.

**[BEAT — let that land]**

---

**[SECTION 2: THE BLOOD TEST THAT SEES THROUGH IT]**

So is there a test that can see through the inflammation? That measures your actual cellular iron status regardless of whether your ferritin is being artificially elevated?

Yes. And it's been available for years. But it's rarely ordered as part of routine blood work.

It's called the soluble transferrin receptor test. Or sTfR.

**[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "sTfR — Soluble Transferrin Receptor"]**

Let me explain what this measures, because the name is intimidating but the concept is simple.

Transferrin is the protein that carries iron through your blood. It picks up iron from your gut and your storage sites, and delivers it to your cells. On the surface of every cell that needs iron, there are transferrin receptors — little docking stations that grab onto transferrin and pull the iron inside.

When your cells have enough iron, they don't need to grab as much. They reduce the number of transferrin receptors on their surface.

But when your cells are iron-starved? They put up more receptors. They're trying to catch every bit of iron they can get. It's like putting out more fishing nets when the fish are scarce.

**[ON-SCREEN: Animation showing cells with few receptors (iron-replete) vs. many receptors (iron-starved)]**

Some of those receptors break off into your bloodstream. That's the "soluble" part. And the amount of soluble transferrin receptor in your blood directly reflects how desperately your cells are trying to get iron.

Here's the key: sTfR is NOT an acute-phase reactant. It doesn't rise with inflammation. It only rises when your cells genuinely need iron.

**[ON-SCREEN: Comparison — Ferritin: rises with inflammation AND iron stores | sTfR: rises ONLY with cellular iron need]**

A 2023 study in the journal Blood confirmed that sTfR is the most reliable marker of functional iron deficiency — the kind where your stores look adequate but your cells aren't getting enough.

---

**[SECTION 3: THE CALCULATION YOU CAN DO TODAY]**

Now, here's something you can do right now. With your existing lab results. At no cost.

If you've had blood work done in the last year that included ferritin, and you also happen to have an sTfR result — or if you can request one — there's a calculation called the sTfR-to-ferritin ratio.

**[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "sTfR ÷ Ferritin = Functional Iron Status"]**

It's simple. You take your sTfR number and divide it by your ferritin number.

If the ratio is greater than 2.0, research from the European Journal of Haematology in 2024 shows that's strongly suggestive of functional iron deficiency — your cells are iron-starved despite a normal or even high ferritin.

**[ON-SCREEN: Ratio scale — <1.0 = adequate iron | 1.0-2.0 = borderline | >2.0 = functional deficiency]**

Now, I want to be practical. Most of you watching this probably don't have an sTfR result. It's not part of standard panels. But here's what you can do.

If you've been told your iron is normal but you have persistent symptoms — fatigue, brain fog, cold intolerance, restless legs, hair loss, poor exercise tolerance — you can ask your doctor to add sTfR to your next blood draw.

It's a simple add-on. It doesn't require a separate visit. And it can reveal what the standard panel misses.

**[PAUSE]**

And if your doctor isn't familiar with it — which is common, because it's more of a hematology test — you can say: "I'd like to check my soluble transferrin receptor to rule out functional iron deficiency. I've read that ferritin can be falsely normal with inflammation, and sTfR isn't affected by that."

That's informed. That's specific. And that's a reasonable request.

---

**[SECTION 4: WHY YOUR CELLS ARE STARVING]**

Let me explain why this matters. Because iron deficiency isn't just about anemia. It's about cellular energy.

Every cell in your body needs iron. Not just your red blood cells — every cell. Iron is a critical component of the electron transport chain in your mitochondria. That's how your cells produce ATP — the energy currency of your body.

When your cells don't have enough iron, they can't produce energy efficiently. It's like trying to run a factory with the power turned down half voltage. Everything still works. But everything works slower.

**[ON-SCREEN: Mitochondria animation — normal ATP production vs. reduced ATP with iron deficiency]**

This is why functional iron deficiency causes fatigue that's disproportionate to any anemia you might have. Your hemoglobin might be normal. Your red blood cell count might be fine. But your cells are running on reduced power.

And it's not just energy. Iron is required for the synthesis of neurotransmitters — dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine. Low cellular iron can contribute to brain fog, low mood, and difficulty concentrating.

Iron is also required for immune function. Your white blood cells need iron to proliferate and fight infection. Functional iron deficiency can make you more susceptible to illness and slower to recover.

**[ON-SCREEN: Three icons — Brain (neurotransmitters), Muscle (energy), Shield (immune) — all connected to iron]**

A 2024 study in the Journal of the American Geriatrics Society found that seniors with functional iron deficiency — normal ferritin, elevated sTfR — had 40 percent worse fatigue scores than those with truly adequate iron, even when their hemoglobin levels were identical.

Forty percent. That's not a small difference. That's the difference between having energy to play with your grandchildren and needing a nap by 2 PM.

---

**[SECTION 5: THREE CAUSES AFTER 60]**

So what causes functional iron deficiency after 60? Let me give you the three most common.

**[ON-SCREEN: Numbered list]**

**Cause number one: Reduced dietary iron absorption.** This connects directly to what I covered in the stomach acid video. Iron from food — especially non-heme iron from plant sources — requires gastric acid for absorption. As stomach acid declines after 60, iron absorption drops. You might be eating the same iron-rich foods you always have, but absorbing significantly less.

**Cause number two: Chronic blood loss.** This is the one that's often missed. Slow, low-grade blood loss from the gastrointestinal tract — from polyps, from gastritis, from hemorrhoids, from medication irritation — can gradually deplete iron stores. You might not see blood in your stool. It might be microscopic. But over months and years, it adds up.

This is one reason why any unexplained iron deficiency in a senior should prompt a discussion about GI evaluation. Not because it's likely to be serious — most of the time it's not — but because it should be ruled out.

**[PAUSE]**

**Cause number three: Medication effects.** NSAIDs — ibuprofen, naproxen, aspirin — can cause slow GI blood loss. Proton pump inhibitors reduce iron absorption. And some medications can interfere with iron metabolism at the cellular level.

If you're on any of these medications and you have unexplained fatigue, the medication-iron connection is worth exploring.

---

**[SECTION 6: WHAT TO ASK YOUR DOCTOR]**

Let me give you that script again. Because this is a conversation that can change how you feel.

**[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Doctor Conversation Script"]**

*"My ferritin came back normal, but I'm still experiencing significant fatigue and brain fog. I've read about functional iron deficiency — where ferritin can be normal because of inflammation, but cells are still iron-starved. Could we check a soluble transferrin receptor test to see if that's what's happening? And could we calculate the sTfR-to-ferritin ratio?"*

If your doctor is unfamiliar with sTfR, you can add:

*"It's a hematology test that measures cellular iron demand. It's not affected by inflammation the way ferritin is. I'd like to rule out functional iron deficiency before we look for other causes."*

**[PAUSE]**

And here's an important follow-up question:

*"If we find functional iron deficiency, what's the best approach to correcting it? Should we try dietary changes, oral supplementation, or is there a reason to consider IV iron?"*

That last question matters because the treatment depends on the cause and severity. And I want to talk about that.

---

**[SECTION 7: THE IRON DECISION TREE]**

If you do have functional iron deficiency, what are your options?

**[ON-SCREEN: Decision tree graphic]**

**Option one: Dietary optimization.** If the cause is reduced absorption, increasing dietary iron from heme sources — red meat, liver, shellfish — can help. Heme iron is absorbed more efficiently than non-heme iron and is less affected by stomach acid levels. Pairing iron-rich foods with vitamin C can further enhance absorption.

But — and this is important — dietary changes alone may not be sufficient if your absorption is significantly impaired or if you have ongoing blood loss.

**Option two: Oral iron supplementation.** This is the most common approach. But here's where it gets nuanced. If your stomach acid is low — which we've established is common after 60 — standard iron supplements like ferrous sulfate may not absorb well.

Liquid iron formulations, heme iron polypeptide supplements, or iron bisglycinate may be better absorbed in low-acid environments. This is worth discussing with your doctor or pharmacist.

**[PAUSE]**

And I need to say this clearly: **do not start iron supplementation without confirming deficiency first.** Excess iron is dangerous. It accumulates in organs — liver, heart, pancreas — and can cause serious damage. Iron is one of those nutrients where more is definitely not better.

**Option three: IV iron supplementation.** For significant functional iron deficiency that hasn't responded to oral supplementation, or for people who can't tolerate oral iron, IV iron can restore stores quickly and bypass the absorption problem entirely.

A 2023 study in Lancet Hematology found that IV iron supplementation in seniors with functional iron deficiency improved fatigue scores by 35 percent and cognitive test scores by 20 percent over 12 weeks.

That's a meaningful improvement. And it's available — you just need to know to ask.

---

**[SECTION 8: THE INFLAMMATION CONNECTION]**

Before I close, I want to come back to the inflammation piece. Because if your ferritin is being elevated by inflammation, simply adding iron won't address the underlying issue.

If you have functional iron deficiency driven by chronic inflammation, the real question is: what's driving the inflammation?

Sometimes it's a known condition — rheumatoid arthritis, chronic infection, autoimmune disease. In those cases, treating the underlying condition is the priority.

But sometimes it's the low-grade, systemic inflammation of aging itself. And while we can't stop aging, there are evidence-based approaches that can reduce inflammatory burden.

Regular moderate exercise. Adequate sleep. Stress management. A diet rich in omega-3 fatty acids and polyphenols. Maintaining a healthy weight — particularly reducing visceral fat, which is metabolically active and produces inflammatory cytokines.

**[ON-SCREEN: Anti-inflammatory lifestyle factors — exercise, sleep, diet, stress, weight]**

These aren't just generic health recommendations. They're specifically relevant to functional iron deficiency because they address the inflammation that's masking your true iron status.

---

**[CLOSING — Warm, hopeful]**

Here's what I want you to take away from this.

If you've been told your iron is normal but you feel anything but normal — you're not imagining it. You're not "just getting older." There's a real, measurable, treatable condition that standard blood work can miss.

Functional iron deficiency affects 15 to 25 percent of seniors. That's potentially one in four people watching this right now. And the solution starts with a test that takes five minutes to order and a conversation that takes five minutes to have.

You deserve to feel better. And sometimes, the answer is simpler than we think — we just need to know what to look for.
