### [SECTION 3 — WHY SIXTY CHANGES EVERYTHING — 11:00–15:30]

[ON-SCREEN GRAPHIC: Side-by-side comparison graphic. Left side: "Age 30" with an icon of a person in sunlight — skin produces vitamin D efficiently (label: "10,000–25,000 IU per day from sun exposure potential"), kidneys convert efficiently. Right side: "Age 70" with similar icon, but dimmer sunlight, smaller vitamin D output ("Reduced by up to 75%"), kidneys less efficient.]

[DR. KANE ON CAMERA — Warmer, more personal tone.]

Let me explain why this conversation matters so much more after sixty than at any other time in life.

When you were thirty years old, your skin was remarkably efficient at producing vitamin D. A few minutes of sun exposure — arms and face, no sunscreen, mid-morning — your skin could manufacture tens of thousands of international units of vitamin D3. Your kidneys were converting that into the active form, calcitriol, with full efficiency. Your liver's CYP24A1 system was in balance — breaking down excess, preserving what you needed.

By the time you've reached sixty or seventy, several things have changed simultaneously, and they compound each other.

First: your skin has thinned, and the concentration of 7-dehydrocholesterol — the precursor molecule your skin needs to make vitamin D from UVB light — has decreased significantly. Some research suggests a seventy-year-old's skin produces roughly seventy-five percent less vitamin D than a twenty-year-old's skin under identical sun exposure.

[ON-SCREEN GRAPHIC: A simple bar graph. Bar 1 (Age 25): Full height, labeled "100% skin synthesis capacity." Bar 2 (Age 60): About 50% height, labeled "~50–60%." Bar 3 (Age 75+): About 25% height, labeled "~25–30%." Clean, easy to read.]

Second: your kidneys. The enzyme 1-alpha-hydroxylase, which converts 25-hydroxyvitamin D into the fully active calcitriol, becomes less active with age. So even the vitamin D you DO have — from supplements, from food, from whatever sunlight you get — is being activated less efficiently.

Third — and this is the piece nobody tells you — your intestinal absorption of vitamin D from food decreases with age as well. The vitamin D you get from fatty fish, fortified dairy, egg yolks — your gut is less efficient at absorbing it.

[B-ROLL: Dr. Kane on camera. Medium close-up. Slow, thoughtful.]

So now picture this. Even without any medications, you're starting from a lower baseline than you were at forty. You're making less, activating less, and absorbing less. And then, on top of that entirely normal aging process, you introduce a medication that tells your liver to destroy what little active vitamin D you do have at an accelerated rate.

That's the perfect storm. And it's why, in my practice, I've found that a surprising number of my patients over sixty on long-term medications are running vitamin D levels between ten and twenty nanograms per milliliter — well below the thirty that most experts consider the minimum for adequate bone health.

[PAUSE — 2 seconds]

Now let me show you what that deficiency does inside your body, because the cascade it sets off is more serious than most people realize.

[DR. KANE ON CAMERA — Medium shot. Serious but not alarming. Clear, measured delivery.]

This is the cascade. And once it starts, it is self-reinforcing.

When your vitamin D level drops below about thirty nanograms per milliliter, your small intestine loses its ability to efficiently absorb calcium from the food you eat. Under normal circumstances, your gut absorbs somewhere between thirty and forty percent of the dietary calcium you consume. When vitamin D drops, that absorption rate can fall to ten, maybe fifteen percent. You could be drinking milk, eating yogurt, taking calcium supplements — and your body is barely using any of it.

Now your blood calcium level starts to dip. Not dramatically — your body doesn't allow that. Because calcium in your blood isn't just about bones. It's about nerve conduction. It's about your heart rhythm. Your body will sacrifice almost anything to keep blood calcium stable. And what it sacrifices is your bones.

[LEAN IN]

The four tiny parathyroid glands sitting behind your thyroid in your neck — each about the size of a grain of rice — they're constantly monitoring your blood calcium. The moment they detect even a slight decline, they secrete parathyroid hormone — PTH. And PTH has one urgent message for your skeleton: Release calcium. Now.

PTH stimulates osteoclasts — the cells that break down bone tissue — to dissolve the calcium out of your bones and release it into your bloodstream. It's an emergency measure. Your body is literally cannibalizing your skeleton to keep your heart and nerves functioning.

[ON-SCREEN GRAPHIC: Magnified illustration of a parathyroid gland with an alarm/exclamation icon, then an arrow to simplified bone cross-section showing osteoclasts actively breaking down bone matrix and releasing calcium (shown as small dots flowing into a blood vessel).]

In the short term, you feel nothing. Your blood calcium is normal. Your doctor's basic metabolic panel looks fine. But your bones are thinning, day after day, month after month.

After sixty, this matters enormously. Hip fractures. Vertebral compression fractures — those are the ones that cause the stooped posture you see in some elderly individuals, the loss of height. Wrist fractures from a simple fall that a younger person would walk away from.

And here's what makes the medication connection so important. A patient on a CYP24A1-inducing drug doesn't just have a mild vitamin D shortfall. They have a metabolic situation where their body is actively destroying the active hormone form of vitamin D faster than they can replace it through normal means. The standard folk recommendation of "just go outside and get some sun" isn't going to compensate for enzymatic destruction of active calcitriol in the liver.

[SLOW DOWN — deliberate, caring delivery]

You need to know this. Not to be frightened. So you can walk into your doctor's office and say exactly the right thing. Which brings me to what I think is the most useful part of this entire video.
