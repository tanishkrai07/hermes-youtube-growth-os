### SCRIPT

[GRAPHIC: Dr. Kane in frame, warm lighting, conversational setting — no white coat]

Let me start with a question. When was the last time you stumbled? Not a full fall — just that moment where your body shifted unexpectedly and you had to catch yourself. Maybe reaching for something on a high shelf. Maybe stepping off a curb that was slightly higher than you expected. Maybe just turning around too quickly in the kitchen.

[PAUSE]

If you're over sixty and that's happened to you recently, you probably told yourself the same thing most people tell themselves: "I just wasn't paying attention." Or "I'm getting old." And you moved on. Because it felt minor. Because everyone talks about balance declining with age like it's as inevitable as gray hair.

But here's what I want you to consider. What if that stumble wasn't carelessness? What if it was information?

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Balance loss after 60 — carelessness or information?"]

I'm Dr. Kane. I'm a vascular medicine physician. And today I want to explain what's actually happening inside your body when your balance starts to change after sixty. Because the reason most people don't get help for this — until something serious happens — is that nobody explains the mechanism. They just say "be more careful." And being more careful is fine. But it's not a solution if there's an underlying physiological cause that can be identified and addressed.

[PAUSE — transition]

Let me give you the framework first, and then we'll go deep on each piece. Because this is one of those topics where the framework itself is useful.

Your balance — your ability to stand upright, walk without veering, turn without getting dizzy — depends on three systems working together. Three. And they all send information to your brain simultaneously, and your brain integrates all of that data in real time to keep you stable.

System one: your vestibular system. That's your inner ear. Specifically, the semicircular canals and the otolith organs. They detect motion, acceleration, and the position of your head relative to gravity. Think of them as your internal gyroscope.

System two: your visual system. Your eyes. They tell your brain where you are in space — what's level, what's moving, how fast. You need your eyes for balance more than most people realize.

[GRAPHIC: Simple animated diagram — human figure with three highlighted zones: inner ear, eyes, feet/legs]

System three: your proprioceptive system. These are sensors in your muscles, joints, and tendons — especially in your feet and ankles — that tell your brain where your body parts are without you having to look at them. That's how you can walk without staring at your feet.

Now here's the critical thing. All three of these systems degrade with age. Not at the same rate, not in the same way, but all three. And when one system starts to decline, the other two compensate — up to a point. You don't notice the decline because your brain is covering for it. Until it can't anymore.

[PAUSE — let that land]

That stumble you had? That might have been the moment the compensation ran out.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "3 Balance Systems: Vestibular | Visual | Proprioceptive — All decline with age"]

Let me walk through each one, because understanding what's changing in each system helps you understand what to watch for.

Starting with the inner ear. Inside your semicircular canals, there are tiny hair cells — stereocilia — that are embedded in a gel-like structure. When you move your head, the fluid in those canals moves, the hair cells bend, and that bending sends electrical signals to your brain about the direction and speed of your movement. It's an elegant system. And it's fragile.

After age sixty, you lose approximately forty percent of those hair cells compared to age twenty. Forty percent. And unlike some other cells in your body, hair cells don't regenerate. Once they're gone, they're gone. The result: your inner ear becomes less sensitive to small movements and position changes. Your brain gets less accurate data about where your head is in space.

[B-ROLL: Clean animation — inner ear structure, hair cells bending in fluid, then showing reduced signal output with aging]

Now, you might think "okay, so my inner ear is aging — that's just what happens." And you'd be right, it is what happens. But here's where it gets practical. There's a specific condition called benign paroxysmal positional vertigo — BPPV — that becomes significantly more common after sixty. It affects roughly ten percent of people over seventy. And it's caused by tiny calcium carbonate crystals — otoconia — that break loose from their normal location in the inner ear and drift into the semicircular canals. When that happens, moving your head in certain directions triggers intense vertigo — the room spinning, nausea, the feeling that you're going to fall.

[PAUSE]

BPPV is treatable. There's a specific physical maneuver — the Epley maneuver — that repositions those crystals. It takes about fifteen minutes and can resolve the vertigo in one or two sessions. But most people who develop BPPV don't know what it is. They just think "my balance is going" and they start restricting their movements. Which actually makes things worse, because reducing movement leads to deconditioning, which further degrades all three balance systems.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "BPPV — 10% of people over 70 | Treatable with Epley maneuver | Often misdiagnosed as 'just aging'"]

That's system one. Now let's talk about system two — your eyes.

This one surprises people. Most folks don't connect their vision to their balance. But your visual system provides roughly seventy percent of the spatial orientation information your brain uses to keep you upright. Seventy percent. So when your vision changes — and it does, significantly, after sixty — your balance is directly affected.

The changes are multiple. Your pupils become smaller and less responsive to light changes, so your eyes adjust more slowly when you go from bright to dim environments. That's why so many people feel unsteady walking into a dark restaurant or getting up at night. Your lens becomes less transparent — early cataracts scatter light, reducing contrast sensitivity. Your peripheral vision narrows. And the muscles that control eye movement become slightly slower, which affects your ability to track moving objects and maintain gaze stability while walking.

[B-ROLL: Split screen — young eye vs. aging eye, showing pupil size, lens clarity, peripheral vision range]

A 2025 study in the Journal of Gerontology followed 1,200 adults over sixty-five for three years and found that those with uncorrected vision changes were 2.3 times more likely to experience a fall compared to those whose vision was properly corrected. Two-point-three times. That's not a small effect. And it's one of the most addressable risk factors for balance problems.

[PAUSE — transition to system three]

System three — proprioception. This is the one I find most interesting from a vascular medicine perspective.

Those sensors in your feet and ankles? They depend on healthy nerve function. And nerve function depends on blood flow. After sixty, peripheral neuropathy — nerve damage in the extremities — becomes increasingly common. It can be caused by diabetes, by vitamin B12 deficiency, by reduced peripheral circulation, or sometimes by medications.

When proprioceptive input from your feet declines, your brain loses its ability to make the tiny, constant micro-adjustments that keep you upright. You know how you can feel yourself making small corrections when you stand on one foot? That's proprioception working. When those signals get weaker or less precise, you become more dependent on your eyes and your inner ear. Which are also declining. So now all three systems are compromised, and the safety margin is thin.

[GRAPHIC: Animated feedback loop — feet sensors → spinal cord → brain → muscle adjustments → stability. Then showing degraded signals with aging.]

Here's a number that I think puts this in perspective. A landmark study published in Age and Ageing in 2024 — analyzing data from over 5,000 adults — found that the combination of mild peripheral neuropathy AND reduced vestibular function increased fall risk by 4.7 times compared to either factor alone. It's not additive. It's multiplicative. The systems compensate for each other until they can't, and then the decline accelerates.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Age and Ageing 2024 | n=5,000+ | Neuropathy + Vestibular decline = 4.7x fall risk"]

[PAUSE — lean toward camera]

Now. I've just described three systems that all decline with age. And if I stopped there, this would be a depressing video. But I'm not going to stop there. Because there's a fourth factor that almost nobody talks about, and it's one that you can actually do something about starting today.

Medications.

[GRAPHIC: Pills spilling from a bottle]

More than half of adults over sixty-five take five or more medications daily. Five or more. And a significant number of commonly prescribed medications have dizziness, vertigo, or balance impairment as a documented side effect. The American Geriatrics Society's Beers Criteria — which is the standard reference for medications that are potentially inappropriate in older adults — lists dozens of drugs that affect balance.

Let me give you the categories.

Blood pressure medications — particularly alpha-blockers and vasodilators — can cause orthostatic hypotension. That's a drop in blood pressure when you stand up. Your brain briefly doesn't get enough blood flow, and you feel lightheaded or unsteady. It's one of the most common medication-related causes of balance problems in older adults.

[B-ROLL: Animation — blood pressure dropping upon standing, brain perfusion decreasing]

Benzodiazepines and sleep medications — drugs like lorazepam, diazepam, zolpidem — they directly suppress vestibular function. They literally slow down the processing in your inner ear and your brain's ability to integrate balance signals. A 2024 meta-analysis in the British Medical Journal found that benzodiazepine use in adults over sixty was associated with a fifty-three percent increased risk of falls requiring emergency department visits.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "BMJ 2024 | Benzodiazepines → 53% ↑ fall-related ER visits in 60+"]

Anticholinergic medications — and this is the sneaky one — are found in many common over-the-counter and prescription drugs. Some antihistamines, some antidepressants, some bladder medications, some anti-nausea drugs. They block acetylcholine, which is a neurotransmitter involved in vestibular processing and spatial orientation. Many people don't even know they're taking an anticholinergic. They just know they feel a bit foggy or unsteady.

And here's the compounding problem. If you're on multiple medications — and again, many people over sixty are — the balance-related side effects can stack. One drug causes mild dizziness. Another causes slight drowsiness. A third affects your blood pressure. Each one alone might be manageable. Together, they can significantly impair your balance without any single drug being obviously responsible.

[PAUSE]

I had a patient — I'll call her Margaret — seventy-one years old, came to me because she'd had two falls in three months. Nothing broken, but she was shaken. She was convinced something neurological was happening. She'd already seen a neurologist who ordered an MRI that came back clean. She was scared.

I reviewed her medication list. She was on seven medications. Seven. One was a blood pressure medication that can cause orthostatic drops. One was an antihistamine she took daily for allergies — anticholinergic. One was a low-dose benzodiazepine she'd been taking for sleep for over a year. Any one of those alone might not have caused a problem. But together, they were creating a perfect storm of balance impairment.

I didn't change her medications — that's her prescribing doctors' decision. But I wrote a letter outlining the balance-related side effects of each drug and suggested she discuss a medication review with her primary care physician. Within two months, her doctor had adjusted two of the medications. Margaret hasn't had a fall since.

[PAUSE — let that story breathe]

I'm not telling you to stop any medication. I would never do that. But I am telling you that if you're experiencing balance changes and you're on multiple medications, the medication list is one of the first things that should be reviewed. And it often isn't, because each doctor prescribed each medication individually, and nobody's looking at the combined effect on your balance.

[GRAPHIC: Checklist appearing on screen]

Alright. So what do you actually do with all of this? Let me give you a practical framework.

First: pay attention to the pattern. When do you feel unsteady? Is it when you first stand up? That suggests blood pressure regulation. Is it when you turn your head quickly? That could be vestibular — possibly BPPV. Is it in dim lighting? That's often visual dependence — your proprioception and vestibular systems aren't compensating well enough when your vision is reduced. Is it constant and vague? That's more likely medication-related or multi-system decline.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Pattern Recognition: Standing = BP | Head turning = Vestibular | Dim light = Visual | Constant = Medications / Multi-system"]

Second: get your vision checked. I know that sounds basic, but a surprising number of people are walking around with outdated prescriptions or early cataracts that haven't been addressed. If your vision is contributing to your balance problem, correcting it is one of the simplest and most effective interventions.

Third: ask your doctor for a medication review specifically focused on balance. Not just "are these medications still necessary" — but "could any of these be contributing to dizziness or unsteady gait?" Bring the Beers Criteria list if you need to. Your doctor will know what it is.

[PAUSE]

Fourth: consider a falls risk assessment. Many primary care offices and geriatric clinics offer these. They typically include a timed up-and-go test — you stand from a chair, walk three meters, turn around, walk back, and sit down. It takes about thirty seconds and it's a remarkably good predictor of fall risk. There's also the Romberg test, where you stand with your feet together and eyes closed — it isolates your proprioceptive system by removing visual input. These are simple, quick, and they give your doctor actionable information.

Fifth — and this is the one I'm most passionate about — move. I know that sounds counterintuitive when you're worried about falling. But physical activity — specifically balance-focused exercise — is one of the most effective interventions for age-related balance decline. A 2025 Cochrane Review — which is the gold standard for evidence synthesis — analyzed 108 randomized controlled trials and found that exercise programs incorporating balance training reduced fall rates by twenty-three percent in adults over sixty. Tai chi, in particular, has strong evidence. It trains all three systems simultaneously — vestibular, visual, and proprioceptive — in a low-impact, controlled way.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Cochrane Review 2025 | 108 trials | Balance training → 23% ↓ fall rate | Tai chi especially effective"]

The worst thing you can do when you start feeling unsteady is stop moving. I understand the instinct. But reduced activity leads to muscle weakness, which leads to worse balance, which leads to more fear of falling, which leads to even less activity. It's a downward spiral. And it's one of the most preventable causes of functional decline in older adults.

[PAUSE — transition to close]

Let me zoom out for a moment.

Falls are the leading cause of injury-related death in adults over sixty-five. That's not a statistic I'm sharing to scare you — I'm sharing it because it's the reason this topic matters enough to spend fifteen minutes on. Every year, roughly 36 million falls occur among older adults in the United States alone. About one in five results in a serious injury. And the fear of falling — even in people who haven't fallen — leads to activity restriction, social isolation, depression, and accelerated physical decline.

But here's what I want you to take away from this video. Balance loss after sixty is not a single problem with a single cause. It's a three-system challenge — vestibular, visual, proprioceptive — that's often compounded by medications. And because it's multi-factorial, the solution is also multi-factorial. There's no single pill, no single exercise, no single fix. But there IS a systematic approach. Identify which systems are contributing. Address what's addressable — vision, medications, BPPV, deconditioning. And keep moving.

[PAUSE — direct to camera]

If you take nothing else from this, take this: the next time you stumble, don't just tell yourself you weren't paying attention. Pay attention. Your body is giving you information. The question is whether you have the framework to interpret it.

[B-ROLL: Warm shot of Dr. Kane, slightly off-center framing]

If this was helpful — and if you've been dismissing balance changes as "just aging" — I'd genuinely love to keep you in the loop. I put out new videos every week that help you understand what's happening inside your body after sixty. Subscribe if you haven't already.

Next week, I'm covering something that causes a lot of anxiety for people over sixty — hand tremors. What's actually causing them, how to tell the difference between something benign and something that needs attention, and why most hand shaking is NOT what people fear it is. You won't want to miss that one.

I'll see you in the next one.
