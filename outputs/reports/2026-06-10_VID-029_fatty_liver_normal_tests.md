Your liver could be accumulating fat right now. That fat could be causing inflammation. ### [SECTION 1: WHAT YOUR LIVER ACTUALLY DOES — AND WHY FAT CHANGES EVERYTHING]

Before I explain why the test fails, you need to understand what we're talking about when we say "fatty liver." Because this isn't about weight. This isn't about diet alone. And this isn't a cosmetic issue.

Your liver is the largest internal organ — about three pounds, sitting under your right rib cage. It processes every nutrient you absorb from your gut. It manufactures proteins your blood needs to clot. It filters toxins, metabolizes medications, stores energy, produces bile for digestion, and regulates cholesterol. When I say it does over 500 distinct biochemical jobs, I'm not exaggerating — I'm rounding down.

[GRAPHIC: Liver anatomy with key functions labeled — filtration, protein synthesis, bile production, glycogen storage, drug metabolism]

Now, a healthy liver contains some fat — up to about 5 percent of its weight is normal. But when fat exceeds that threshold — when fat droplets start accumulating inside liver cells themselves — the organ's function begins to change. Not dramatically at first. Not in ways you'd feel. But the biochemistry shifts.

This condition is called non-alcoholic fatty liver disease — NAFLD. And in adults over 60, it's astonishingly common. Studies published in *Hepatology* in 2024 estimate that between 25 and 35 percent of adults over 60 have some degree of fatty liver. That's roughly 1 in 3 people watching this video.

[ON-SCREEN: "25-35% of adults over 60 have fatty liver" — with citation: Hepatology 2024]

Here's what makes NAFLD particularly insidious after 60: it's not just about the fat. The fat triggers inflammation. Inflammation triggers fibrosis — scar tissue that replaces healthy liver cells. Fibrosis can progress to cirrhosis over years or decades. And cirrhosis significantly increases the risk of liver cancer.

But — and this is the critical point — you can have significant fat accumulation, even early fibrosis, and your standard liver blood tests will read completely normal.

Let me explain why.

---

### [SECTION 2: THE TEST THAT LIES — WHY ALT AND AST MISS 70-80% OF CASES]

When doctors want to check your liver, they order what's called a liver function panel. The key numbers are ALT (alanine aminotransferase) and AST (aspartate aminotransferase). These are enzymes that live inside liver cells. When liver cells are damaged or die, ALT and AST leak into the bloodstream, and the levels rise.

So the logic seems sound: if the liver is damaged, the enzymes go up. If the enzymes are normal, the liver is fine.

[LEAN IN]

Except that logic has a fundamental flaw. ALT and AST measure liver cell damage — specifically, they measure liver cells that are dying or dead. They do not measure fat. They do not measure inflammation that hasn't yet killed cells. And they absolutely do not measure fibrosis — scar tissue that forms without killing the cells around it.

[GRAPHIC: Three panels — (1) Healthy liver: normal enzymes, (2) Fatty liver with inflammation: normal enzymes, (3) Liver cell death: elevated enzymes]

Think of it this way. Imagine a house where the foundation is slowly cracking. The walls are shifting. The structure is compromised. But the house hasn't collapsed yet. If your only test for structural integrity was "is the house standing?" — you'd say it passes. Every time. Right up until the day it doesn't.

That's what ALT and AST measure. They measure whether liver cells are dying right now. They do not measure whether your liver is fatty. They do not measure whether fibrosis is building. And they do not measure whether the organ's function is declining.

A landmark study published in *Hepatology* — the journal of the American Association for the Study of Liver Diseases — found that between 70 and 80 percent of patients with confirmed NAFLD (confirmed by imaging or biopsy, not blood tests) had ALT and AST levels within the normal range.

[ON-SCREEN: "70-80% of NAFLD patients have NORMAL ALT/AST" — Hepatology, AASLD]

Read that again. Four out of five people with fatty liver disease — confirmed by the gold standard — were told their liver tests were normal. Not because the test was wrong. Because the test was measuring the wrong thing.

And here's what makes this even more relevant after 60: as we age, our liver cells actually become more resistant to damage. The same amount of fat and inflammation that might cause enzyme elevation in a 40-year-old can produce completely normal readings in a 60-year-old. Your liver gets quieter as it gets sicker.

---

### [SECTION 3: THE TEST THAT ACTUALLY WORKS — FIB-4 AND FIBROSCAN]

So if ALT and AST can't be trusted, what can you do? This is where it gets practical, because there are tools that work — they're just not part of the standard panel.

The first is something you can calculate today, with blood work you may already have. It's called the FIB-4 score.

[GRAPHIC: FIB-4 formula displayed — Age × AST / (Platelets × √ALT)]

FIB-4 stands for Fibrosis-4. It's a simple calculation that uses four values: your age, your AST, your ALT, and your platelet count. You already know your age. AST, ALT, and platelets are all part of standard blood panels — the complete metabolic panel and complete blood count that your doctor orders every year.

The FIB-4 score was developed and validated across multiple large studies, including a 2024 analysis in *Hepatology* that confirmed its accuracy in predicting liver fibrosis in adults over 60. Unlike ALT alone, which only measures cell death, the FIB-4 score captures the relationship between liver enzymes, platelet count (which drops as fibrosis progresses because the spleen enlarges), and age (because fibrosis risk accumulates over time).

Here's how to interpret it:

- **FIB-4 below 1.3**: Low probability of significant fibrosis. Your liver is likely okay.
- **FIB-4 between 1.3 and 2.67**: Intermediate range. This is the gray zone that needs further testing.
- **FIB-4 above 2.67**: High probability of significant fibrosis. This warrants imaging and possibly a specialist referral.

[ON-SCREEN: FIB-4 scoring thresholds with color coding — green/yellow/red]

Now, here's the practical part. You don't need to convince your doctor to order exotic tests. You need to ask them to calculate a number from tests you've already had. Say this:

*"I've been reading about NAFLD and the FIB-4 score. Could you calculate my FIB-4 from my recent blood work? I'd like to know if my liver enzymes tell the full story."*

That's it. That's the conversation. You're not demanding. You're not self-diagnosing. You're asking your doctor to interpret existing data through a more accurate lens.

If your FIB-4 is in the intermediate or high range, the next step is imaging. And this is where the second tool comes in: FibroScan.

[GRAPHIC: FibroScan device and procedure — ultrasound-based, non-invasive, 10-minute exam]

FibroScan is a specialized ultrasound that measures liver stiffness — which correlates directly with fibrosis — and also measures fat content using something called the CAP score (controlled attenuation parameter). It takes about 10 minutes. It's non-invasive. It's painless. And it's far more accurate than standard ultrasound for detecting fatty liver.

A standard abdominal ultrasound can detect moderate to severe fat accumulation, but it misses mild-to-moderate NAFLD in up to 30 percent of cases. FibroScan catches what standard ultrasound misses.

The challenge is availability. Not every primary care office has a FibroScan. But many gastroenterology and hepatology practices do, and some larger primary care networks are starting to offer them. If your FIB-4 score is elevated, asking for a FibroScan referral is a reasonable, evidence-based next step.

### [SECTION 4: WHY THIS MATTERS MORE AFTER 60 — THE COMPOUNDING EFFECT]

I want to be clear about something. NAFLD is not a death sentence. In fact, in its early stages, it's reversible. But after 60, the stakes are higher for three specific reasons.

First, you have less time to catch it. Fibrosis progresses slowly — often over 15 to 20 years. If fatty liver begins at 50 and isn't detected until 70, you may already have significant scarring by the time it's found. Early detection in your 60s gives you the window to intervene before irreversible damage occurs.

[ON-SCREEN: Timeline graphic — "Age 50: Fat begins → Age 60: Inflammation → Age 70: Fibrosis → Age 80: Cirrhosis risk"]

Second, your liver is already processing more medications. After 60, the average adult takes four or more prescription medications. Every one of those is metabolized by your liver. A fatty, inflamed liver metabolizes drugs less predictably — which means medication side effects can increase, drug interactions become more likely, and dosing that worked for years may suddenly be too high.

Third — and this is the connection most people miss — NAFLD is not just a liver problem. It's a cardiovascular risk factor. Multiple large studies, including a 2024 meta-analysis in the *Journal of Hepatology*, have confirmed that NAFLD independently increases the risk of heart attack and stroke, even after controlling for diabetes, obesity, and cholesterol. The fatty liver doesn't just sit there. It produces inflammatory molecules that circulate through your entire vascular system.

[GRAPHIC: Liver → inflammatory markers → blood vessels → heart/brain — showing systemic connection]

This is why I'm so passionate about this topic from a vascular medicine perspective. When I see a patient with unexplained cardiovascular risk — someone whose cholesterol is controlled, whose blood pressure is managed, but who keeps having vascular events — one of the first things I look at is the liver. Because the liver may be the missing piece of the puzzle.

### [SECTION 5: WHAT ACTUALLY HELPS — BEYOND "LOSE WEIGHT"]

If you've been diagnosed with NAFLD — or if your FIB-4 score suggests you might have it — what do you actually do?

The standard advice is "lose weight and exercise." And while that's not wrong, it's incomplete. Let me give you the specifics that actually move the needle.

**The single most effective intervention for NAFLD is weight loss of 7-10 percent of body weight.** Not 20 percent. Not getting to your college weight. A 2024 study in *Gastroenterology* found that losing just 7 percent of body weight reduced liver fat by over 40 percent and improved inflammation markers significantly. For a 200-pound person, that's 14 pounds. Achievable.

But here's what's equally important: how you lose the weight matters. Rapid weight loss — crash diets, extreme calorie restriction — can actually worsen NAFLD by flooding the liver with free fatty acids from rapid fat breakdown. The goal is gradual, sustained loss. One to two pounds per week.

[ON-SCREEN: "7-10% weight loss → 40%+ liver fat reduction" — Gastroenterology 2024]

**Exercise independently reduces liver fat, even without weight loss.** A 2023 study in *Hepatology* showed that 150 minutes per week of moderate exercise — brisk walking counts — reduced liver fat by 20-30 percent even in participants who didn't lose any weight. The mechanism: exercise improves insulin sensitivity in liver cells, which reduces the liver's fat production.

**Coffee — and I mean actual coffee, not sugary coffee drinks — is protective.** Multiple studies have shown that drinking 2-3 cups of coffee per day is associated with lower liver fibrosis scores. A 2024 analysis confirmed this specifically in adults over 60. The protective compounds are thought to be cafestol and kahweol, which have anti-fibrotic properties. Black or with a small amount of milk is fine. The five-dollar caramel frappuccino doesn't count.

[ON-SCREEN: "2-3 cups coffee/day → lower fibrosis risk" — with coffee cup graphic]

**Avoid fructose-sweetened beverages.** This is different from the fructose in whole fruit. The fructose in soda, sweetened juices, and processed foods is processed almost entirely by the liver, and in excess, it directly contributes to liver fat production. A 2024 study in *Diabetes Care* found that replacing one sugary drink per day with water or unsweetened tea reduced liver fat by 15 percent over six months.

**Get your vitamin E levels checked.** This is one that most doctors don't mention. Vitamin E — specifically alpha-tocopherol — has been shown in clinical trials to improve liver histology in NAFLD patients. A blood test can determine if you're deficient. If you are, supplementation under medical supervision may help. But don't mega-dose vitamin E on your own — at high doses, it can increase bleeding risk, especially if you're on blood thinners.

### [CLOSING — THE CONVERSATION THAT CHANGES NOTHING AND EVERYTHING]

I want to close with this thought. The most dangerous health conditions are not the ones that announce themselves with pain or obvious symptoms. They're the ones that progress silently, behind a wall of "normal" test results, while the clock keeps ticking.

NAFLD is one of those conditions. It affects up to a third of people over 60. It can progress to fibrosis, cirrhosis, and liver cancer. It increases your risk of heart attack and stroke. And the standard test your doctor orders to check for it misses 70 to 80 percent of cases.

But here's the good news. Unlike many conditions we discuss on this channel, NAFLD in its early stages is reversible. The liver is one of the few organs that can regenerate. If you catch it early — if you look past the "normal" label and ask the right questions — you can change the trajectory.

So here's what I'd like you to do. Not tomorrow. Not next week. Today, or the next time you have access to your blood work. Look at your AST, ALT, and platelet count. Calculate your FIB-4 score — there are free calculators online, or ask your doctor to do it. If it's above 1.3, ask about a FibroScan. If it's above 2.67, ask for a referral to a liver specialist.

You're not being difficult. You're not being a hypochondriac. You're being a patient who understands that "normal" is sometimes just the beginning of the conversation, not the end of it.

I'm Dr. Kane. If this was helpful, I'd genuinely appreciate it if you'd subscribe — I put out new videos every week that help you understand what's actually happening inside your body after 60. And if you want to go deeper on how your liver connects to your heart health, I'm covering that next.

Take care of your liver. It's doing over 500 things for you right now. The least you can do is make sure the test you're relying on is actually measuring what matters.
