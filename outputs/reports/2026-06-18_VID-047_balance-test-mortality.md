In 2024, a study was published in the British Medical Journal that should have been front-page news. It wasn't. But it should have been.

Researchers followed over 2,000 adults between the ages of 51 and 75. They asked each of them to do exactly what you just did — stand on one leg for ten seconds. No holding onto anything. Eyes open. Just balance.

And then they followed those people for the next seven years.

The results were striking. The people who couldn't hold the position for ten seconds? They were nearly twice as likely to die from any cause during the follow-up period compared to the people who could.

[ON-SCREEN: Key statistic — "2x all-cause mortality risk if unable to hold 10 seconds"]

Twice the risk. From a ten-second balance test.

Now, I want to be very clear about something. The test isn't *causing* anything. Standing on one leg doesn't make you live longer. The test is a mirror. It reflects something about the systems inside your body that keep you alive.

And that's what I want to talk about today. Because understanding *why* this test predicts mortality is more useful than the test itself.

[SHIFT — teaching mode, warm but precise]

Here's what most people think the balance test measures: balance. Your ability to not fall over. And that's... partially true. But it's like saying a stress test measures your ability to walk on a treadmill. Technically yes. But that's not why it matters.

The single-leg stance test is powerful because it simultaneously stresses four completely separate body systems. And all four have to work together, in real time, for you to stay upright.

[ON-SCREEN: Four systems appearing one by one]

System one: your vestibular system. That's the fluid-filled canals in your inner ear that tell your brain which way is up. They're like a biological gyroscope. When you stand on one leg, your vestibular system detects the subtle sway and sends correction signals to your muscles.

System two: your proprioception. These are sensors in your joints, your ankles, your feet, that tell your brain where your body is in space without looking. When you're on one leg, the sensors in your standing ankle are firing constantly, making micro-adjustments you're not even aware of.

System three: your visual system. Your eyes are constantly feeding information to your brain about your position relative to the room. When you close your eyes on one leg, you're removing an entire input channel. That's why it's harder with your eyes closed.

And system four — the one nobody talks about: your cerebral blood flow regulation.

[LEAN IN — this is the key insight]

This is the vascular piece. And it's the reason I wanted to make this video.

When you stand on one leg, your blood pressure changes. Gravity pulls blood downward. Your heart has to work a little harder to push blood up to your brain. And your brain — which is the most oxygen-hungry organ in your body — has to maintain its blood supply within a very narrow range.

The system that does this is called the baroreflex. It's a set of pressure sensors in your neck and chest that detect blood pressure changes and adjust your heart rate and blood vessel tone in real time. When you stand on one leg, the baroreflex is working. When you stand on one leg and your baroreflex is degraded, your brain gets slightly less blood. You sway. You wobble. You put your foot down.

[PAUSE]

And here's the connection to mortality. A degraded baroreflex doesn't just affect your balance. It affects your brain's ability to regulate blood flow during every activity — standing up, walking, climbing stairs, getting out of bed. It's a marker of autonomic nervous system decline. And autonomic decline is one of the strongest predictors of cardiovascular death in seniors.

[ON-SCREEN: "The baroreflex: your brain's blood pressure thermostat"]

So when you fail the ten-second test, you might think, "I need to practice balance." And you'd be partially right. But what you really need is to understand *which* of the four systems is failing. Because the fix is different for each one.

[SHIFT — practical, specific]

Let me walk you through what typically declines after 60.

The vestibular system loses about 40 percent of its hair cells by age 70. That's the inner ear sensors that detect motion. Fewer cells means less precise signals. You sway more because your brain is getting a fuzzier picture of where you are.

Proprioception declines because the sensors in your joints become less sensitive. Ankle proprioception — which is the most important for single-leg balance — declines about 25 percent between ages 40 and 70. Your brain is getting less information from the very joint that's doing the most work.

Vision changes are obvious — presbyopia, reduced contrast sensitivity, slower adaptation to light changes. But the visual contribution to balance is often underestimated. Up to 70 percent of the sensory information your brain uses to maintain balance comes from your eyes and your vestibular system combined.

And the baroreflex? Baroreceptor sensitivity declines about 30 to 50 percent after age 60. The pressure sensors in your neck become stiffer, less responsive. Your brain's blood pressure thermostat becomes sluggish.

[ON-SCREEN: Four-system decline summary with percentages]

So when you stand on one leg and you can't make it to ten seconds, you're not just "bad at balance." You're revealing which of these four systems has declined the most. And that tells you exactly what to work on.

[PAUSE — transition to norms]

Now, what should you be able to do at your age?

The BMJ study established these norms: if you're in your 60s, you should be able to hold single-leg stance for at least 8 seconds. In your 70s, at least 5 seconds. In your 80s, at least 3 seconds.

These are averages. Some people do better. Some do worse. But if you're significantly below these thresholds — if you're 65 and can't make it past 3 seconds — that's worth discussing with your doctor. Not because the test is diagnostic, but because it's a signal that one or more of your four systems needs attention.

[LEAN IN — the "what to do" section]

So what do you do if you failed the test?

First: don't panic. The test is a snapshot, not a sentence. The beautiful thing about all four of these systems is that they respond to training. Even after 60. Even after 70.

Second: identify which system is the weakest link. Here's a quick self-assessment.

[ON-SCREEN: Self-assessment flow chart]

Try the test again, but this time, close your eyes. If you can do it with eyes open but not eyes closed, your proprioceptive and vestibular systems are doing okay — it's your visual dependence that's the issue. That's actually the most common pattern.

If you struggle even with eyes open, try it near a wall — just knowing the wall is there. If that helps significantly, your vestibular system may be the primary issue.

If you feel dizzy or lightheaded when you stand on one leg — not just unsteady, but actually dizzy — that's a baroreflex or blood flow issue. Mention that to your doctor. Ask about orthostatic blood pressure testing.

Third: train the system that needs it.

[DEMONSTRATE — Dr. Kane shows exercises]

For vestibular training: stand on one leg while turning your head side to side. Sounds simple. It's not. This forces your vestibular system to maintain balance while processing conflicting motion signals. Start with 10 seconds per side. Build to 30.

For proprioception: stand on a folded towel instead of a flat floor. The unstable surface forces your ankle sensors to work harder. When that gets easy, close your eyes on the towel. That's advanced.

For baroreflex and cerebral blood flow: this is where it gets interesting. The best exercise for baroreflex sensitivity is — and this might surprise you — slow, deep breathing. Six breaths per minute, in through the nose, out through the mouth. Five minutes, twice a day. A 2023 study in the Journal of Hypertension showed that this specific breathing pattern improved baroreflex sensitivity by 25 percent in seniors after eight weeks.

[ON-SCREEN: Breathing protocol — 6 breaths/min, 5 min, 2x daily]

And for general balance: the single-leg stance test *is* the exercise. Practice it. Every day. Near a wall if you need to. Thirty seconds per leg. Build up.

[SHIFT — warm close]

I had a patient — a woman, 78, former teacher — who came to me after a fall. Nothing broken, but she was shaken. She told me she'd been afraid of falling for years. She'd stopped going to the grocery store. She'd stopped visiting friends. She was, in her words, "waiting to fall."

When I tested her single-leg stance, she couldn't make it past two seconds. Eyes open, holding the table.

We worked on all four systems. Vestibular exercises. Proprioception training. Breathing for her baroreflex. And daily single-leg practice — starting with the wall, then a chair, then nothing.

Three months later, she held it for eleven seconds.

She called me the next week. She'd gone to the grocery store. Alone. She said, "I walked up the ramp and I didn't hold the railing. I didn't even think about it."

[PAUSE]

That's what this test measures. Not just balance. Independence. Confidence. The ability to move through the world without fear.

You don't need to be perfect. You don't need to hold it for a minute. You need to hold it long enough that your four systems are communicating. That your brain is getting the blood it needs. That your body trusts itself.

Ten seconds. That's the threshold. And it's trainable.
