Tell me if this sounds familiar.

Your doctor prescribes a new medication. You take it every day, same time, exactly as directed. And for the first few months, it works. You can tell because the numbers improve, or the symptoms ease, or you just feel better.

And then, slowly — not overnight — it stops working.

Not dramatically. You don't crash. But the benefit fades. The numbers creep back up. The symptoms return, maybe a little different than before. And your doctor's response, understandably, is to increase the dose. Or switch to a different drug in the same class.

[PAUSE]

I had a patient — I'll call her Margaret — who went through this exact cycle with her thyroid medication. Levothyroxine. One of the most prescribed drugs in the country. She'd been on the same dose for three years, and her TSH was beautifully controlled. Then, over about six months, her TSH started rising. Her doctor increased the dose. It came back down for a few weeks, then crept up again. Dose increased again. Same pattern.

Margaret was understandably frustrated. She told me, "I'm taking the exact same pill, at the exact same time. Why is it suddenly not working?"

And the answer — which took us about twenty minutes to figure out — wasn't in her thyroid. It was in her stomach.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "When a medication stops working — the answer might be in your stomach"]

I'm Dr. Kane. I'm a vascular medicine physician. And today I want to talk about something that affects the majority of adults over sixty but that almost nobody discusses: the dramatic decline in stomach acid production that happens as you age — and what that quietly does to the medications you're depending on.

This isn't about whether your drugs are "good" or "bad." This is about whether your body can actually absorb them. And after sixty, the answer to that question changes more than most people realize.

---

**PART 1: THE STOMACH IS NOT JUST A BAG**

[GRAPHIC: Animated cross-section of the stomach — showing parietal cells, chief cells, the mucus layer, and the acid pool]

Let me give you the anatomy quickly, because the anatomy is where the story starts.

Your stomach lining contains several types of cells, but the ones we need to talk about today are called parietal cells. These are the cells that produce hydrochloric acid — HCl — the acid that gives your stomach its famously low pH.

In a healthy young adult, the pH of your stomach sits between 1.5 and 3.5. That's remarkably acidic. For reference, that's approaching the pH of battery acid. And your stomach is designed to handle that — it has a thick mucus lining that protects the tissue from its own acid production.

Why does your stomach need to be that acidic? Some reasons are obvious — breaking down food, killing bacteria. But there's another reason that doesn't get discussed enough: acid is the activator for almost every oral medication you take.

Here's what I mean. Many medications are formulated with enteric coatings — a special polymer shell that's designed to resist dissolving in acid. That coating is there for a reason. Maybe the drug would damage your stomach lining. Maybe your stomach acid would destroy the drug before it could be absorbed. So the medication is engineered to pass through the stomach intact and dissolve in the more alkaline environment of your small intestine.

That's the design. Your stomach is the lock. The acid is the key. And when the acid level drops... the key doesn't work anymore.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Enteric coating = acid-resistant shell → dissolves in small intestine, NOT stomach"]

[PAUSE — let that mechanism settle]

But it's not just enteric-coated medications. Acid also affects drug solubility — the degree to which a pill dissolves into a form your intestinal wall can absorb. Some drugs need an acidic environment to break down from their solid form into a soluble ionic form. Without enough acid, those drugs pass through your GI trail partially or completely undissolved. You took the pill. It went through your system. But the active compound never actually got into your bloodstream.

That's the mystery Margaret was living with. And it's the mystery that explains why so many medications seem to "stop working" in older adults.

---

**PART 2: THE 40 PERCENT DECLINE**

[ON-SCREEN: "The ~40% Drop — What Happens to Stomach Acid After 60"]

Here's the scope of the problem.

Research published in *Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology* and confirmed by multiple subsequent studies shows that gastric acid secretion declines substantially with age. By the time you reach your sixties, your stomach is producing roughly 40 percent less hydrochloric acid than it did in your thirties. In some studies, the decline is even greater.

Forty percent. That's not a subtle shift.

The mechanism is straightforward. You lose parietal cells as you age — the stomach lining atrophies slowly, similar to how other tissues thin over time. A condition called atrophic gastritis — chronic inflammation and thinning of the stomach lining — affects an estimated 25 to 30 percent of adults over sixty. And even in people who don't meet the clinical threshold for atrophic gastritis, parietal cell mass and acid output decline meaningfully.

The result: your stomach pH rises. Instead of 1.5 to 2.5, it may rise to 3.5, 4, or even higher. Your stomach becomes a less acidic, less effective environment for dissolving and activating medications.

[GRAPHIC: Side-by-side — young stomach (pH ~1.5) vs. aging stomach (pH ~3.5-4.0) with pill dissolving animation]

Now, for most drugs, this pH shift doesn't matter much. They're formulated to dissolve across a wide pH range. But for specific categories of medications, even a modest pH increase can significantly reduce absorption. And these are not obscure drugs. Some of them are among the most commonly prescribed medications in the country.

Let me walk you through the ones that concern me most.

---

**PART 3: THE DRUGS THAT ARE QUIETLY FAILING**

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "5 Drug Classes Affected by Low Stomach Acid After 60"]

**Drug one: Levothyroxine — thyroid medication.**

This is the Margaret drug. Levothyroxine — Synthroid, Levoxyl, the generic — is one of the top five most prescribed medications in the United States. It's the standard treatment for hypothyroidism, which affects roughly 10 to 15 percent of adults over sixty, with higher rates in women.

Levothyroxine absorption is acid-dependent. The tablet needs an acidic environment to dissolve properly. A 2018 study in *Thyroid* found that patients with reduced gastric acid absorption had significantly lower levels of absorbed T4 despite taking the same dose. The researchers specifically linked the absorption deficit to elevated gastric pH.

This is so well-documented that the FDA has required specific labeling on levothyroxine products noting absorption variability. But in practice, when a patient's TSH creeps up, most doctors increase the dose before they ever consider absorption as the issue. The patient ends up on a higher dose than they actually need — which can cause symptoms of hyperthyroidism, including palpitations and bone loss — when the real fix is addressing the timing of the dose relative to when acid production is highest.

[PAUSE]

**Drug two: Calcium supplements — specifically calcium carbonate.**

This is a big one. Calcium carbonate — the most common and least expensive form of calcium supplement — requires stomach acid to convert it into an absorbable ionic form. No acid, no conversion, no absorption.

This is not theoretical. A landmark study published in the *New England Journal of Medicine* by Dr. Robert Recker at Creighton University demonstrated that patients with low stomach acid absorbed significantly less calcium from calcium carbonate compared to those with normal acid levels. The study concluded that calcium carbonate "is poorly absorbed in individuals with achlorhydria" — complete absence of stomach acid — and that "absorption is reduced in those with low acid output."

For adults over sixty trying to prevent osteoporosis — already at elevated risk for fractures — taking calcium carbonate that their body can't absorb is not just useless. It's giving them a false sense of security. They think they're protecting their bones. They're swallowing a pill that's passing through undissolved.

There's an alternative — calcium citrate, which doesn't require acid for absorption — which I'll come back to in a moment. But the reason this matters extends beyond calcium to every mineral supplement that depends on acid for solubility.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Calcium Carbonate → Needs Acid | Calcium Citrate → Acid Not Required"]

[BRIEF PAUSE — visual transition]

**Drug three: Iron supplements.**

Similar mechanism to calcium carbonate. Ferrous sulfate — the most common iron supplement — requires an acidic gastric environment for optimal absorption. The iron needs to be converted from its ferric form (Fe3+) to its ferrous form (Fe2+) for absorption in the duodenum, and that conversion is acid-dependent.

A 2022 meta-analysis in *Nutrients* reviewed 12 studies on iron absorption in older adults and found that reduced gastric acid was one of the primary factors contributing to poor iron absorption in seniors — independent of dietary intake. People were eating iron-rich foods and taking supplements, and their iron levels were still dropping, because the acid needed to liberate and convert the iron simply wasn't there.

[ON-SCREEN: "Iron + Low Acid = Poor Absorption → Fatigue Attributed to Aging, Not Deficiency"]

**Drug four: Certain anticoagulants and cardiovascular medications.**

This is where my background in vascular medicine makes me particularly attentive. Several cardiovascular drugs have pH-dependent absorption profiles.

Rivaroxaban — Xarelto, a commonly prescribed anticoagulant for atrial fibrillation and blood clot prevention — has a formulation component (the drug is formulated with a solubilizing agent) whose absorption is reduced when gastric pH is elevated. The prescribing information itself notes that co-administration with drugs that increase gastric pH (like proton pump inhibitors) can reduce rivaroxaban absorption.

I know what some of you might be thinking: "Wait, aren't you about to tell me to stop taking my acid reducer?" No. I'm about to tell you to have a conversation with your doctor about which of your medications need acid for absorption — because proton pump inhibitors themselves are one of the most commonly prescribed drug classes in older adults, and they dramatically reduce stomach acid production. When you layer a PPI on top of already-declining acid production, you can drop absorption of certain medications to near zero.

[PAUSE]

That's not a reason to stop your PPI. For some people, that medication is protecting them from serious esophageal or gastric damage. It IS a reason to review your full medication list with your doctor and ask: "Which of these drugs need stomach acid to work?"

**Drug five: Certain antifungal and antiviral medications.**

Itraconazole — a commonly used antifungal — and atazanavir — an antiviral — both require an acidic gastric environment for adequate absorption. This is well-documented in pharmacology literature. For older adults being treated for fungal infections or who are on complex antiviral regimens, low stomach acid can convert a therapeutic dose into a subtherapeutic one.

---

**PART 4: THE PPIs — A COMPLICATION WORTH NAMING**

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "PPIs: Protecting Your Stomach, Weakening Your Other Medications"]

I need to address this directly, because it's important and I don't want to create anxiety where none is warranted.

Proton pump inhibitors — drugs like omeprazole, esomeprazole, pantoprazole — are among the most prescribed medications in the world. They work by blocking the final step of acid production in parietal cells. They're extraordinarily effective at reducing stomach acid. For people with GERD, peptic ulcers, or Barrett's esophagus, they can be necessary and protective.

But here's the cascade I want you to understand.

You start with age-related acid decline. Maybe your stomach acid is already down 30 or 40 percent. Then you add a PPI — which can reduce acid production by 90 to 95 percent beyond baseline. Now your gastric pH may be 5 or 6, compared to the normal 1.5 to 2.5.

At that pH, the enteric coatings on medications don't dissolve in the right place. The drugs that need acid for solubility barely dissolve at all. The mineral supplements pass through untouched. And the medications that were formulated based on the assumption of normal gastric pH are being delivered in a body that no longer matches their design specifications.

[GRAPHIC: Cascade diagram — Normal acid → Age decline → PPI added → Near-zero acid → Multiple medications fail]

This doesn't mean PPIs are bad. It means that if you're on a PPI, you need to have a more intentional conversation with your doctor about which of your other medications — and supplements — might be affected.

In my practice, I've seen patients whose calcium supplements were essentially inert because they were on a PPI. They thought they were protecting their bones. They were paying for pills that were passing through their GI tract untouched.

The conversation I have is always the same: "Let's look at everything you're taking together. Let's identify which ones need acid. And let's adjust either the formulation or the timing so you're actually getting the benefit."

---

**PART 5: WHAT YOU CAN ACTUALLY DO**

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "What To Discuss With Your Doctor — A Protocol"]

Okay. Here's the practical part. And I want to be specific, because vague advice isn't useful.

**One: Review your medication list for acid-dependent drugs.**

The ones I've identified today — and there are others — should prompt a conversation. Not a demand. Not a self-directed medication change. A conversation. Bring your full medication and supplement list to your doctor and ask: "Do any of these require stomach acid for proper absorption?"

Most electronic medical records don't flag this automatically. It requires a human to cross-reference your medications with the absorption requirements. That's a conversation worth having.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Ask: 'Which of my medications need stomach acid to work?'"]

**Two: Consider timing.**

For medications that are acid-dependent but that you need to keep taking, timing relative to PPI dosing matters. PPIs have a peak effect roughly two hours after ingestion and a duration of action that varies. Taking your acid-dependent medication at a slightly different time from your PMI — or taking it when your natural acid production is highest (typically earlier in the day) — may improve absorption. This is something your doctor or pharmacist can help you optimize.

**Three: Consider alternative formulations.**

Calcium is the clearest example. If you're on a PPI or you know your acid is low, calcium citrate absorbs independently of acid. It's slightly more expensive, but it actually works. Switching from calcium carbonate to calcium citrate is one of the simplest, evidence-based changes you can make.

For thyroid medication, liquid or soft-gel formulations of levothyroxine appear to be less affected by low gastric acid than tablet forms. A 2019 study in *Endocrine* found that liquid levothyroxine maintained consistent absorption even in patients with elevated gastric pH. This is worth discussing with your doctor if your TSH has been fluctuating.

[ON-SCREEN PILL CLOCK]

**Four: Don't supplement blindly.**

If you're over sixty and you started taking calcium carbonate on your own because someone said it's good for bones — consider that it may not be doing what you think. Get your vitamin D levels checked. Discuss calcium formulation with your doctor. And understand that more is not better if none of it is being absorbed.

**Five: If you're experiencing a medication "stopping working" — mention stomach acid.**

When your doctor suggests increasing the dose of a medication, it's completely appropriate to say: "Before we increase the dose, could we check whether I'm absorbing this properly? Could my stomach acid levels be a factor?"

That's not being difficult. That's being an informed patient. And most doctors will respect the question.

[PAUSE — Dr. Kane sits back slightly]

Margaret, the patient I mentioned at the start — her TSH stabilized when we switched her from tablet levothyroxine to the liquid formulation and adjusted the timing of her PPI. No dose increase needed. The medication had been working all along. Her stomach just wasn't activating it anymore.

That's what I want for you. Not lower doses. Not fewer medications. Just the right ones, getting into your body the way they're supposed to.

[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Your medication might NOT be working after 60 — here's why"]

Your stomach acid drops about 40% after age 60. [PAUSE] That matters — because many common medications NEED that acid to dissolve and enter your bloodstream.

Thyroid medication. Calcium supplements. Iron. Even some blood thinners.

If your acid is too low... you can take the pill every single day and get almost zero benefit. The medication passes through your body untouched.

Here's what to do:
- Ask your doctor: "Which of my medications need stomach acid to absorb?"
- If you take calcium — switch to calcium citrate. It absorbs without acid.
- If your thyroid dose keeps creeping up — mention stomach acid before they increase it again.

Taking the medication is only half the equation. Getting it into your body is the other half.
