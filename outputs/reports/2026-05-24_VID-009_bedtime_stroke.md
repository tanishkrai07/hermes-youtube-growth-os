**[SECTION 1: THE 3 AM STROKE PROBLEM]**

[ON-SCREEN: "Most strokes happen between 10 PM and 6 AM"]

When you sleep, your body does something remarkable.

Your blood pressure drops.

This is called "nocturnal dipping" — and it's supposed to happen. Your systolic blood pressure should fall by 10 to 20 percent during sleep. This dip is protective. It gives your blood vessels a rest. It allows your brain to clear metabolic waste. It's part of your body's nightly repair cycle.

But here is the problem.

A significant number of seniors — especially those with high blood pressure — don't experience this dip. Their blood pressure stays elevated through the night. Sometimes it even goes UP.

This pattern is called "non-dipping," and it is one of the most dangerous — and most overlooked — risk factors for stroke.

[ON-SCREEN: "Non-dipping: When BP fails to drop 10-20% during sleep"]
[ON-SCREEN: "40% of hypertensive seniors are non-dippers"]

Research published in the journal *Hypertension* — from the American Heart Association — found that non-dippers have a stroke risk 2 to 3 times higher than those who dip normally. A meta-analysis in *Stroke* — the AHA's dedicated stroke journal — confirmed that non-dipping is an independent predictor of stroke, even when daytime blood pressure is well-controlled.

Let me say that again, because it matters.

You can have perfectly controlled blood pressure during the day — take your meds, watch your salt, exercise — and still be at significantly elevated stroke risk if your blood pressure doesn't dip at night.

The standard blood pressure cuff check at your doctor's office? It measures you sitting in a chair at 2 in the afternoon. It tells you almost nothing about what's happening at 3 AM.

[PAUSE]

But the good news — and this is the part that excites me — is that nocturnal dipping is not fixed. It's not genetic destiny. It's influenced by what you do in the hours before bed.

---

**[SECTION 2: THE 4-STEP 90-SECOND BEDTIME ROUTINE]**

[ON-SCREEN: "The 90-Second Bedtime Routine"]

Here is the routine I give every patient. I want you to learn it tonight. Not next week. Tonight.

**Step One: Leg Elevation — 30 seconds**

[ON-SCREEN: Step 1 — Leg Elevation, 30 seconds]

Before you get into bed — while you're still on the edge of the mattress or on the couch — lie on your back and elevate your legs. Place them on a pillow or against the wall so they're above your heart. Hold for 30 seconds.

Why? Because throughout the day, fluid pools in your legs due to gravity. When you lie flat, that fluid rushes back into your circulation. For a healthy person, this is no problem. But for a senior whose blood vessels are stiffer and whose heart is working harder, this sudden fluid shift increases nighttime blood pressure.

Elevating your legs before bed helps your body begin the fluid redistribution process gradually, so it doesn't hit your cardiovascular system all at once when you lie down.

Think of it like opening a valve slowly versus all at once.

[ON-SCREEN: Visual — Person lying on back, legs elevated on pillow]

**Step Two: Hydration Check — 15 seconds**

[ON-SCREEN: Step 2 — Hydration Check, 15 seconds]

Take a small sip of water. About 3 to 4 ounces. Not a full glass — just enough to avoid overnight dehydration.

Here is why this matters. During sleep, you lose water through breathing and perspiration. By morning, you're mildly dehydrated. In seniors, this dehydration increases blood viscosity — the thickness of your blood. Thicker blood flows more slowly, and slow-moving blood is more prone to clotting.

But — and this is important — you also don't want to drink a full glass of water before bed, because that will wake you up to use the bathroom, disrupting your sleep. And disrupted sleep raises blood pressure.

It's a balance. A small sip. Just enough.

[ON-SCREEN: "3-4 oz water — not a full glass"]

**Step Three: Left-Side Positioning — 5 seconds to set up**

[ON-SCREEN: Step 3 — Left-Side Positioning]

Position yourself to begin sleeping on your left side. I know — some of you just groaned. But hear me out.

Your aorta — the largest artery in your body — curves to the left as it descends from your heart. When you sleep on your left side, gravity assists blood flow through this natural curve. It reduces the workload on your heart.

A study published in the *Journal of the American College of Cardiology* found that left-sided sleeping was associated with improved nocturnal blood pressure profiles compared to right-sided or supine sleeping.

Now, I'm not going to tell you that you must sleep on your left side all night. Nobody controls their position during sleep. But starting on your left side increases the chances you'll spend meaningful time in that position during the critical first few hours of sleep — when your body is establishing its dipping pattern.

Five seconds to set up. That's it.

[ON-SCREEN: Visual — Diagram showing aorta curve and left-side sleeping position]

**Step Four: The 4-7-8 Breathing Reset — 40 seconds**

[ON-SCREEN: Step 4 — 4-7-8 Breathing, 40 seconds]

This is the most important step, and it takes the longest. But it's still less than a minute.

It's called the 4-7-8 breathing technique. Here's how it works.

[ON-SCREEN: "Inhale 4 seconds → Hold 7 seconds → Exhale 8 seconds"]

Breathe in through your nose for 4 seconds.

Hold your breath for 7 seconds.

Exhale slowly through your mouth for 8 seconds.

Do this cycle 4 times. Total: 40 seconds.

[PAUSE]

Now, why does a breathing exercise before bed affect stroke risk?

[ON-SCREEN: "Breathing activates the vagus nerve → makes blood vessels more flexible → helps blood pressure dip"]

The vagus nerve is the longest nerve in your body. It runs from your brainstem to your abdomen, and it controls — among many other things — your heart rate and the flexibility of your blood vessels.

When you do slow, controlled exhales, you stimulate the vagus nerve. This sends a signal to your vascular system: relax. Open up. Let the blood flow more easily.

This is called vasodilation, and it directly supports the nocturnal blood pressure dip that protects your brain at night.

A study in the *Journal of the American Heart Association* found that slow breathing exercises before bed significantly improved nocturnal dipping patterns in hypertensive patients — after just two weeks of consistent practice.

Forty seconds. Four cycles. That's all it takes to activate the single most important nerve for your cardiovascular health.

[ON-SCREEN: The Full Routine — 90 Seconds Total]

Let's add it up.

Step 1: Leg elevation — 30 seconds
Step 2: Hydration check — 15 seconds
Step 3: Left-side position — 5 seconds
Step 4: 4-7-8 breathing — 40 seconds

Total: 90 seconds.

[LEAN IN]

Ninety seconds to potentially prevent the most devastating type of stroke. I don't think that's a bad trade.

And here's what I tell every patient: you will not be perfect at this. Some nights you'll forget. Some nights you'll only do the breathing. Some nights you'll fall asleep mid-routine.

That's fine. Consistency over perfection. If you do this 5 nights out of 7, you're making a meaningful difference.

[PAUSE]

Now — I need to be clear about something.

This routine is not a replacement for medication. It's not a substitute for seeing your doctor. If you've been diagnosed with high blood pressure, continue everything your doctor prescribed.

What this IS is an addition. A low-cost, zero-risk supplement to whatever else you're doing.

And honestly? I think it's one of the most important things you can do.

---

**[SECTION 4: HOW TO KNOW IF YOU'RE A NON-DIPPER]**

[ON-SCREEN: "How to Check If You're a Non-Dipper"]

If you want to know whether your blood pressure dips at night, there is a test. It's called ambulatory blood pressure monitoring — or ABPM for short.

It involves wearing a blood pressure cuff for 24 hours that takes automatic readings throughout the day and night. Your doctor can order it.

This is the single best test for detecting non-dipping. And it's dramatically more useful than a single office reading.

[ON-SCREEN: "Ask your doctor about: 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM)"]

I recommend this test for every patient over 60 who has any history of high blood pressure, stroke in the family, or other cardiovascular risk factors.

If you find out you're a non-dipper, the 90-second bedtime routine becomes even more important. And your doctor may adjust your medication timing — some blood pressure medications work better when taken at night rather than in the morning. This is called "chronotherapy," and the research supporting it is strong.

A major study — the Hygia Chronotherapy Trial, published in the *European Heart Journal* — found that taking blood pressure medication at bedtime reduced the risk of major cardiovascular events by 45% compared to taking it in the morning.

But that's a conversation for you and your doctor. I just want you to know the option exists.
