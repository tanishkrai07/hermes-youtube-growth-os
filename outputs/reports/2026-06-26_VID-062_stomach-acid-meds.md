**[COLD OPEN — Direct to camera, conversational]**

**[SECTION 1: THE STOMACH CHEMISTRY SHIFT]**

Let me explain what's actually happening inside your stomach. And I promise — this matters more than you think.

Your stomach lining contains parietal cells. These are the cells that produce hydrochloric acid — the acid that breaks down your food and creates the chemical environment your medications need to work properly.

In your 30s and 40s, these cells are prolific. Your stomach pH — that's the measure of how acidic it is — sits around 1.5. That's roughly the same acidity as battery acid. Strong enough to dissolve metal, strong enough to break down a steak, and strong enough to activate the chemical processes your medications depend on.

**[ON-SCREEN: pH scale animation — 1.5 highlighted, labeled "Normal stomach pH at age 40"]**

But here's what happens after 60. Parietal cell mass declines. Not dramatically overnight — gradually, year by year. By the time you're in your mid-60s, research from Gastroenterology in 2024 shows you may have 30 to 50 percent fewer parietal cells than you did at 40.

And your stomach pH? It rises. From 1.5 to 2.5, sometimes 3.5 or higher.

**[ON-SCREEN: pH scale animation — 3.5 highlighted, label "Common stomach pH after 60"]**

Now, on a pH scale, that might sound like a small change. But pH is logarithmic. A shift from 1.5 to 3.5 means your stomach is roughly 100 times less acidic than it used to be.

One hundred times.

And that change — that quiet, gradual, invisible shift — has real consequences for every single pill you swallow.

**[BEAT — let that land]**

---

**[SECTION 2: THREE WAYS YOUR MEDS ARE FAILING]**

Let me walk you through the three specific mechanisms. Because this isn't theoretical. This is pharmacology.

**[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Mechanism #1: Enteric Coating Failure"]**

**Mechanism number one: enteric coating failure.**

Many medications are designed with an enteric coating — a special outer layer that's supposed to survive the stomach and dissolve in the small intestine instead. This is done deliberately. Either because the drug irritates the stomach lining, or because it needs to be released further down the digestive tract to absorb properly.

That coating is engineered to stay intact at pH levels below 5.5. Above that threshold, it dissolves.

**[ON-SCREEN: Animation showing pill with enteric coating dissolving in stomach vs. intestine]**

When your stomach pH was 1.5, that coating had no problem surviving. But at pH 3.5 or higher — which is common after 60 — the coating can start to break down prematurely. The medication releases in your stomach instead of your intestine. Some of it gets destroyed by whatever acid is still there. Some of it irritates your stomach lining. And less of it reaches your bloodstream.

A 2023 study in the Journal of Pharmaceutical Sciences found that at pH levels above 3.5, enteric-coated formulations can lose 20 to 35 percent of their intended bioavailability. That means you're getting 20 to 35 percent less of the medication you were prescribed.

**[PAUSE]**

Now, mechanism number two.

**[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Mechanism #2: Acid-Dependent Absorption"]**

**Acid-dependent absorption.**

Some medications literally require an acidic environment to be absorbed. They need that low pH to dissolve properly, to break down into the form your intestinal wall can take in.

Levothyroxine — the thyroid medication millions of seniors take every morning — is one of them. It requires gastric acid to dissolve from its tablet form into the absorbable form. At higher pH levels, dissolution is incomplete. You swallow the pill, but your body only absorbs a fraction of it.

Ketoconazole — an antifungal — is even more dependent. Its absorption drops by over 50 percent when gastric pH rises above 3.

And ferrous sulfate — iron supplements — need acidic conditions to convert from the ferric form to the ferrous form that your body can actually use. Without enough acid, you're essentially swallowing expensive chalk.

**[ON-SCREEN: List of acid-dependent drugs with absorption drop percentages]**

A 2024 study in Clinical Pharmacology & Therapeutics found that acid-dependent drug absorption drops 30 to 50 percent at pH levels above 3. That's not a minor reduction. That's the difference between a therapeutic dose and a subtherapeutic one.

**[BEAT]**

And then there's mechanism number three — and this one can actually be dangerous.

**[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Mechanism #3: Extended-Release Dumping"]**

**Extended-release dumping.**

Many medications prescribed to seniors are extended-release formulations. They're designed to release slowly over 12 or 24 hours. This is achieved through a special matrix or membrane that controls the rate of dissolution.

But that rate is calibrated for a specific pH environment. When the pH rises, the dissolution rate can change. Instead of releasing slowly over 12 hours, the matrix breaks down faster. You get a larger dose all at once, followed by a period where there's not enough medication in your system.

This is called "dose dumping." And it means you might experience a spike in drug levels shortly after taking the pill — potentially causing side effects — followed by a trough where the drug level drops too low to be effective.

**[ON-SCREEN: Graph showing normal extended-release curve vs. "dumping" curve with spike and trough]**

For medications like extended-release metformin, certain blood pressure medications, or extended-release pain formulations, this can mean the difference between stable symptom control and unpredictable fluctuations.

**[SECTION 3: THE FIVE DRUG CLASSES MOST AFFECTED]**

Now, let me get specific. Because I know some of you are wondering — does this apply to my medications?

Here are the five drug classes most affected by rising gastric pH after 60.

**[ON-SCREEN: Numbered list appearing as Dr. Kane speaks]**

**Number one: Thyroid medications.** Levothyroxine and liothyronine. If your thyroid levels have been drifting despite consistent dosing, and your doctor keeps adjusting the dose without explaining why — this could be the reason. Ask about checking your gastric pH or whether a liquid or soft-gel formulation might absorb better.

**Number two: Antifungals.** Ketoconazole, itraconazole, fluconazole. These are profoundly acid-dependent. If you're being treated for a fungal infection and it's not responding as expected, absorption may be the issue — not the drug's effectiveness.

**Number three: Iron supplements.** Ferrous sulfate, ferrous gluconate. If you're taking iron for anemia and your levels aren't improving, the problem might not be the iron — it might be your stomach's ability to process it. More on this in a moment.

**Number four: Enteric-coated medications.** This includes enteric-coated aspirin, some NSAIDs, certain proton pump inhibitors themselves — yes, the drugs designed to reduce stomach acid can have reduced efficacy if their enteric coating fails. The irony isn't lost on me.

**[SLIGHT SMILE]**

**Number five: Extended-release formulations.** Any medication with "ER," "XR," "SR," or "CR" after the name. Extended-release metformin, extended-release nifedipine, extended-release morphine — the list is long. If your symptom control has become unpredictable, the release mechanism may be the culprit.

**[SECTION 4: WHY YOUR DOCTOR MISSES THIS]**

Now, you might be wondering — if this is real, why hasn't my doctor mentioned it?

Fair question. And the answer isn't that your doctor doesn't know. It's that this particular issue falls into a gap between specialties.

Your primary care doctor checks your organ function — liver, kidneys, thyroid. They check your drug levels when available. They check for drug interactions. But gastric pH isn't part of a standard workup. There's no routine blood test for it. It doesn't show up in a metabolic panel.

Gastroenterologists can test for it — through a Heidelberg capsule test or gastric analysis — but they're usually evaluating you for ulcers or reflux, not medication absorption.

And pharmacists — who are the real experts on drug absorption — often don't have the full clinical picture of your stomach function.

So the issue falls through the cracks. Your medication isn't working optimally. Your doctor increases the dose. It helps a little. But the underlying problem — the changed stomach chemistry — never gets addressed.

**[PAUSE]**

This is one of those situations where being an informed patient — not a demanding one, an informed one — can genuinely change your care.

**[SECTION 5: THE TIMING PROTOCOL]**

So what can you actually do? Let me give you a practical framework. And I want to be very clear — this is not about changing your medications on your own. This is about having a specific, informed conversation with your doctor.

**[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "The Timing Protocol — Discuss With Your Doctor"]**

Here's what the research suggests about timing and formulation.

**For acid-dependent medications** — like levothyroxine or iron — taking them with a small amount of acidic food or drink can improve absorption. A few ounces of orange juice, or taking the pill with a vitamin C supplement, can temporarily lower gastric pH enough to improve dissolution.

But — and this is important — this doesn't work if you're also taking a proton pump inhibitor. Which brings me to the next point.

**[BEAT]**

**For enteric-coated medications** — these should be taken on an empty stomach, with a full glass of water, and ideally 30 minutes before eating. Food can raise gastric pH further, increasing the risk of premature coating dissolution.

**For extended-release formulations** — consistency is critical. Take them at the same time every day, with the same amount of food or liquid. Variable conditions lead to variable absorption.

And here's one that surprises people: **if you're taking a proton pump inhibitor** — omeprazole, esomeprazole, pantoprazole, lansoprazole — these reduce your stomach acid even further. Which is sometimes necessary. But if you're also taking acid-dependent medications, the PPI can reduce their absorption by an additional 20 to 40 percent.

**[ON-SCREEN: Warning symbol — "PPIs can reduce absorption of other medications by 20-40%"]**

A 2024 analysis in Drug Safety confirmed this interaction across multiple drug classes. If you're on a PPI and your other medications seem less effective, this combination could be why.

**[SECTION 7: THE MAGNESIUM CONNECTION]**

Now, I want to connect this to something else. Because there's a compounding factor that many seniors don't realize.

Proton pump inhibitors — the most commonly prescribed stomach acid reducers — don't just reduce acid. They also reduce magnesium absorption. And magnesium deficiency after 60 is already common due to reduced dietary intake and decreased intestinal absorption.

**[ON-SCREEN: Flow chart — PPI → reduced stomach acid → reduced magnesium absorption → magnesium deficiency → worsened cardiovascular outcomes]**

So you can end up in a situation where the PPI is reducing your stomach acid, which is reducing absorption of your other medications, AND reducing your magnesium levels — which affects your heart, your muscles, your blood pressure.

It's a cascade. And it starts with a medication that many people take without realizing its full systemic effects.

If you're on a PPI and you're also experiencing muscle cramps, irregular heartbeat, or worsening blood pressure control — the magnesium connection is worth discussing with your doctor.

Let me give you your action plan. Three things you can do this week.

**[ON-SCREEN: Checklist appearing item by item]**

**One:** Make a list of your current medications. Note which ones are enteric-coated, extended-release, or known to be acid-dependent. I've covered the main ones in this video, but your pharmacist can help you identify others.

**Two:** Note any symptoms that suggest reduced efficacy. Blood pressure creeping up. Thyroid levels drifting. Pain less controlled. Iron levels not improving. These are the clues.

**Three:** Bring this information to your next doctor's appointment. Use the script I provided. Ask about gastric pH, alternative formulations, or timing adjustments.

**[PAUSE]**

And here's one more thing. If you're taking a proton pump inhibitor, don't stop it abruptly. That can cause a rebound increase in acid production that's worse than before. If you and your doctor decide to adjust it, it needs to be tapered gradually.
