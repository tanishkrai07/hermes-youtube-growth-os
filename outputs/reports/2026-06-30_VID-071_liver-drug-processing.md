### Follow This Pill

[ON-SCREEN: Dr. Kane standing, holding a single pill between thumb and forefinger, looking at it, then looking at camera]

I want you to think about the last medication you took this morning. Whatever it was — a blood pressure pill, a statin, something for your thyroid, a pain reliever.

[BEAT]

You swallowed it. And then you probably didn't think about it again. But your body did. And specifically, your liver did — because that's where most medications go to be processed, broken down, and cleared from your bloodstream.

[ON-SCREEN: Simple animation — pill traveling from stomach → portal vein → liver → bloodstream]

Now here's the thing. If you're over 60, your liver is doing that job differently than it was ten years ago. Not because you're doing anything wrong. Not because your liver is diseased. But because the liver itself — the size, the blood flow, the enzymes — has aged.

[PAUSE]

And the difference is roughly 30 percent.

[B-ROLL: Animation slows down, showing "30% slower" label appearing on the liver stage"

I call it the 30% Rule. And I think every person over 60 who takes any medication should understand it.

---

### Where the 30% Comes From

[ZOOM CUT: Close on Dr. Kane, seated now, conversational]

The 30% isn't one thing. It's three things converging simultaneously. Let me walk you through each — because understanding the "why" is what helps you know which of your medications might be affected.

**First: your liver is physically smaller.**

Not dramatically. Not in a way you'd notice. But a 2024 meta-analysis published in Radiology — looking at thousands of CT scans — found that liver volume decreases by about 20 to 30 percent between age 40 and age 80. Less liver tissue means less capacity to process drugs. It's like a factory that used to have 100 workstations and now has 75. The same orders come in. Fewer stations to fill them.

[ON-SCREEN: Factory analogy — 100 workstations → 75 workstations, same workload]

**Second: less blood reaches your liver.**

The liver receives about 25% of your cardiac output through the portal vein and hepatic artery. After 60, cardiac output declines — and specifically, the portal vein flow that delivers drug-containing blood from your intestines to your liver drops by 30 to 40 percent by age 65, according to a 2023 study in the Journal of Hepatology.

[PAUSE — let this settle]

Think about what that means for your medication. You swallow a pill. It dissolves in your stomach and small intestine. It enters the portal vein, heading for the liver. But the river it's traveling on — the blood flow — is slower now. So the drug arrives at the liver in a different pattern. Sometimes more at once. Sometimes hanging around longer in the bloodstream before it even gets to the liver.

[B-ROLL: River analogy — fast current vs. slow current affecting delivery]

**Third — and this is the most important one: your enzyme engines are running slower.**

Inside your liver cells are enzymes — proteins that chemically transform medications so they can be eliminated. The biggest family of these enzymes is called cytochrome P450. And the workhorse of that family is CYP3A4, which processes roughly 50% of all commonly prescribed medications.

A 2024 study in Clinical Pharmacology & Therapeutics measured CYP3A4 activity across age groups. The finding: adults over 65 had 15 to 30 percent lower CYP3A4 activity compared to adults under 50.

[ON-SCREEN: Age vs. CYP3A4 activity bar chart — declining]

So let's add these up. Smaller liver. Slower delivery. Weaker enzymes. No single change is catastrophic. But combined, they mean your liver processes many medications roughly 25 to 35 percent more slowly after 60.

That's the 30% Rule.

---

### What the 30% Rule Means for You

[Dr. Kane, still seated, now leaning slightly forward]

Here's the practical consequence — and this is the part that matters most:

If your liver processes a drug 30% more slowly, the same dose you were taking at 55 effectively becomes a 30% higher dose at 65.

[ON-SCREEN: "Same pill. Same dose. Effective dose +30%."]

Not because you took more. Because your body cleared less.

[BEAT]

That's why side effects can appear years after you start a medication. It's not that you've developed a "new allergy" or a "new sensitivity." It's that your body has been slowly losing its ability to clear the drug, and the effective concentration in your bloodstream has been gradually climbing.

A 2024 study in JAMA Internal Medicine found that medication-related adverse events are 2.5 times more common in adults over 65 than in younger adults — despite no increase in the number of medications taken. Think about that. Same number of drugs, same doses, 2.5 times the adverse events. The difference isn't the medication. The difference is the liver.

[ZOOM CUT: tight on Dr. Kane's face for emphasis]

---

### The Medications Most Affected

[ON-SCREEN: "5 Drug Classes Where the 30% Rule Matters Most"]

Now, not every medication is equally affected. Some drugs are cleared by the kidneys, not the liver. Some have very wide safety margins where a 30% increase is negligible. But for others, that 30% crosses a threshold.

Here are five categories where age-related liver changes matter most:

[LEAN IN, counting on fingers]

**One: Statins.** Atorvastatin, simvastatin, lovastatin — these are CYP3A4 substrates. As CYP3A4 slows, statin levels in the blood rise. This is one of the reasons muscle aches from statins become more common after 60. It's not "statin intolerance" — it's effective dose creep.

**Two: Certain blood pressure medications.** Amlodipine, nifedipine — these calcium channel blockers are CYP3A4 substrates. Higher effective levels can mean more ankle swelling, more dizziness on standing.

**Three: Some antidepressants.** Sertraline, citalopram — while these have complex metabolic pathways, CYP3A4 plays a role, and dose adjustments are often needed after 60.

**Four: Pain medications.** Oxycodone, tramadol — CYP3A4 and CYP2D6 substrates. This is an area where the 30% Rule has serious safety implications. Higher effective opioid levels mean higher fall risk, higher confusion risk. This is well-documented in the geriatric pharmacology literature.

**Five: Blood thinners.** Warfarin, though it's metabolized by a different CYP enzyme (CYP2C9), is extremely sensitive to any change in liver function because of its narrow therapeutic window. Even small changes in clearance can move a safe INR into a dangerous range.

[PAUSE]

If you're taking something in one of these categories, I'm not telling you to stop. I'm telling you to have a conversation — a specific one.

---

### The Grapefruit Amplifier

[Dr. Kane, conversational, a slight shift to a more everyday tone]

Before we get to that conversation, there's one more thing I want to flag — and it connects directly to the 30% Rule.

You may have heard that grapefruit interacts with some medications. The mechanism is real and worth understanding. Grapefruit contains compounds called furanocoumarins that block CYP3A4 enzymes in the intestinal wall — not the liver, the gut.

[ON-SCREEN: Intestinal wall CYP3A4 being blocked by grapefruit compounds]

When these gut enzymes are blocked, more of the drug passes through the intestine unchanged and reaches the liver in a higher concentration. Under normal circumstances, the liver can handle the extra load. But when your liver is already processing 30% more slowly because of age... the combination is multiplicative.

If grapefruit increases drug absorption by 30-50% (which it can for some CYP3A4 drugs), and your liver is clearing 30% more slowly, the effective dose isn't 30% higher — it can be 60 to 80% higher.

[ON-SCREEN: "Grapefruit + 30% Rule = 60-80% effective dose increase for some drugs"]

This isn't a reason to panic about grapefruit. It's a reason to check whether grapefruit affects your specific medication — and if it does, the interaction matters more after 60 than it did before.

---

### The Doctor Conversation

[Dr. Kane, warm but clear]

If anything in this video resonated — if you've noticed new side effects, or side effects that seem to be getting stronger on the same dose, or if you're on one of the medication classes I mentioned — here's how to bring this up with your doctor.

[ON-SCREEN: "What to Ask Your Doctor"]

"Can we review my medications with my age in mind? I've learned that liver processing changes after 60, and I want to make sure my doses are still appropriate."

That's it. You're not demanding a change. You're requesting a review. And every competent physician will welcome that conversation.

If you're on five or more medications — which, according to CDC data, describes about 40% of adults over 65 — you can also ask specifically: "Can we do a medication reconciliation? I'd like to go through each one and confirm that the dose still makes sense for my current age and liver function."

[PAUSE]

A medication reconciliation is a structured review where you and your doctor go through every medication, every dose, every interaction, and ask: is this still right? It's something that should happen every year after 60, and in my clinical experience, it rarely happens unless the patient initiates it.

---

### One More Thing About Liver Tests

[Dr. Kane, slightly more technical but still clear]

You might be wondering: "Can't I just get a liver test to see if mine is working properly?"

[ZOOM CUT: close, addressing this directly]

The standard liver panel — ALT, AST, bilirubin — measures liver *damage*. It tells you if liver cells are injured or dying. It does not measure liver *capacity* — how well the liver is metabolizing drugs.

[ON-SCREEN: "Liver Damage Tests ≠ Liver Capacity Tests"]

Your liver function tests can be completely normal while your liver is processing medications 30% more slowly. Because the liver isn't sick. It's just older. And there's no routine clinical test for "how quickly does this person's CYP3A4 metabolize medications."

This is why age itself is the most important variable — and why the conversation with your doctor matters more than the lab report.

---

### What You Take Away

[Dr. Kane, settled, looking at camera with the expression of someone giving a gift]

Let me give you the simplest version of everything I just said.

After 60, your liver processes many medications about 30% more slowly. The same dose becomes a higher effective dose in your body. Side effects that appear years after starting a medication often aren't new sensitivities — they're the result of gradual dose creep.

[BEAT]

The fix isn't to stop your medications. It's to have them reviewed. Ask your doctor: "Are my doses still appropriate for my age?" That one question can change your experience of every medication you take.

[LEAN IN, warm]

And if you watched my video about bladder changes after 60 — about the muscle that weakens — this is the other half of that conversation. Because some of the medications most affected by the 30% Rule are the same ones prescribed for overactive bladder. Which means the medication might be contributing to the very symptom it was meant to treat. Not always. But sometimes. And it's worth asking.

[BEAT]

Subscribe if you want to keep understanding what's really happening inside your body after 60. I'll see you next time.

## EXTRACTED SHORT (VID-SHORT-049)

**Title:** "Same Medication, 30% Bigger Dose After 60 — The Liver Rule Nobody Tells You"

After 60, your liver processes medications about 30% more slowly. Same pill. Same dose. But a 30% higher effective dose in your bloodstream.

Three reasons: your liver is physically smaller (20-30% less tissue by age 80), blood flow to your liver drops 30-40%, and the specific enzymes that break down medications — especially CYP3A4, which handles half of all drugs — run 15-30% slower.

This is why side effects can appear years after you start a medication. It's not a new allergy. It's effective dose creep. Your body has been gradually losing clearance capacity, and the drug concentration has been slowly climbing.

The move isn't to stop your medication. It's to ask your doctor: "Are my doses still appropriate for my age?"
