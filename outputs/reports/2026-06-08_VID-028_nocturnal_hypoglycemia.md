### [COLD OPEN — THE BAFFLING PATIENT]

Let me tell you about a patient. I'll call her Miriam — 68 years old, otherwise healthy, comes in for her annual visit. Everything looks good. Standard blood work, all within range. She's managing mild type 2 diabetes with a common medication, takes a beta-blocker for blood pressure, and an ACE inhibitor that her cardiologist added last year.

Then, six months later, she's back. And this time she's worried.

[PAUSE]

"Dr. Kane," she says, "something strange has been happening at night."

She describes waking up around 2 or 3 in the morning — drenched in sweat, heart pounding sometimes confused, the room not quite right. The episodes last twenty to thirty minutes. She'd feel shaky, disoriented, and then... it would pass. She'd fall back asleep.

She'd been to the ER twice. Both times, by the time they checked her, everything was normal. Her EKG was fine. Her blood sugar at the ER — drawn hours after the episodes — was normal. One doctor told her it was probably anxiety. The other suggested menopause.

[PAUSE]

Miriam is not menopausal — she's 68. And she's not anxious. She's experiencing something that affects an estimated 4 to 10 percent of older adults on certain medication combinations, and it's alarmingly underdiagnosed.

She's having nocturnal hypoglycemia. Her blood sugar is dropping to potentially dangerous levels in the middle of the night, and her body's alarm system is broken.

Today, I'm going to explain exactly why this happens, which medication combinations cause it, and how to catch it before the ER misses it — like it missed Miriam. Twice.

### [SECTION 1: WHAT NOCTURNAL HYPOGLYCEMIA ACTUALLY IS]

First, a quick physiology primer so everything that follows makes sense.

Your brain runs on glucose. It consumes about 20 percent of your body's total glucose supply despite being only about 2 percent of your body weight. When blood sugar drops below about 70 milligrams per deciliter, your body activates an emergency response: the adrenal glands release adrenaline, which tells your liver to release stored glucose.

That adrenaline surge causes the symptoms: sweating, rapid heartbeat, trembling, anxiety, confusion. These are your body's emergency signals.

[ON-SCREEN GRAPHIC: Blood glucose threshold chart]
- Normal fasting: 70-100 mg/dL
- Hypoglycemia threshold: <70 mg/dL
- Severe hypoglycemia: <54 mg/dL
- Brain dysfunction: <45 mg/dL

After 60, three changes disrupt this system:

**Change one:** Your insulin response becomes less precise. Aging beta cells are slower to reduce insulin output as blood sugar falls, so insulin may stay elevated overnight.

**Change two:** Your counter-regulatory response weakens. The glucagon signal — your "raise blood sugar" hormone — blunts after 60. Your liver doesn't get the signal as quickly.

**Change three:** Certain medications amplify both of these problems. This is the piece Miriam's ER doctors missed.

---

### [SECTION 2: THE THREE-DRUG INTERACTION THAT MAKES IT SILENT]

Here's what makes this different from typical medication warnings. This isn't about one dangerous drug. It's about a combination where each drug is reasonable individually — but together, they create a silent nighttime threat.

**Drug one: Sulfonylureas**
Glipizide, glyburide, glimepiride. They stimulate your pancreas to produce more insulin. They're effective, decades-old, commonly prescribed. They're also the single most common cause of hypoglycemia in older adults with diabetes — because they stimulate insulin production continuously, even when blood sugar is falling.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: Glipizide | Glyburide | Glimepiride]

Glyburide is the biggest concern. It has a long duration of action, and in older adults with declining kidney function, it accumulates.

**Drug two: Beta-Blockers**
Metoprolol, atenolol, propranolol, carvedilol. Prescribed for blood pressure, heart rate control, post-heart attack, atrial fibrillation. Extremely common after 60.

[LEAN IN]

Here's the critical piece: beta-blockers **mask** the symptoms of hypoglycemia.

The adrenaline surge I described — sweating, rapid heartbeat, trembling — those are mediated by beta-adrenergic receptors. Beta-blockers block those receptors. So when Miriam's blood sugar drops to 55 at 3 AM, her body tries to sound the adrenaline alarm, but the beta-blocker mutes it.

[ON-SCREEN GRAPHIC: "The Muted Alarm" — alarm bell with a BLOCKED overlay]

The only symptoms that persist are the ones NOT blocked by beta receptors: sweating — which occurs via a separate cholinergic pathway — plus confusion, drowsiness, and behavioral changes. So the classic presentation changes. Instead of the textbook "sweaty, tachycardic, trembling" hypoglycemia, what you get is an older adult who wakes up confused and drenched in sweat — and everyone assumes it's a nightmare, a stroke, or "just aging."

**Drug three: ACE Inhibitors**
Enalapril, lisinopril, ramipril. The third companion in this trio. ACE inhibitors increase insulin sensitivity by about 15 to 20 percent — that's actually one of their cardiovascular benefits. But when layered on top of a sulfonylurea's boosted insulin production and a beta-blocker's masked warning system, it tips the scales further toward hypoglycemia.

[ON-SCREEN GRAPHIC: THE THREE-DRUG STACK]
- Sulfonylurea → MORE insulin
- Beta-blocker → MASKED warning symptoms
- ACE inhibitor → INCREASED insulin sensitivity
- NET EFFECT: Silent nighttime glucose crash

This is exactly what happened to Miriam. Three individually appropriate medications creating a combination nobody specifically looked for.

---

### [SECTION 3: WHY THE MISSES IT — AND WHY YOU SHOULDN'T]

Let me be direct here, because this matters: the standard medical workup for "waking up confused at night" in an older adult with diabetes is not designed to catch nocturnal hypoglycemia. Here's why:

**Reason one: Timing.** By the time Miriam got to the ER — usually 30 to 60 minutes after her episode — her body's remaining counter-regulatory mechanisms had already raised her blood sugar back to normal. The episode was over by the time anyone checked. This is called "reactive correction" and it makes the problem invisible to standard testing.

**Reason two: HbA1c doesn't capture variability.** Miriam's HbA1c — the three-month average blood sugar test — was 6.8 percent. That's good control. But HbA1c is an average. You can have an average of 6.8 while spending two hours a night at 48 mg/dL and two hours after meals at 220 mg/dL, and the average looks fine. HbA1c cannot detect hypoglycemia. This is one of its most dangerous blind spots.

[PAUSE]

**Reason three: The symptom profile looks like other things.** Confusion and sweating at night in an older adult triggers cardiac workup, neurological workup, maybe an EEG. Blood sugar checked once, during waking hours, comes back normal. And the patient is sent home.

A 2023 study in *Diabetes Care* found that nocturnal hypoglycemia in older adults on sulfonylureas had a mean duration of 74 minutes per episode, but only 12 percent of episodes were captured on standard glucose monitoring. The other 88 percent occurred between checks — invisible to conventional testing.

---

### [SECTION 4: THE SELF-DETECTION PROTOCOL]

So how do you catch something that hides? Here's what I told Miriam, and what I'd recommend if any of this sounds familiar:

**Step one: Get a glucometer and check at 3 AM.**

This sounds simple because it is. If you're waking up at night with sweating, confusion, or unusual symptoms — and you're on any combination of diabetes medications, beta-blockers, or ACE inhibitors — set an alarm for 3 AM, check your blood sugar, and write down the number.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: 3 AM PROTOCOL]
- Keep glucometer on nightstand
- Check when waking with symptoms
- Check at 3 AM for 5 consecutive nights
- Write down every number
- Below 70? Call your doctor with the data

If your blood sugar is below 70 mg/dL at any overnight check, that's clinically significant hypoglycemia. Below 54, that's severe — and that's your data to bring to your doctor.

**Step two: Consider a continuous glucose monitor.**

This is the gold standard for detecting invisible hypoglycemia. A CGM — like the Freestyle Libre or Dexcom — measures glucose every one to five minutes through a small sensor worn on your arm. It will catch every dip, every crash, every rebound — 24 hours a day, including all night long.

Many insurance plans now cover CGMs for insulin-dependent diabetes. For non-insulin-dependent type 2 diabetes, coverage is improving but can be inconsistent. Ask your doctor specifically: "Would a continuous glucose monitor help me detect nighttime hypoglycemia?" That specific clinical question makes it easier for your doctor to justify the prescription.

[PAUSE]

I'll be honest — CGMs are one of the most underutilized tools in geriatric diabetes management. They reveal patterns that no lab test can, and for someone like Miriam, the data from a two-week CGM session would have immediately shown the problem.

**Step three: Track your symptoms alongside your numbers.**

Keep a small notebook or use your phone. When you wake up sweaty — what time? What was your glucose? When did you eat dinner? What did you eat? Did you have alcohol? Did you exercise that afternoon? Patterns emerge when you track, and those patterns are what your doctor needs to see.

---

### [SECTION 5: THE MEDICATION CONVERSATION — WHAT TO DISCUSS WITH YOUR DOCTOR]

This is where I want you to be informed and empowered, not frightened into stopping your medications.

If your overnight glucose checks or CGM data reveal nocturnal hypoglycemia, here's the conversation framework:

**First: "My overnight blood sugars are dropping below 70. Can we review my diabetes medication?"**

Specifically for patients on sulfonylureas, the conversation with your doctor might be about transitioning to a different class of diabetes medication — one that doesn't cause hypoglycemia. Medications like metformin, DPP-4 inhibitors (sitagliptin), GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide), or SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin) have essentially zero risk of hypoglycemia when used alone. They work, they protect your heart and kidneys, and they don't crash your blood sugar at night.

The 2024 American Diabetes Association Standards of Care specifically recommend *avoiding sulfonylureas* in older adults when safer alternatives are available. I'll repeat that, because it's important: this is not fringe advice. This is the current standard of care. If you're over 60 and on a sulfonylurea, asking your doctor about alternatives is medically appropriate.

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: ADA 2024 — "Avoid sulfonylureas in older adults when safer alternatives exist"]

**Second: "Is there an alternative to my beta-blocker?"**

This requires a careful conversation with your cardiologist, because beta-blockers are often prescribed for critical reasons — post-heart attack, heart failure, arrhythmia control. You cannot simply stop a beta-blocker; abrupt discontinuation can cause dangerous rebound effects.

But if your beta-blocker is for primary blood pressure management — and your cardiologist agrees — there may be alternatives that don't mask hypoglycemia symptoms. This is the conversation that prevents the next Miriam.

**Third: "Should we adjust the timing or dose of my evening medications?"**

Sometimes the solution isn't changing the medication; it's adjusting the dose or timing. A smaller evening dose of a sulfonylurea, or shifting it to morning-only, can reduce overnight hypoglycemia risk while maintaining daytime glucose control.

---

### [SECTION 6: THE EMERGENCY — WHEN TO CALL 911]

I want to be clear about this, because I don't want the educational content in this video to delay an emergency response.

If you or someone you care for wakes up and:

- Cannot be roused from sleep
- Is having a seizure
- Is profoundly confused or combative
- Has blood sugar below 40 mg/dL that isn't responding to oral glucose

[ON-SCREEN TEXT: CALL 911 — Unconscious | Seizure | BG <40 unresponsive]

Call 911. This is severe hypoglycemia and it's a medical emergency. If the person is awake and able to swallow, give oral glucose — juice, glucose tablets, regular soda. If they can't swallow safely or are unconscious, a glucagon injection or nasal glucagon (Baqsimi) is indicated if available.

[PAUSE]

Every household where someone is on diabetes medication should have a glucagon kit available. If you don't have one, ask your doctor about prescribing it. It costs between 30 and 60 dollars with most insurance plans, and it can be life-saving.

---

### [CLOSING — MIRIAM'S RESOLUTION]

Here's what happened to Miriam.

We put her on a continuous glucose monitor for two weeks. The data was striking: four episodes where her blood sugar dropped below 50 between 2 and 4 AM. Four. In two weeks. Every single one corresponded to her waking up drenched in sweat.

We transitioned her from glyburide to a GLP-1 receptor agonist. We kept her beta-blocker — her cardiologist felt it was important for her cardiac history. We adjusted her ACE inhibitor dose downward by half.

Within two weeks, the nighttime episodes stopped completely. Within a month, her sleep quality had dramatically improved. She told me she'd forgotten what a full night of sleep felt like.

[PAUSE]

Miriam's case is not rare. It's underrecognized. The combination of a long-acting sulfonylurea, a beta-blocker, and an ACE inhibitor in an older adult is a pattern I see regularly in my practice — and it's a pattern where simple adjustments can be life-changing.

If any part of this video sounded familiar — the confusion, the sweating, the 3 AM dread, the ER visits that found nothing — please talk to your doctor. Bring this video if it helps. Show them your glucose data. Ask about a CGM. Ask whether your medication combination puts you at risk.

You deserve to sleep through the night. You also deserve to know what your medications are doing when you're not watching.

I'm Dr. Victor Kane. Stay safe tonight, and I'll see you in the next one.

## ORIGINAL INSIGHTS

1. **"The Muted Alarm" framework** — Beta-blockers don't just "mask symptoms"; they specifically eliminate the beta-adrenergic warning signs while leaving the cholinergic (sweating) and neuroglycopenic (confusion) symptoms intact. This creates an alternative symptom profile that most clinicians aren't trained to recognize as hypoglycemia. No competitor explains the *selective* masking mechanism.

2. **Three-drug convergence model** — The specific combination of sulfonylurea (too much insulin) + beta-blocker (masked alarm) + ACE inhibitor (increased sensitivity) creates what I'm calling a "silent hypoglycemia stack." This is not a mono-drug risk; it's a systems-level interaction that requires thinking about medication *combinations*, not individual drugs.

3. **"HbA1c blind spot" explanation** — Making the math visible: average of 6.8% can hide hours at 48 mg/dL and hours at 220 mg/dL. This reframes the "your labs look fine" dismissal as a limitation of the test, not evidence of health.

4. **3 AM Protocol** — A concrete, zero-cost self-testing protocol that empowers patients with data. This positions Dr. Kane as giving viewers a clinical tool, not just information.
