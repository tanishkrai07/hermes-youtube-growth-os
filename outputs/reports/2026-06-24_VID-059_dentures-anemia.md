You tell yourself it's just that they're hard to chew, or they don't taste the same, or you've just... moved on from those foods.

[LEAN IN]

What if I told you that the reason you're tired has nothing to do with getting older — and everything to do with what's happening inside your mouth?

Today, I want to explain a connection that almost nobody makes. Between your dentures. And your energy. And the slow, invisible narrowing of your diet that's been happening for years.

Because this is real. It's more common than you think. And it's fixable.

### THE HIDDEN MECHANICAL PROBLEM

**DR. KANE:**
Let me start with something most people don't know about dentures.

[ON-SCREEN: "Dentures Are Not Permanent"]

When you lose a tooth — whether it's one or all of them — the bone that used to support that tooth begins to resorb. It dissolves. This is a normal physiological process, but it's relentless. After tooth loss, the jawbone loses approximately 0.5 to 1 millimeter of height per year.

[ON-SCREEN: Jawbone resorption timeline — Year 1, Year 5, Year 10, Year 20]

That doesn't sound like much. But over 10 or 20 years, it's dramatic. The ridge that your denture sits on shrinks. Slowly. Invisibly. Day by day, you don't notice.

And here's the problem. Your denture was fitted to your jaw at a specific point in time. But your jaw is changing shape every single year after that.

[PAUSE]

A 2023 study in the Journal of Dental Research confirmed what dentists have known for decades: continuous bone resorption after tooth loss means that dentures fitted 5, 10, or 15 years ago are sitting on a fundamentally different jaw shape than the one they were made for.

The result? Reduced contact. Reduced stability. Reduced chewing efficiency.

And reduced chewing efficiency has consequences that go far beyond your mouth.

---

### THE CHEWING-EFFICIENCY REVELATION

**DR. KANE:**
Here's a number that surprises most people.

[ON-SCREEN: "Ill-fitting dentures reduce chewing efficiency by 50-75%"]

Ill-fitting dentures reduce chewing efficiency by 50 to 75 percent.

That means if you're trying to chew a piece of steak, or raw broccoli, or a handful of nuts — you're getting only a quarter to half of the mechanical breakdown that a natural dentition would provide.

Now, you might think: "So I just don't eat those foods." And that's exactly what happens. But you don't make that decision consciously. It's not "I've decided to avoid iron-rich foods." It's "this is uncomfortable to chew, so I'll have something softer."

[ON-SCREEN: Foods denture wearers avoid — steak, raw vegetables, nuts, apples, crusty bread]

A 2024 study in Oral Diseases tracked dietary patterns in denture-wearing seniors. They found that denture wearers avoid an average of 3 to 5 food categories. The most commonly avoided: tough meats, raw vegetables, nuts, apples, and crusty breads.

[PAUSE]

Notice what those foods have in common. They're not just "hard to chew." They're also among the most nutrient-dense foods in a typical diet. Particularly for iron.

And here's the part that makes this especially insidious. The avoidance is unconscious. Nobody decides "I'm going to stop eating iron-rich foods." What happens is: steak is uncomfortable, so you choose ground beef. Ground beef is uncomfortable, so you choose ground turkey. Ground turkey is difficult, so you have soup. The narrowing happens over months and years. And at each step, it feels like a preference change, not a nutritional decline.

---

### THE FOOD-AVOIDANCE CASCADE

**DR. KANE:**
Let me walk you through what I call the food-avoidance cascade. Because it's not one decision. It's a series of small, unconscious adjustments that add up over years.

[ON-SCREEN: "The Food-Avoidance Cascade"]

**Year one after denture fitting:** Your dentures fit well. You can chew most things. Maybe you notice that raw carrots are a bit harder, but you manage.

**Year three:** The dentures feel a bit looser. You start cutting your food into smaller pieces. You avoid very tough cuts of meat. You choose ground beef instead of steak. This is subtle. You don't think of it as dietary change — you think of it as "what I feel like eating."

**Year five:** The dentures slip occasionally when you chew. You've stopped eating nuts entirely — they get under the denture and hurt. Raw vegetables are difficult. You cook everything until it's soft. You gravitate toward soups, pasta, mashed potatoes, soft bread.

**Year ten:** You've quietly eliminated most textured foods. Your diet has narrowed to soft, processed options. You tell people you prefer this food now. And you do — because it's what's comfortable.

[PAUSE]

But here's what's happened nutritionally. You've systematically eliminated the foods highest in bioavailable iron. Red meat. Dark leafy greens. Legumes. Nuts. And you've replaced them with foods that are low in iron or contain iron in a form that's poorly absorbed.

[ON-SCREEN: "Iron-rich foods → replaced by → low-iron soft foods"]

This isn't a choice. It's a mechanical consequence. And it takes years. Which is why nobody connects it.

---

### HOW IRON DEFICIENCY DEVELOPS SILENTLY

**DR. KANE:**
Let me explain why your blood work might have been "normal" even as this was happening.

[ON-SCREEN: "The Iron Depletion Timeline"]

Iron deficiency doesn't happen overnight. It's a staged process.

**Stage one: Depletion.** Your iron stores — measured by ferritin — begin to drop. But your hemoglobin is still normal. Your doctor checks the CBC, sees a normal hemoglobin, and says your iron is fine. But your reserves are empty.

**Stage two: Deficient production.** Without adequate iron, your bone marrow can't produce healthy red cells. The cells it does produce are smaller than normal — microcytic. But the hemoglobin might still be in the "normal" range, just barely.

**Stage three: Anemia.** Now your hemoglobin drops below normal. You're officially anemic. But this stage may not arrive for years after the dietary narrowing began.

[PAUSE]

A 2023 study in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition found that dietary iron narrowing — the gradual elimination of iron-rich foods — was associated with a 2.5-fold increased risk of microcytic anemia in seniors over 65.

Two and a half times more likely. And the connection was missed in the majority of cases because nobody asked about chewing ability.

[ON-SCREEN: "Nobody asks about chewing ability"]

Think about that. In a medical system that checks your blood, your heart, your lungs — almost nobody asks whether you can actually chew your food.

And there's a compounding factor. As you eat less iron-rich food, your body's iron stores — measured by ferritin — begin to drop. But your hemoglobin stays normal for months or even years. Your doctor checks the CBC, sees a normal hemoglobin, and says your iron is fine. But your reserves are being depleted. By the time your hemoglobin drops below normal, you've been iron-deficient for a long time. The fatigue you've been feeling? It started years before the lab abnormality appeared.

---

### WHY YOUR DOCTOR MISSES THIS

**DR. KANE:**
I want to be clear: this is not a failure of your doctor. It's a failure of how we think about the body.

[ON-SCREEN: "Why the Connection Is Missed"]

**Reason one: Specialization.** Your doctor looks at your blood work. Your dentist looks at your dentures. These are two different appointments. Two different professionals. And they don't talk to each other as much as they should.

**Reason two: Gradual onset.** The fatigue from iron deficiency develops slowly. You don't wake up one day exhausted. You become a little more tired over months. You attribute it to age, to poor sleep, to stress. And honestly? Those explanations are often correct. But when they're not, the real cause hides behind the obvious one.

**Reason three: You don't mention it.** Most people don't tell their doctor "I've stopped eating steak because it's hard to chew." It doesn't seem like a medical issue. It seems like a preference. So it doesn't come up.

**Reason four: The dentures "work."** They're in your mouth. They allow you to eat SOMETHING. So from your perspective, they're functioning. The fact that they've narrowed your diet by 30 or 40 percent isn't visible to you — because you don't remember what you used to eat.

[PAUSE]

A 2024 study in Age and Ageing found that 60 percent of seniors with unexplained anemia had their fatigue attributed to "normal aging" before the actual cause was identified.

Sixty percent. That's not a small oversight. That's a systematic blind spot.

### THE DENTURE ASSESSMENT

**DR. KANE:**
So what should you do? If you're over 60, you wear dentures, and you've been feeling tired — or you've noticed your diet narrowing — here's what I'd suggest.

[ON-SCREEN: "The Denture Assessment Protocol"]

**Step one: The bite test.** Try to bite into a firm food — an apple, a piece of crusty bread, a firm vegetable. If it's difficult, uncomfortable, or your denture shifts, that's information. Your dentures may not be fitting properly.

**Step two: The food diary.** For one week, write down everything you eat. Then compare it to what you ate five years ago. If you've eliminated multiple food categories — particularly proteins and vegetables — that's a red flag.

**Step three: Ask your dentist specifically about chewing efficiency.** Don't just ask "are my dentures okay?" Ask: "Are my dentures allowing me to chew a full range of foods? Is there bone resorption affecting the fit?"

[LEAN IN]

**Step four: Request a nutritional panel.** Not just a CBC. Ask for ferritin, serum iron, TIBC, and transferrin saturation. The CBC alone can miss early iron deficiency.

**Step five: Consider a reline or remake.** If your dentures are more than 5 years old, they almost certainly need adjustment. Denture relining — adding material to the underside to improve fit — is a simple procedure that can dramatically improve chewing efficiency. A full remake may be needed if significant resorption has occurred.

[PAUSE]

I've seen patients go from eating only soft foods to eating steak again after a denture reline. The difference in their energy, their nutrition, their quality of life — it's remarkable.

### WHAT ACTUALLY HELPS

**DR. KANE:**
Let me give you the practical framework. Because knowing the problem is only useful if you know what to do about it.

[ON-SCREEN: "What Actually Helps"]

**Immediate actions:**

**Get your dentures assessed.** If it's been more than 5 years since they were fitted, or if you notice any slipping, discomfort, or food getting underneath — see your dentist. Ask specifically about bone resorption and chewing efficiency.

**Get a full iron panel.** Not just hemoglobin. Ferritin, serum iron, TIBC. If your ferritin is below 50 ng/mL, that's worth discussing even if your hemoglobin is "normal."

**Reintroduce iron-rich foods gradually.** Start with softer iron sources — ground beef, lentils, spinach cooked until tender. As your dentures improve, expand to tougher textures.

**Pair iron with vitamin C.** Iron absorption increases 2-3x when consumed with vitamin C. A squeeze of lemon on your spinach. Orange juice with your iron supplement. Simple but effective.

[PAUSE]

**If your dentures can't be adequately repaired:**

**Consider implant-supported dentures.** This is a bigger investment, but the difference in chewing efficiency is dramatic. Implant-supported dentures can restore 70-80% of natural chewing function, compared to 25-40% for traditional dentures on a resorbed ridge.

[ON-SCREEN: "Traditional dentures: 25-40% chewing efficiency → Implant-supported: 70-80%"]

Not everyone is a candidate. There needs to be adequate bone for implant placement. But it's worth asking about.
