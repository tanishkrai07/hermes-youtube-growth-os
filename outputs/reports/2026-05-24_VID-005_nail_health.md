I had a patient come in last month — I'll call her Margaret. She was 68, pretty sharp, came in for a routine blood pressure check. Standard visit. While I was taking her pressure, I glanced down at her hands resting on the exam table.

Something wasn't right with her fingernails.

They were almost entirely white. Like someone had painted them with a milky, opaque polish. Just a thin band of normal pink at the very tips.

Margaret had been seeing doctors for years. Nobody had ever mentioned it.

Now — before anyone panics — white nails can be completely benign. But they can also be one of the earliest external signs of liver stress. And the problem is, most people have no idea that nails can tell you anything useful about what's happening inside your body.

**[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "5 Nail Changes Your Body Is Using to Signal Disease"]**

That's what I want to walk you through today. Five specific nail changes that, once you know what they look like, you can actually spot in about ten seconds. For each one, I'll tell you what it typically looks like, what body system it's trying to flag, and — this is the important part — exactly what to bring up with your doctor.

Let's start with the one I just mentioned.

---

**[B-ROLL: Close-up photograph of Terry's nails — fingers showing predominantly white/pale nail beds with narrow pink distal band.]**
**[TEXT OVERLAY: "Terry's Nails"]**

**[SECTION 1: TERRY'S NAILS — THE LIVER SIGNAL]**

So these are called Terry's nails. Named after Richard Terry, the physician who first described the pattern back in 1954. Here's what to look for: the nail bed — that's the flat surface under your nail — turns mostly white or pale gray. The normal pink color that comes from blood flow in the tissue underneath basically disappears. You're left with this almost porcelain look, and usually a thin band of darker color right at the tip, maybe a millimeter or two wide.

Now here's what most people don't realize about Terry's nails. They are not a liver disease diagnosis. They're a sign. There's an important distinction. About 80% of people with severe liver cirrhosis will develop Terry's nails at some point. But the reverse is NOT true — having Terry's nails does NOT mean you have liver disease.

What's actually happening is that the nail bed is changing at a tissue level. The connective tissue underneath becomes thicker and more fibrous. That change blocks the normal blood vessel pattern from showing through the nail. And the liver is one of the organs that most commonly triggers that kind of tissue remodeling when it's under stress.

**[PAUSE]**

Terry's nails have also been associated with congestive heart failure, diabetes, and even just normal aging. Yes — some perfectly healthy people over 70 develop them with no underlying disease at all.

So what do you actually do? Here's what I tell my patients. If you look at your nails and they've changed significantly in color over the past few months, don't Google yourself into a panic. Instead, the next time you see your doctor, say this:

"I've noticed the color of my nails has changed. My nail beds have become much paler than they used to be. Can we check my liver function?"

That's it. A simple blood panel — AST, ALT, bilirubin, albumin — will give your doctor a clear picture. You're not diagnosing yourself. You're raising a flag that's worth investigating.

**[TRANSITION — Dr. Kane shifts slightly, as though a different thought just occurred to him.]**

Now that thing about tissue remodeling under the nails — the same concept applies to the next one, except instead of the nail bed going white, the surface itself gets disrupted.

---

**[B-ROLL: Photograph showing Beau's nails — visible horizontal ridges/depressions across multiple nail plates.]**
**[TEXT OVERLAY: "Beau's Lines"]**

**[SECTION 2: BEAU'S LINES — YOUR NAILS KEEP A DIARY]**

Beau's lines. These are horizontal grooves or indentations that run across the nail, side to side. Not lengthwise ridges — those are extremely common with aging and usually mean nothing. Beau's lines cut across the nail horizontally, like someone pressed a tiny trench into the surface.

Here's the fascinating and slightly unsettling thing about Beau's lines: they're basically your nails keeping a diary of past physical stress.

Your nails grow from a little factory underneath the skin at the base called the matrix. When your body goes through significant physical stress — a severe infection, major surgery, a period of extreme malnutrition, even a really bad bout of pneumonia — the matrix temporarily slows down or briefly stops producing nail material. When it starts up again, it leaves a visible line where the growth was interrupted.

**[LEANS FORWARD SLIGHTLY]**

Because fingernails grow at roughly three millimeters per month, the position of the Beau's line tells you approximately when the stressful event happened. If the line is about six millimeters from the base of your nail, that stress event happened roughly two months ago. I've actually used this with patients to help reconstruct a timeline when someone couldn't remember exactly when they'd been sick.

Common causes: uncontrolled diabetes, peripheral vascular disease, severe zinc deficiency, chemotherapy, and acute infections. In older adults, one of the most common causes nobody talks about is a myocardial infarction — a heart attack. Yes, a heart attack can show up on your nails weeks later in the form of a Beau's line. The stress the body sustained during the event disrupts nail matrix function.

What to tell your doctor: "I've noticed horizontal grooves across several of my fingernails that weren't there before. I want to check whether there's been an underlying illness or stress my body hasn't fully recovered from."

Your doctor may want to run an HbA1c — that checks your average blood sugar over the past three months — a complete metabolic panel, and possibly look at your cardiovascular risk profile. You can also estimate when the disruption occurred based on where the line sits, and cross-reference it with anything significant that happened health-wise around that time.

Alright. Next one is a big deal, and it's one I think about more carefully than almost any other nail sign because of what it can represent.

**[B-ROLL: Photograph comparison showing normal fingertips vs. clubbed fingertips. Highlight the increased curvature and widening of the nail base.]**
**[TEXT OVERLAY: "Clubbing"]**
**[ANIMATION: Diamond window test — two fingernails pressed together, showing the normal diamond-shaped gap vs. the gap disappearing in clubbing.]**

**[SECTION 3: CLUBBING — THE FINGER SIGNAL THAT REACHES DEEP]**

Clubbing. This one is worth paying attention to because it has one of the strongest associations with serious systemic disease of any nail finding we're discussing today.

Here's how to spot it. Look at your fingertips from the side. Normally, there's a slight angle where the nail meets the skin — about 160 degrees or so. When clubbing develops, the fingertip becomes wider and rounder. The nail curves more dramatically, almost wrapping around the tip of the finger. The base of the nail may feel spongy or soft when you press on it, because the tissue beneath has thickened.

There's a very simple test you can do right now. It's called the diamond window test, or Schamroth's window test. Take your two index fingers and press the nails together, back to back. If you can see a small diamond-shaped gap of light between the nail beds, that's normal. That window of light should be there. If that gap disappears — if the nail beds just press flat against each other with no diamond shape — that's a positive sign of clubbing.

**[SLOW DOWN]**

Now here's why I take this one seriously. Clubbing has been linked to lung cancer, bronchiectasis, interstitial lung disease, and congenital heart disease. In a study looking at patients with lung cancer, about 35% had some degree of nail clubbing at the time of diagnosis. It's also strongly associated with inflammatory bowel disease — specifically Crohn's disease.

What's actually happening is that the tissues at the tips of the fingers are getting excess blood flow and fluid buildup from growth factors that aren't being properly filtered. In lung diseases, the theory is that megakaryocytes — these are large bone marrow cells — get past the lung's filtering mechanism and lodge in the fingertips, triggering that spongy tissue growth.

This takes weeks to months to develop. It doesn't happen overnight. So if it's there, your body has been dealing with something for a while.

Important note: some people are born with clubbing that's purely genetic and completely harmless. It runs in families. If your parent or sibling has similar-looking fingertips and they're healthy, you may have familial clubbing. That's worth knowing before you alarm yourself.

But if you're noticing this change and it's NEW — you haven't always had it — here's what to bring up with your doctor:

"I've noticed my fingertips look wider and rounder than they used to, and the nail curve has changed. I'd like to rule out any underlying lung or heart condition."

Ask specifically for a chest X-ray and a comprehensive pulmonary function assessment. If your doctor is thorough, they'll also listen carefully to your heart. You may want to mention any history of shortness of breath, chronic cough, or digestive symptoms, even if those seem unrelated to your fingers.

**[TRANSITION — Dr. Kane relaxes his posture slightly, shifts to a warmer, slightly lighter tone.]**

Three down. Let me tell you about one that's the complete opposite of clubbing in what it looks like — and it's incredibly common in older women especially.

**[B-ROLL: Side-by-side images. Left: normal convex nail. Right: spoon-shaped nail (koilonychia) with visible concavity. Optionally, a shot of a drop of water sitting in the curved nail to illustrate the spoon shape.]**
**[TEXT OVERLAY: "Spoon Nails / Koilonychia"]**

**[SECTION 4: SPOON NAILS — THE BOWL SHAPE THAT POINTS TO IRON]**

Koilonychia — or spoon nails. Instead of the nail curving gently downward like a normal nail, the center dips inward and the edges curl upward, so the nail itself forms a little scoop or bowl shape. If you placed a drop of water on the nail, the water would sit there and not run off. That's where the name comes from.

Spoon nails are most strongly associated with iron deficiency anemia. And here's the piece I think matters most for our audience: iron deficiency in adults over 60 is not always about diet. Yes, not eating enough iron-rich foods can cause it. But in older adults, the more common culprits are gastrointestinal blood loss — from ulcers, from polyps, from certain medications, from malabsorption disorders like celiac disease.

Which means spoon nails can be the first visible clue that there's slow, chronic blood loss happening somewhere in the digestive tract. Blood loss you can't see. Blood loss you might not feel.

I had a patient — 71-year-old woman, came in because her hair was thinning. She was convinced it was genetic. But when I looked at her nails, they were distinctly spoon-shaped. Her iron levels were dangerously low. Further investigation found a small lesion in her colon that was bleeding almost imperceptibly. Would've become a much bigger problem down the road if we hadn't caught it when we did.

Now, spoon nails can also appear in thyroid disorders — both underactive and overactive — and sometimes in people who work with petroleum-based solvents. They can also, like Terry's nails, sometimes just be a benign finding. But for my money, in anyone over 60, the iron connection is the one you want to rule out first.

What to tell your doctor:

"My nails have started to curve inward — they're shaped like little spoons. I'd like to get my iron levels and ferritin checked, and a complete blood count."

Ferritin is the one I really want to highlight. A lot of doctors will check hemoglobin alone. That tells you if you're anemic. But ferritin tells you your iron stores. You can have normal hemoglobin and still be severely depleted in iron stores — the anemia hasn't kicked in yet, but your body is running on empty. That's the early stage. That's when you want to catch it.

Ask for: CBC, serum iron, total iron-binding capacity, ferritin, and if there's any concern about GI sources, your doctor may recommend a colonoscopy or imaging of the upper GI tract.

**[TRANSITION — Dr. Kane's tone shifts. More deliberate. Slightly more measured.]**

Last one. This is the one I want you to take most seriously — not because it's the most common, but because missing it has the highest stakes.

---

**[B-ROLL: Close-up photograph of a single dark longitudinal streak (longitudinal melanonychia) running from cuticle to tip on a thumbnail. Then, a second image showing a wider, irregularly colored streak with pigmentation spreading to the surrounding skin — Hutchinson's sign.]**
**[TEXT OVERLAY: "Dark Longitudinal Streaks"]**
**[TEXT OVERLAY: "Watch for: width >3mm, irregular color, pigment spreading to skin (Hutchinson's sign)"]**

**[SECTION 5: DARK STREAKS — THE LINE THAT COULD SAVE YOUR LIFE]**

Longitudinal melanonychia. That's the clinical term. What it looks like is a brown or black stripe running lengthwise along the nail, from the base to the tip. One stripe, on one nail. Not multiple nails. Not both sides. One.

Now, first — a crucial point. Dark streaks in the nails are very common in people with darker skin tones. African American, Asian, and Hispanic adults can have perfectly benign brown stripes in their nails that are just normal melanin deposits. That's not what I'm talking about, and this is not something that should cause alarm if it's been present since youth and hasn't changed.

What I AM talking about is when a new dark streak appears, especially in a lighter-skinned adult, especially after age 50, especially on one nail only. When the streak is wider than three millimeters. When the color is uneven — darker in the middle, lighter at the edges, or vice versa. And — this is the one that really gets my attention — when the pigment spreads beyond the nail itself onto the surrounding skin. That's called Hutchinson's sign, and it's an important clinical marker.

That pattern can indicate subungual melanoma. Melanoma of the nail matrix. It is rare. It accounts for only about 1-3% of all melanoma cases. But it is aggressive, and it is frequently diagnosed late because people assume it's a bruise, a fungal infection, or just part of aging.

It's also one of the few melanoma types that doesn't require UV exposure to develop. It occurs under the nail, in the matrix, and it can appear in anyone regardless of skin color or sun exposure history.

If you have a dark streak on ONE nail that appeared recently, is widening, and the color isn't uniform — do not wait. Do not assume it's nothing. Do not put nail polish over it and forget about it. Bring it to a dermatologist's attention within the next few weeks, not months.

What to say to your doctor, or better yet, directly to a dermatologist:

"I have a new dark streak on one of my fingernails that wasn't there before. It's wider than a pencil line, and I'm concerned about the possibility of melanoma. I'd like a dermoscopic evaluation."

If your doctor wants to take a "wait and see" approach, here's what I'll say about that: reasonable monitoring of a very thin, uniform, ambiguous streak on a younger patient, discussed with an expert, can be appropriate. But after 60, with a new, widening, pigmented stripe? I would push for a biopsy. Early detection of subungual melanoma has a five-year survival rate above 90%. Late detection drops that dramatically.

I'm not telling you this to frighten you. I'm telling you this because early detection of this particular condition is one of the highest-value things you can do for your own health. A fifteen-second glance at your nails, once a month, is all it takes.

**[PAUSE]**

**[B-ROLL: Gentle, calm graphic — the five nail changes displayed in a simple visual list with icons.]**

So. Five nail changes. Let me bring this together in a way that's actually useful instead of just alarming.

Terry's nails — white nail beds. Think: liver function panel. Could be benign. Worth asking about.

Beau's lines — horizontal grooves. Think: metabolic stress. Diabetes, cardiovascular events, severe infections. Check HbA1c and comprehensive metabolic panel.

Clubbing — widened, spongy fingertips. Think: lung and heart. Get the diamond window test done. Ask for chest imaging and pulmonary assessment.

Spoon nails — concave, scooped nails. Think: iron stores and gastrointestinal blood loss. Don't just check hemoglobin — check ferritin.

Dark streaks — single, widening, pigmented stripe. Think: dermatologist, soon. Not a wait-and-see situation if you're over 60 and it's new.

Here's what I want you to do. Not today, not this week, but sometime in the next time you're sitting with nothing to do — look at your fingernails. Really look at them. Compare them to photos of yourself from a few years ago if you can find them. Nail changes are slow. You might have been living with a sign for months and just never had someone tell you what to look for.

And then add one question to your next doctor's appointment. Just one. Find the one that fits what you're seeing and ask that question. You are not diagnosing yourself. You are being a good reporter. The best patients I've ever had are the ones who come in with specific observations. "My nails look different" is vague. "My fingertips look wider than they did a year ago and I can't see a diamond shape when I press my nails together" — that's specific. That gives your doctor something real to work with.
