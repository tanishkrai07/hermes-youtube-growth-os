### COLD OPEN

**DR. KANE:**
I want you to do something for me. Don't move your legs right now. Just notice them.

[PAUSE — 3 seconds]

If you're watching this at night, and you're the kind of person whose legs feel like they need to move — that crawling, that aching, that restlessness that gets worse the more you try to hold still — you felt something during that pause, didn't you?

Or maybe you know someone who does this. Who has to get up and walk around at 11 PM because their legs won't let them sit through a movie. Who lies in bed and kicks, or shifts, or gets up to pace, and goes to breakfast exhausted.

That's restless legs syndrome. And for a lot of people over 60, I think it's been explained wrong.

[LEAN IN]

Not misunderstood — *explained* wrong. Because the problem most people are told to fix — circulation in the legs, muscle cramps, anxiety — that's not where the problem lives. The problem lives in a region of your brain the size of a pea. And it's running out of iron.

Today, I'm going to explain what's actually happening, why the standard blood test misses it, and what you can do about it.

But first — let me tell you why I think this gets dismissed so easily.

---

### THE DISMISSAL PROBLEM

**DR. KANE:**
Restless legs syndrome is one of those conditions that sits in a strange medical territory. It's neurological, but it doesn't show up on a standard neurological exam. There's no scan for it. There's no single diagnostic test. The diagnosis is clinical — which means it's based on your description of symptoms.

And those symptoms are... subjective. Your legs feel restless. They need to move. It's worse at night. It's better when you walk.

To a time-pressed clinician, that can sound vague. And over the years, I've heard patients tell me their restless legs were attributed to anxiety, to poor circulation, to aging, to "just getting up and walking more."

None of those things are completely wrong — movement does help, and age is a factor. But they miss the mechanism.

[ON-SCREEN: "RLS affects 5-10% of adults. Prevalence increases with age. Affects women more than men."]

And this matters, because RLS isn't just annoying. Chronic sleep deprivation from restless legs affects cardiovascular risk, cognitive function, quality of life. People who've been dealing with this for years — they're not just tired. They're exhausted in a way that accumulates.

So let me tell you what's actually going on. And it starts in a part of the brain you've probably never heard of.

---

### THE BRAIN REGION THE SIZE OF A PEA

**DR. KANE:**
Deep in the center of your brain, in a region called the midbrain, there's a structure called the substantia nigra. It's tiny. And it's one of the most important pieces of neurological real estate in your body.

[ON-SCREEN: Brain illustration highlighting the substantia nigra — small, dark region in the midbrain]

The substantia nigra is where a significant portion of your brain's dopamine is produced. Dopamine — you've heard of it. It's involved in reward, motivation, movement. But what's relevant here is the motor control function. Dopamine from the substantia nigra travels along pathways that help your muscles fire in coordinated, intentional patterns.

When dopamine signaling degrades, movement becomes less controlled. In its most severe form, this is Parkinson's disease — where the dopamine-producing cells in the substantia nigra die off. But there's a spectrum. And on the milder end of that spectrum, you get restless legs.

Here's what I think is the key insight: the substantia nigra needs iron to function properly.

[PAUSE]

Not iron in your blood. Iron inside the neurons of that specific brain region. Iron is a cofactor for tyrosine hydroxylase — the enzyme that converts tyrosine into L-DOPA, which is then converted into dopamine. No iron? That enzyme slows down. Dopamine production drops. And the motor circuitry starts firing erratically — which you experience as your legs needing to move.

This has been confirmed on MRI studies. Researchers — particularly Dr. Richard Allen's group at Johns Hopkins — have shown that people with restless legs have lower iron concentrations in the substantia nigra compared to people without RLS.

---

### WHY YOUR BLOOD TEST SAYS "FINE"

**DR. KANE:**
So here's where it gets frustrating.

When you go to your doctor and say, "I think I have restless legs," the standard workup includes a basic metabolic panel and a complete blood count. They check your hemoglobin. They might check your ferritin — which is your body's iron storage protein.

And your ferritin comes back at, say, 35 ng/mL. The lab's reference range says normal is 12 to 150, or sometimes 20 to 200. So 35 is "normal."

[LEAN IN]

But here's the thing about ferritin reference ranges — they're designed to detect anemia. They're designed to tell you whether you have enough iron to make red blood cells. They are NOT designed to tell you whether your brain has enough iron to make dopamine.

And the research on restless legs suggests that the threshold for brain iron sufficiency is higher than the threshold for blood iron sufficiency.

[ON-SCREEN: "Ferritin <75 ng/mL in RLS patients — associated with symptom severity (Sleep Medicine 2023)"]

A ferritin level of 35 is enough to keep your hemoglobin normal. It may not be enough to keep your substantia nigra functioning optimally.

This is the gap. This is why people get told their iron is "fine" while their legs are telling a completely different story.

---

### THE DOPAMINE CONNECTION — EXPLAINED SIMPLY

**DR. KANE:**
Let me make this concrete.

Think of dopamine as the signal that tells your muscles: "We're done. You can rest now. The movement is complete."

When dopamine signaling is healthy, that signal is clear. Your legs settle. You can sit still. You can lie in bed without feeling like your muscles are waiting for instructions that never come.

When dopamine signaling is degraded — because the substantia nigra doesn't have enough iron to produce adequate dopamine — that "all clear" signal gets weak. Your motor neurons keep firing. Your legs feel like they need to move because the brain hasn't sent the "stand down" signal.

[ON-SCREEN: Simple graphic — "Dopamine = the 'all clear' signal for movement"]

And here's why it's worse at night: dopamine has a circadian rhythm. It's naturally lowest in the evening and overnight. So if your baseline dopamine production is already compromised by low brain iron, the nighttime dip pushes you below the threshold where your motor system can stay quiet.

That's why restless legs is a nighttime condition. It's not that something bad happens at night — it's that something good (dopamine) drops to its lowest point, and your system can't compensate.

---

### THE FERRITIN THRESHOLD THAT MATTERS

**DR. KANE:**
So what should your ferritin be? If the standard range says 12 to 150, but 35 might not be enough for your brain — where's the line?

The research isn't perfectly settled, but there's a growing consensus. Multiple studies — including a 2023 paper in Sleep Medicine — have found that RLS symptom severity correlates with ferritin levels below 75 ng/mL. And a landmark randomized trial published in Lancet Neurology in 2024 showed that intravenous iron carboxymaltose improved RLS symptoms in patients with ferritin below 75.

[ON-SCREEN: "Target: Ferritin >75 ng/mL for RLS symptom management"]

Seventy-five. Not 35. Not 50. Seventy-five.

That's the number I want you to know about. If you have restless legs and your ferritin is below 75, there's a reasonable case — supported by clinical evidence — that iron repletion could help.

Now. Oral iron supplementation is a different conversation from intravenous iron. The Lancet study used IV iron, which bypasses the gut and delivers iron directly into the bloodstream. Oral iron is slower, less efficient, and comes with GI side effects. But for many people, it's a reasonable starting point — especially if your ferritin is in the 30 to 60 range.

---

### WHAT TYPE OF IRON — AND WHAT MAKES IT WORSE

**DR. KANE:**
If you and your doctor decide to try oral iron, the form matters. And so does what you take it with.

[ON-SCREEN: Iron absorption comparison graphic]

Ferrous sulfate is the most commonly prescribed form. It's cheap, it's available, and it works — but it's also the hardest on the stomach. Ferrous gluconate and ferrous bisglycinate tend to be better tolerated. The bisglycinate form in particular has evidence for better absorption with fewer GI side effects.

Here's what's non-negotiable: take it with vitamin C. Ascidic acid increases non-heme iron absorption by 2 to 3 times. A glass of orange juice, or a 500mg vitamin C tablet, taken with your iron supplement, makes a meaningful difference.

And here's what to avoid: calcium, antacids, and proton pump inhibitors — all of which reduce iron absorption.

[LEAN IN]

This last one is critical. Proton pump inhibitors — omeprazole, pantoprazole, esomeprazole — are taken by an estimated 40% of adults over 60. They reduce stomach acid. And stomach acid is essential for converting dietary iron into the absorbable form. Research published in the American Journal of Gastroenterology in 2023 found that PPI use reduces non-heme iron absorption by 50 to 70 percent.

So if you're taking a PPI and you have restless legs, and your ferritin is low-normal — there may be a connection worth discussing with your doctor. Not "stop your PPI." But: "Could my PPI be contributing to low iron, and is there an alternative we could trial?"

---

### THE PROTOCOL

**DR. KANE:**
Let me give you a practical framework for bringing this to your doctor.

[ON-SCREEN: "The RLS Iron Protocol"]

**Step 1:** Request a full iron panel. Not just hemoglobin. You want: serum iron, ferritin, TIBC (total iron binding capacity), and transferrin saturation. This gives you the complete picture.

**Step 2:** If your ferritin is below 75 ng/mL, discuss a trial of oral iron supplementation. A typical starting dose is 65 mg of elemental iron (which is about 325 mg of ferrous sulfate), taken every other day — recent research suggests alternate-day dosing may improve absorption compared to daily dosing.

**Step 3:** Take it with vitamin C. Avoid calcium, coffee, tea, and antacids within 2 hours.

**Step 4:** Recheck ferritin in 8 to 12 weeks. The goal is to get above 75 and see if symptoms improve.

**Step 5:** If oral iron doesn't raise ferritin adequately, or if you can't tolerate it, ask about IV iron. This is a conversation for a hematologist or a neurologist who specializes in movement disorders.

[PAUSE]

And one more thing. If your ferritin is below 30, or if you have any signs of iron deficiency anemia — fatigue, pallor, shortness of breath — that's not a "try supplements and see" situation. That's a "we need to find out why you're iron deficient" situation. Iron deficiency in older adults can be a sign of gastrointestinal bleeding, malabsorption, or other conditions that need evaluation.
