### SECTION 1: THE NUMBER THAT DOESN'T CHANGE

**DR. KANE:**
Let me back up and explain why this is different from everything you've been told about cholesterol.

[ON-SCREEN: Animated graphic — LDL particle vs Lp(a) particle]

You've heard of LDL — low-density lipoprotein. The "bad" cholesterol. And you've probably been told that if your LDL is low, you're protected. That's generally true. But LDL is only part of the picture.

Lp(a) — that's lipoprotein little a — is a particle that looks almost identical to LDL. But it has one extra piece attached to it. A protein called apolipoprotein(a). And that one extra piece changes everything.

[ON-SCREEN: Close-up animation — apo(a) protein attached to LDL-like particle, with "Velcro-like" sticky properties highlighted]

Think of LDL as a delivery truck driving through your bloodstream. It drops off cholesterol at cells that need it. Lp(a) is the same truck — but with a sticky hook welded to it. That hook grabs onto the walls of your arteries. And it doesn't let go.

[PAUSE]

Here's what makes Lp(a) different from every other cardiovascular risk factor:

[ON-SCREEN: Three bullet points appearing one at a time]

One: **It's genetic.** You inherited your Lp(a) level from your parents. About 80% of your Lp(a) level is determined by your DNA.

Two: **It doesn't change.** Your LDL can go down with diet, exercise, statins. Your blood pressure can respond to medication. Your blood sugar can shift with lifestyle changes. Lp(a) stays roughly the same from the day you're born until the day you die.

Three: **It's common.** About 20% of the population — roughly 1 in 5 people — have elevated Lp(a). That's about 60 million Americans.

[LEAN IN]

And here's the part that should concern you: fewer than 5% of adults have ever been tested for it.

---

### SECTION 2: WHY YOUR DOCTOR DOESN'T TEST IT

**DR. KANE:**
Now, I want to be clear about something. I'm not telling you this because I think your doctor is failing you. I'm telling you this because the medical system has a gap — and this gap has a name.

[ON-SCREEN: "The Lp(a) Testing Gap"]

The standard lipid panel — the blood test your doctor orders every year — measures total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, and triglycerides. It does not include Lp(a). It has never included Lp(a). And most clinical guidelines, until very recently, didn't recommend routine testing.

[PAUSE]

There are reasons for this. For a long time, we didn't have treatments that specifically lowered Lp(a). So the thinking was: if we can't change it, why test for it?

[ON-SCREEN: "Old thinking: Can't change it → Why test?" shifting to "New thinking: Can't change the number → But you CAN change your response to it"]

That thinking is changing. And here's why.

Knowing your Lp(a) level doesn't just tell you about one risk factor. It recontextualizes ALL your other risk factors. A person with an Lp(a) of 150 nmol/L and an LDL of 85 has a cardiovascular risk equivalent to someone with an LDL of 190. The Lp(a) amplifies everything else.

[ON-SCREEN: Risk comparison graphic — Lp(a) 150 + LDL 85 ≈ LDL 190 risk]

And there are now three drugs in clinical trials — including one called lepodisiran — that specifically target Lp(a) and can lower it by 80-90%. The first of these could be approved within the next one to two years.

[PAUSE]

So the question is shifting from "why test?" to "how do we make sure everyone knows their number before these treatments arrive?"

### SECTION 3: WHAT THE NUMBER MEANS

**DR. KANE:**
Let me give you the actual numbers, because this is where it gets specific.

[ON-SCREEN: Lp(a) risk scale]

**Below 30 nmol/L:** This is considered normal risk. About 60-70% of the population falls here.

**30 to 50 nmol/L:** Borderline. Slightly elevated risk. Worth monitoring.

**50 to 125 nmol/L:** Elevated. This is where cardiovascular risk starts to increase meaningfully. About 15-20% of people fall in this range.

**Above 125 nmol/L:** High risk. This is the range where we start talking about aggressive management of every other risk factor. About 5-10% of people.

[LEAN IN]

And I want to be precise about something. The units matter. Lp(a) can be reported in two different ways — nmol/L or mg/dL. Most experts now prefer nmol/L because it measures the actual number of particles, not just the weight. When you ask your doctor, ask for the result in nmol/L.

[ON-SCREEN: "Ask for: Lp(a) in nmol/L"]

If your lab only reports mg/dL, you can roughly divide by 2.5 to get nmol/L. But ideally, ask the lab to run it in nmol/L directly.

---

### SECTION 4: THE ORIGINAL INSIGHT — THE "GENETIC MULTIPLIER" FRAMEWORK

**DR. KANE:**
Now here's something I haven't heard anyone else explain, and I think it's the most important part of this video.

[ON-SCREEN: "The Genetic Multiplier Effect"]

Most people think about risk factors as additive. High cholesterol plus high blood pressure plus smoking equals more risk. You add them up.

But Lp(a) doesn't work that way. Lp(a) is a multiplier.

[ON-SCREEN: Visual — Addition vs Multiplication]

If your Lp(a) is elevated, it doesn't just add risk on top of your other factors. It multiplies the damage that every other risk factor does. An LDL particle carrying that sticky apo(a) protein doesn't just deposit cholesterol in your artery wall — it also carries oxidized phospholipides that trigger inflammation. It brings clotting factors that make clots more likely. It impairs your body's ability to dissolve clots.

[PAUSE]

So a person with elevated Lp(a) who also has high blood pressure isn't just dealing with two separate problems. The high blood pressure is damaging artery walls, and the Lp(a) is more aggressively depositing into those damaged walls. They feed each other.

[ON-SCREEN: "Lp(a) × Other Risks = Multiplied Damage"]

This is why knowing your Lp(a) number changes how aggressively you should manage everything else. If your Lp(a) is high, your LDL target isn't 100 — it might need to be below 70. Your blood pressure target isn't 140 — it might need to be below 130. The Lp(a) raises the stakes on every other number.

[LEAN IN]

This is the Genetic Multiplier framework. And it's not in any guideline I've seen. But it's how I think about it with my patients, and I think it's how you should think about it too.

### SECTION 5: WHAT TO DO — THE DOCTOR CONVERSATION

**DR. KANE:**
Okay. So you're watching this and you're thinking, "I want to know my number." Here's exactly how to bring it up with your doctor.

[ON-SCREEN: "The Lp(a) Doctor Script"]

Don't walk in and demand a test. That puts your doctor on the defensive. Instead, try this:

[PAUSE]

"Doctor, I've been reading about Lipoprotein(a) as a genetic cardiovascular risk factor. I understand it's not on the standard lipid panel. I'd like to have it checked — it's a one-time test since the level doesn't change. Could we add it to my next blood draw?"

[ON-SCREEN: Script appearing word-for-word]

That's it. That's the whole script. You're not challenging their expertise. You're showing that you've done your homework. And you're making it easy — it's one extra tube of blood drawn at the same time as your regular labs.

[PAUSE]

If your doctor says it's not necessary, you can add: "I understand it's not routinely recommended. But given that it's genetic and doesn't change, I'd feel more comfortable knowing my baseline. If it's elevated, I'd want to be more aggressive with my other risk factors."

[LEAN IN]

And if they still push back — which would be unusual, but it happens — you can order it yourself. Services like Walk-In Lab or Request a Test offer Lp(a) testing for about $35-50 out of pocket. It's a simple blood test. No doctor's order needed in most states.

### SECTION 6: IF YOUR NUMBER IS HIGH

**DR. KANE:**
Let's say you get tested and your Lp(a) comes back elevated. What then?

[ON-SCREEN: "If Lp(a) is elevated — 4 action steps"]

First: **Don't panic.** Elevated Lp(a) is not a death sentence. It's a risk factor, not a disease. Many people with elevated Lp(a) never have cardiac events — especially if they manage their other risk factors aggressively.

Second: **Get a coronary calcium score.** This is a CT scan of your heart that takes about 10 minutes, costs $75-150, and shows whether plaque has actually started building in your coronary arteries. It's the best way to know if your elevated Lp(a) has started doing damage.

[ON-SCREEN: "Coronary Calcium Score — 10 min CT scan — $75-150"]

Third: **Tighten every other number.** This is where the Genetic Multiplier framework matters. If your Lp(a) is high, your LDL should be as low as possible — ideally below 70. Your blood pressure should be well-controlled. If you smoke, this is the single best reason to quit. Every other risk factor becomes more important.

Fourth: **Talk to your doctor about aspirin.** For people with elevated Lp(a) and evidence of plaque, low-dose aspirin may provide additional protection. This is a conversation to have with your doctor — don't start it on your own.

[PAUSE]

And keep an eye on the drug trials. Lepidisiran, olpasiran, and other Lp(a)-lowering therapies are in Phase 3 trials right now. Within a few years, we may have medications that directly target this. Knowing your number now means you'll be ready when those treatments arrive.
