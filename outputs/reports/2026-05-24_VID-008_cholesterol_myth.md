**[SECTION 1: THE THREE NUMBERS THAT CHANGE EVERYTHING]**

[ON-SCREEN: "The 3 Numbers Your Doctor Should Check Together"]

Here is what most people know about cholesterol.

High bad cholesterol = bad. Low bad cholesterol = good. Done.

Here is what most people do NOT know.

Total cholesterol number alone — the one sent in the email with the little red arrow pointing up — is almost meaningless without two companion numbers.

[ON-SCREEN: Number 1 — Triglycerides]

**Number one: triglycerides.**

These are fat particles circulating in your blood from food, especially from processed carbohydrates and sugar. When triglycerides are high, your body is struggling to process energy efficiently. And here's the critical connection — high triglycerides change what LDL cholesterol actually does in your blood vessels. They make LDL particles smaller and denser, and small dense LDL is the kind that penetrates arterial walls and starts plaque formation.

Big fluffy LDL? Much less dangerous. Small dense LDL? Very dangerous.

But a standard cholesterol panel doesn't tell you particle size. It just gives you a total.

[ON-SCREEN: Number 2 — HDL Cholesterol]

**Number two: HDL cholesterol.**

This is often called "good cholesterol," but that's oversimplified to the point of being misleading. HDL is actually a cleanup crew. It picks up excess cholesterol from blood vessel walls and takes it back to the liver for disposal. Think of it as your body's internal janitorial service.

When HDL is high, your cleanup crew is well-staffed. When it's low, garbage accumulates.

Now here is the number that most doctors never calculate.

[ON-SCREEN: Number 3 — Triglyceride:HDL Ratio]

**Number three: the triglyceride-to-HDL ratio.**

You divide your triglyceride number by your HDL number. That's it. Simple division.

This single ratio is one of the most powerful predictors of heart disease risk available from a standard blood test. Research published in the journal *Circulation* — analyzing data from over 300,000 participants — found that the triglyceride-to-HDL ratio was a stronger predictor of cardiovascular events than LDL alone.

[ON-SCREEN: Ratio Calculation Visual]
- Ratio below 2.0: Excellent
- Ratio 2.0 – 3.0: Moderate risk
- Ratio above 3.0: Significantly elevated risk
- Ratio above 4.0: High risk — even if total cholesterol looks "normal"

My patient — the one his daughter called me about? The 248 total cholesterol guy?

His triglycerides were 98. His HDL was 62.

His ratio was 1.58.

[PAUSE]

That is excellent. Outstanding, actually. His body was processing fat efficiently, his cleanup crew was well-staffed, and his LDL particles were likely the large, fluffy, harmless kind.

His daughter was ready to start him on a daily medication based on one oversimplified number.

[ON-SCREEN: "The Cholesterol Paradox After 60"]

Now, I want to be careful here. Because what I am about to say next, some people will misunderstand. So listen carefully.

I am NOT saying cholesterol is not important. I am NOT saying you should ignore it. And I am definitely NOT telling you to stop taking any medication. That is between you and your doctor.

What I AM saying is that the relationship between cholesterol and health changes after 60. And the one-size-fits-all approach may actually be causing harm.

Here is what the research shows.

[PAUSE]

A study published in the *British Medical Journal* in 2016 — following over 68,000 adults aged 60 and older for an average of 10 years — found something that surprised many cardiologists.

Among participants over 75, those with the HIGHEST total cholesterol had the LOWEST mortality. From any cause. Not just heart disease. Any cause.

Read that again.

The group with the highest cholesterol lived the longest.

This finding is not isolated. It has been replicated. A study in the *Journal of the American Geriatrics Society* confirmed that in adults over 70, higher total cholesterol was associated with better survival. Another study from UCLA, published in *PLOS ONE*, found that elderly individuals over 85 with cholesterol levels that would be considered "elevated" by standard guidelines actually had the lowest risk of cognitive decline and dementia.

[ON-SCREEN: "In elderly populations, higher cholesterol is associated with LOWER mortality — multiple peer-reviewed studies"]

Why would this be?

[LEAN IN]

**[SECTION 3: WHY CHOLESTEROL IS ESSENTIAL AFTER 60 — THE MECHANISMS]**

[ON-SCREEN: "Cholesterol: Your Body's Essential Tool"]

First — cholesterol is not a toxin. Your liver manufactures it for a reason. It is an essential structural molecule.

Every cell membrane in your body — every single one — contains cholesterol. It provides rigidity and stability to the membrane. Without adequate cholesterol, your cells literally become fragile.

Second — cholesterol is the precursor to every steroid hormone your body produces. Cortisol, which manages stress and inflammation. Estrogen and testosterone, which maintain bone density, muscle mass, and cognitive function. Aldosterone, which regulates blood pressure.

When you aggressively lower cholesterol in someone whose body is already struggling to produce these hormones — someone over 70, for instance — you can inadvertently accelerate the very decline you're trying to prevent.

[ON-SCREEN: Cortisol, Estrogen, Testosterone, Aldosterone — all require cholesterol as precursor]

Third — and this is the one almost nobody talks about — cholesterol is essential for myelin production.

Myelin is the fatty sheath that insulates your nerve fibers. It's like the rubber coating on electrical wiring. Without it, nerve signals slow down, misfire, or stop entirely.

After 60, myelin naturally begins to thin. Your body needs a steady supply of cholesterol for ongoing myelin repair. Cholesterol-depleted, or cholesterol-starved, neurons are slower, more vulnerable, and less capable of repair.

This is one of the reasons why some research suggests that low cholesterol in the elderly may be associated with cognitive decline and even dementia.

[PAUSE]

Now — does this mean high cholesterol is always good after 60? No.

Does it mean you should ignore your cholesterol entirely? Absolutely not.

What it means is that CONTEXT matters. The ratio matters. Your age matters. Your overall health status matters. And treating a single number without understanding the full picture is not medicine. It's number-manipulation.

**[SECTION 4: WHEN THE "LOWER IS BETTER" APPROACH GOES WRONG]**

[ON-SCREEN: "The Overtreatment Problem"]

Let me share something that bothers me about the current state of cardiovascular medicine.

The push to lower LDL as much as possible — what some cardiologists call "the lower the better" approach — is based largely on studies conducted on middle-aged patients, typically aged 40-65, who had already experienced a cardiac event.

These studies showed that aggressively lowering LDL in this population reduced the risk of subsequent events. That finding is legitimate and has saved lives.

But here is the problem. These findings were then applied — broadly, uncritically — to everyone over 60, including people with no history of heart disease, no significant risk factors, and no evidence of arterial plaque.

[ON-SCREEN: "Evidence from: middle-aged patients WITH prior cardiac events"]
[ON-SCREEN: "Applied to: ALL adults over 60 WITHOUT evidence of heart disease"]

That is not good medicine. That is a statistical mistake with real consequences.

Because here is what can happen when you aggressively lower cholesterol in a healthy 72-year-old.

One: fatigue. Deep, persistent fatigue that the patient attributes to "just getting older," but is actually driven by cortisol precursor depletion.

Two: muscle pain and weakness — which, by the way, is already a recognized side effect of statin medications that affects 5-10% of users. In younger patients, this is manageable. In a 75-year-old, muscle weakness leads to decreased activity, which leads to decreased bone density, which leads to falls.

Three — and this is the serious one — cognitive fog.

Multiple studies, including one published in *Pharmacotherapy* and another in *JAMA Internal Medicine*, have documented cognitive side effects associated with statin use in elderly patients. Forgetfulness. Confusion. Slowed processing speed. These effects are often reversible when the medication is adjusted, but many patients never connect the dots because they assume it's just "age."

[LEAN IN]

I am not saying statins are bad. I am not saying no one over 60 should take them.

I AM saying that the decision should be based on the complete picture — not a single number, not a blanket recommendation, and not fear.

**[SECTION 5: WHAT TO ACTUALLY FOCUS ON INSTEAD]**

[ON-SCREEN: "What to Focus On Instead — Dr. Kane's Protocol"]

If total cholesterol alone isn't the best predictor after 60, what should you focus on?

Here is what I tell my patients. These are the markers that actually tell the story.

[ON-SCREEN: Marker #1 — Triglyceride-to-HDL Ratio]

**Marker one: Your triglyceride-to-HDL ratio.** We already covered this. Ideally below 2.0. This tells you how efficiently your body is processing fat and how well your cleanup crew is working.

[ON-SCREEN: Marker #2 — High-Sensitivity C-Reactive Protein (hs-CRP)]

**Marker two: hs-CRP.** This measures chronic inflammation in your blood vessels. Inflammation is what makes cholesterol dangerous — it's not the cholesterol itself, but the inflammatory environment that causes cholesterol to stick to arterial walls and form plaque. A study in *The New England Journal of Medicine* — the famous JUPITER trial — showed that reducing inflammation was as important as reducing heart events. Your hs-CRP should ideally be below 1.0 mg/L.

[ON-SCREEN: Marker #3 — Apolipoprotein B (ApoB)]

**Marker three: ApoB.** This is a direct count of atherogenic particles — the actual number of LDL particles in your bloodstream, regardless of size. It is a more accurate risk marker than LDL-C alone, because two people can have the same LDL number but completely different particle counts. One might have mostly large, harmless particles. The other might have hundreds of tiny, artery-penetrating ones. ApoB catches the difference.

[ON-SCREEN: "Ask your doctor about: 1) TG:HDL ratio, 2) hs-CRP, 3) ApoB"]

**Marker four: Homocysteine.** Elevated homocysteine is an independent risk factor for heart disease and stroke, and it is almost never checked in standard panels. It reflects B-vitamin status — specifically B6, B12, and folate. In seniors, B12 deficiency is extremely common and can drive homocysteine up silently. This is a simple fix — identify it, supplement appropriately — but only if you look for it.

And number five — the most important one.

[PAUSE]

**Your symptoms. Your function. Your quality of life.**

I have patients with "perfect" cholesterol numbers who can't walk up a flight of stairs.

I have patients with "elevatory" cholesterol who run 5Ks.

[ON-SCREEN: "Treat the patient, not the number. — Dr. Kane"]

The number is a data point. It is not the patient.

**[SECTION 6: WHAT I TELL MY PATIENTS — A SIMPLE FRAMEWORK]**

[ON-SCREEN: "Dr. Kane's Cholesterol Framework — Do This Now"]

Here is the framework I give every patient who asks about their cholesterol after 60.

**Step one: Get the complete panel.** Not just total cholesterol. Demand the full picture — LDL, HDL, triglycerides, hs-CRP, ApoB. If your doctor won't order these tests, ask why. You are allowed to advocate for yourself.

**Step two: Calculate your ratio.** Divide triglycerides by HDL. Below 2 is excellent. Above 3 is a red flag regardless of total cholesterol.

**Step three: Consider your age and history.** If you are over 70, have no history of heart disease, no diabetes, and your ratio is good — your cholesterol number is far less meaningful than the guidelines suggest. Have a REAL conversation with your doctor about whether medication is necessary.

**Step four: Focus on inflammation.** If your cholesterol is elevated but your inflammation markers (hs-CRP, homocysteine) are normal, your risk profile is fundamentally different from someone with elevated cholesterol AND elevated inflammation. Treat inflammation first — through diet, movement, sleep, stress management. The cholesterol often self-corrects when the underlying inflammation resolves.

**Step five: Never stop medication without your doctor.** But DO bring this information to your next appointment. Ask: "Given my age, my ratio, my inflammation markers, and my overall health — is this medication still the best choice for me?" That is a smart, educated question. Any good doctor will welcome it.
